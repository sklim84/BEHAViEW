\begin{abstract}
In anti-money laundering (AML) transaction graphs, suspicious accounts have predominantly benign neighbors, so graph neural network (GNN) aggregation dilutes a rare suspicious signal. We trace this to a topology-behavior mismatch: the evasion-shaped transaction network fails to reflect homophily within the suspicious class. To address this mismatch, we present \method, a self-supervised graph contrastive learning framework that performs topology repair through behavioral homophily recovery. \method builds a topology-independent recovered neighborhood graph from behavioral similarity (e.g., amount distributions) and aligns it with the transaction graph through contrastive learning. On \hofinet, a national interbank transfer network, the repair raises the suspicious ego-neighborhood ratio from 1:5.7 to 1:1.4. At 1\% labels, \method achieves the highest suspicious-class F1 on all three AML datasets, with a 0.010-0.016 gap over the strongest supervised baseline. These findings position \method as a label-efficient building block for money laundering detection under realistic label-delay constraints.
\end{abstract}

\keywords{topology repair, behavioral homophily recovery, anti-money laundering, transaction networks, heterophily, self-supervised learning, graph contrastive learning}

Money laundering, accounting for an estimated 2--5\% of global GDP~\cite{unodc_ml}, has become more sophisticated through tactics such as structuring, layering, and mule networks~\cite{fatf2018pml,li2020flowscope}. Earlier AML studies used network analysis and typology-driven perspectives to characterize laundering behaviors and transaction structures~\cite{drezewski2015application,teichmann2020recent}. More recently, GNN-based methods have been explored to learn structural patterns in transaction networks beyond rule-based detection~\cite{cheng2025graph,cardoso2022laundrograph}. In AML, however, neighborhood aggregation is only useful when a node's neighbors carry class-relevant evidence. For the suspicious class, this evidence is often weak because suspicious accounts intentionally mix with benign counterparties. We focus on three challenges in AML transaction networks: suspicious-class neighborhood heterophily~\cite{zhu2020beyond}, severe class imbalance, and delayed or limited labels~\cite{fatf2013guidance}. To address these jointly, we adopt a topology-repair perspective on the topology--behavior mismatch.

\textbf{Challenge 1: evasion-shaped neighborhoods dilute suspicious signals.} Money-laundering accounts deliberately mix with benign accounts, forming mule networks. In \hofinet's transaction graph, about 85\% of suspicious-incident edges connect to benign accounts (S-S:S-B $=$ 1:5.7). As shown in Figure~\ref{fig:intro_topology_repair}, many suspicious accounts lie even deeper in benign neighborhoods. Message passing thus struggles to accumulate suspicious evidence. Standard graph contrastive learning (GCL) augmentations~\cite{you2020graph,velivckovic2018deep,zhu2021graph} preserve observed account linkages; therefore, they do not directly address the topology–behavior mismatch around suspicious accounts.

\textbf{Challenge 2: extreme class imbalance limits supervision.} Suspicious accounts typically represent under 2\% of all accounts~\cite{hilal2022financial}, so even large transaction graphs contain only a small number of positive nodes. This makes supervised training highly sensitive to sampling and thresholding, encouraging majority-class shortcuts. Models therefore yield unstable gradients unless they employ careful reweighting or specialized imbalanced-learning strategies~\cite{he2009imbalanced,chawla2002smote,dou2020enhancing,liu2021pick}. In practice, the scarcity of positives also makes early model selection and error analysis difficult, since validation sets contain only a handful of suspicious examples.

\textbf{Challenge 3: delayed and sparse labels create a cold start.} Labels are often only available after retrospective investigations and reporting processes that can take months~\cite{fatf2013guidance,kofia2024aml}. Combined with weak suspicious-class neighborhood signals, these constraints cause cold-start problems for supervised fraud models in production.

We propose \method, a self-supervised graph contrastive learning framework that repairs evasion-shaped detection topologies by restoring behavioral homophily. We study two steps. First, whether the recovered graph creates class-relevant suspicious neighborhoods. Second, whether pooling on those neighborhoods preserves or dilutes the recovered signal. Normalization determines whether this mechanism is realized reliably under the BYOL-style objective.

The central contribution is the AML-specific role assigned to the behavior-only k-NN graph: it serves as topology repair for suspicious-class neighborhoods. Prior methods construct feature-derived k-NN or affinity graphs and contrast them with the original topology~\cite{shang2023mlgcl,fan2021featuretopology,chen2023asp,lu2024graph}. In AML, the observed transaction topology can be evasion-shaped: suspicious accounts deliberately connect to benign counterparties, so transaction neighborhoods are poor carriers of suspicious-class evidence. The repaired branch supplies the missing aggregation neighborhood for suspicious-class evidence, while the final representation still retains both transaction-topology and repaired-topology signals. This leads to two testable consequences. Behavioral features should repair the topology better than connectivity-derived or all-feature affinities, because the latter inherit topology bias. Aggregation should also operate on the repaired topology, not only on the original transaction graph. We validate these consequences in Section~\ref{sec:experiments}.

On \hofinet, the recovered neighborhood graph improves the S-S:S-B ratio from 1:5.7 to 1:1.4, with similar gains on AMLworld~\cite{amlpatterns2024realistic} and AMLNet. At 1\% labels, \method attains the highest suspicious-class F1 on all three datasets. Our contributions are:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*, topsep=2pt, itemsep=1pt, parsep=0pt, partopsep=0pt]
    \item \textbf{Topology repair via behavioral homophily recovery.} We propose \method, which constructs a topology-independent recovered neighborhood graph from behavioral similarity to repair evasion-shaped suspicious-class topologies. We establish a probabilistic homophily-recovery guarantee under mild feature-separation and a lower bound on the homophily gap.
    \item \textbf{Signal preservation under aggregation.} A sign-flip threshold determines when pooling on the repaired topology preserves rather than dilutes suspicious signals; we empirically validate this regime.
    \item \textbf{Self-supervised representations under label scarcity.} Frozen representations of \method outperform supervised tabular and GNN baselines at 1\% labels and remain competitive at 10\% labels, addressing the multi-month label delays typical in AML.
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}\label{sec:related_work}
We survey three research areas related to \method: heterophily-aware GNNs, graph contrastive learning with feature-derived auxiliary graphs, and fraud or anomaly detection with limited labels.

\subsection{Heterophily-aware GNNs}
Prior work improves message passing under low label homophily by separating ego and neighbor embeddings, modifying propagation, or decoupling structure from attributes (e.g., H2GCN~\cite{zhu2020beyond}, Geom-GCN~\cite{pei2020geom}, LINKX~\cite{zhu2021linkx}). Yet these models still depend on the observed transaction topology; in AML, suspicious accounts intentionally mix with benign counterparties, so suspicious--suspicious edges are rare. \method instead repairs the topology, complementing heterophily-aware propagation by building a class-relevant neighborhood from a topology-independent behavioral homophily recovery graph.

\subsection{GCL with Feature-Derived Auxiliary Graphs}
Self-supervised GCL enforces consistency across multiple graph branches~\cite{velivckovic2018deep,you2020graph}. Multi-view GCL often contrasts the observed graph with another topology view, such as a diffusion view in MVGRL~\cite{hassani2020contrastive}. More directly related to \method, feature-topology methods construct a k-NN or affinity graph from node attributes and learn across the feature-derived view and the original topology~\cite{shang2023mlgcl,fan2021featuretopology,chen2023asp}. In AML, GCPAL also uses a k-NN view together with stochastic graph augmentations for contrastive pretraining~\cite{lu2024graph}. These works show that feature-derived topologies can improve representation learning, but they generally treat the k-NN graph as an auxiliary or augmented view. \method instead asks whether the observed transaction topology is evasion-shaped and whether a behavior-only k-NN graph repairs suspicious-class neighborhoods. This distinction matters because all-feature affinity graphs can mix behavioral similarity with topology-correlated variables. \method instead excludes network-derived and structural variables from repair construction and evaluates the recovered graph as a topology repair mechanism.

This auxiliary-view interpretation is natural when the observed graph already encodes semantic affinity, as in many citation or social benchmarks. AML transaction graphs play a different role: edges record fund transfers, while suspicious-class evidence may be hidden by benign-looking counterparties. The transaction topology is therefore useful for fund-flow context but can be weak as an aggregation neighborhood for detection. This motivates our repair view of the behavior-only k-NN graph, which supplies class-relevant neighborhoods while the transaction branch preserves transfer structure.

\subsection{Money Laundering and Fraud Detection}
In AML and fraud detection, supervised GNNs can mitigate class imbalance via neighborhood selection, reweighting, and imbalanced-learning strategies~\cite{dou2020enhancing,liu2021pick,zhao2021graphsmote,song2022tam}, but require timely, abundant labels that AML rarely provides. Recent AML-specific representation learning also explores self-supervised pretraining on transaction graphs~\cite{cardoso2022laundrograph,lu2024graph}. Unsupervised graph anomaly detection avoids labels~\cite{ding2019dominant,liu2021anomaly,jin2021anemone,tang2022rethinking,liu2022bond}, yet is primarily evaluated on generic graph benchmarks and does not address suspicious-class neighborhood mismatch in transaction networks. \method overcomes this by combining topology-independent behavioral homophily recovery with self-supervised learning and by analyzing aggregation-level signal preservation on the repaired topology.

\section{Method: \method}
\label{sec:method}

\method comprises a behavioral homophily recovery graph, a topology-repair contrastive encoder with subgraph pooling, and BYOL-style training.

\subsection{Problem Formulation}
We model the transaction network as a directed graph $\mathcal{G}_{\text{trans}} = (\mathcal{V}, \mathcal{E}, \mathbf{X})$, where $\mathcal{V}$ is the set of accounts (nodes, $|\mathcal{V}|=N$), $\mathcal{E} \subseteq \mathcal{V} \times \mathcal{V}$ is the set of transfer transactions (edges), and $\mathbf{X} \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times F}$ is the node feature matrix. Each node $v_i$ has a suspicion label $y_i \in \{0, 1\}$, but we use a self-supervised setting that ignores labels during training. Our goal is to learn node representations $\mathbf{Z} \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times D}$ that identify suspicious accounts based purely on the graph structure and node features.

\subsection{Feature Taxonomy}
\label{subsec:feature_taxonomy}
Table~\ref{tab:features} groups node features into behavioral, network-derived, and structural categories. Behavioral features describe transaction patterns via amount statistics, time-window frequencies, and transaction-type entropies. Network-derived features (transaction counts and degrees) are fed to the encoder but excluded from recovered-neighborhood construction, while structural features (centrality, hub/authority, topology) are used only for analysis and auxiliary comparison. We denote by $\mathbf{X}^{\text{enc}}$ the behavioral and network-derived features used by the GNN encoder, and by $\mathbf{X}^{\text{bhv}}$ the 20 behavioral features used to build a behavioral homophily recovery graph. Because suspicious accounts often transact with benign ones to evade detection, they show low neighborhood homophily in the observed topology. We address this by recovering topology-independent behavioral homophily, linking behaviorally similar accounts even when they are not directly connected.

\subsection{Recovered Neighborhood Graph}
\label{subsec:knn_view}
\method repairs topology by constructing a concrete, topology-independent recovered neighborhood graph $\mathcal{G}_{\text{bhv}}$ that provides a repaired neighborhood branch for heterophilous suspicious accounts in the transaction graph. As illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:framework}, unlike standard augmentation-based GCL, which perturbs the original graph by dropping edges or masking features and thus preserves original neighborhoods, we build $\mathcal{G}_{\text{bhv}}$ solely from behavioral features, independent of the edge structure of $\mathcal{G}_{\text{trans}}$, preventing any connectivity-derived information from affecting the repair.

\textbf{Recovered-neighborhood construction.} We standardize the behavioral features $\mathbf{x}_i^{\text{bhv}} \in \mathbb{R}^{20}$ to zero mean and unit variance to remove scale differences, then apply L2 normalization so that similarity depends only on the feature vector direction:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:l2norm}
    \bar{\mathbf{x}}_i = \text{StandardScaler}(\mathbf{x}_i^{\text{bhv}}), \quad
    \tilde{\mathbf{x}}_i = \frac{\bar{\mathbf{x}}_i}{\|\bar{\mathbf{x}}_i\|_2}
\end{equation}

Because the Euclidean distance between two L2-normalized vectors is monotonic in their cosine similarity, we retrieve the $k$ nearest neighbors of each node $v_i$ using a ball-tree search over Euclidean distance. Connecting each node to these neighbors yields the recovered neighborhood graph $\mathcal{G}_{\text{bhv}} = (\mathcal{V}, \mathcal{E}_{\text{bhv}})$:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:knn_edge}
    \mathcal{E}_{\text{bhv}} = \{(v_i, v_j) \mid v_j \in \operatorname{kNN}(v_i, k)\}.
\end{equation}

We use the symmetrized (bidirectional) version of this recovered neighborhood graph for message passing and pooling, adding $(v_j, v_i)$ whenever $(v_i, v_j) \in \mathcal{E}_{\text{bhv}}$.

$\operatorname{kNN}(v_i, k)$ denotes the set of $k$ nodes closest in Euclidean distance to $\tilde{\mathbf{x}}_i$, with $k=10$ by default.

Money-laundering accounts that split transactions and layer transfers tend to share similar amount distributions, time-window frequencies, and transaction-type entropies~\cite{fatf2018pml,li2020flowscope}. The recovered neighborhood graph therefore links suspicious accounts not directly connected by transactions, restoring suspicious-to-suspicious neighborhoods missing from the transaction topology. Under mild feature-separation conditions, this construction provably yields high homophily in the recovered neighborhood graph (Theorem~\ref{thm:knn_homophily}, Appendix~\ref{app:theory}).

\subsection{Topology-Repair GNN Encoder}
\label{subsec:dual_view}
\textbf{Topology-repair contrastive branches.} Contrastive learning aligns the observed transaction topology, which captures transfer structure, with the recovered neighborhood topology, which captures behavioral homophily of the same account. \method builds these two branches from $\mathcal{G}_{\text{trans}}$ and $\mathcal{G}_{\text{bhv}}$.

\textbf{Observed and repaired topologies.} Using only the transaction topology mixes suspicious and benign accounts, while using only the recovered neighborhood graph discards transaction-structure information. \method jointly learns account representations from both branches so embeddings capture both transaction structure and behavioral homophily.

\textbf{GNN encoder.} A shared online encoder $f_\theta$ produces embeddings $\mathbf{H}^{(1)} = f_\theta(\mathbf{X}^{\text{enc}}, \mathcal{E}_{\text{trans}})$ and $\mathbf{H}^{(2)} = f_\theta(\mathbf{X}^{\text{enc}}, \mathcal{E}_{\text{bhv}})$. The target encoder $f_\xi$ is an EMA copy that provides stop-gradient representations for the opposite BYOL branch. The online encoder stacks GCNConv~\cite{kipf2016semi} layers with batch normalization, nonlinearity, and dropout. \method is encoder-agnostic and supports nine encoders, including GIN~\cite{xu2018powerful}, MVGRL, and GBT; Section~\ref{subsec:rq2} studies the impact of batch normalization.

\subsection{Subgraph Pooling}
\label{subsec:subgraph_pool}
\textbf{Subgraph-level signal aggregation.} \method compares both individual node representations and a subgraph representation that pools each account with its neighbors (Subgraph Pooling in Figure~\ref{fig:framework}). This reflects the intuition that an account’s suspiciousness is often clearer from its relationships with neighbors than from its own attributes alone.

\textbf{Subgraph representation.} For each topology branch $t \in \{1,2\}$, we define the subgraph representation of node $v_i$ as the average of its own representation and those of its neighbors in that branch:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:subgraph_pool}
    \mathbf{s}_i^{(t)} = \frac{1}{|\mathcal{N}^{(t)}(v_i)| + 1}
    \left(\mathbf{h}_i^{(t)} + \sum_{v_j \in \mathcal{N}^{(t)}(v_i)} \mathbf{h}_j^{(t)}\right)
\end{equation}

$\mathcal{N}^{(1)}(v_i)$ denotes the neighborhood of $v_i$ in the transaction topology, and $\mathcal{N}^{(2)}(v_i)$ its recovered neighborhood. For both topology branches, we perform message passing and pooling on a bidirected graph, so $\mathcal{N}^{(t)}(v_i)$ is the union of in- and out-neighbors. The mean pooling in Eq.~\eqref{eq:subgraph_pool} is the default in \method.

\textbf{Repair-aware pooling effect.} Subgraph pooling is applied separately to each topology branch: transaction-graph neighbors for the observed topology and behaviorally similar neighbors for the repaired topology. Because pooling aggregates neighbor information, the chosen topology shapes the result. The repaired neighborhood brings behaviorally similar suspicious accounts closer, so pooling on it tends to preserve or amplify suspicious signals. In the transaction graph, suspicious accounts often connect to benign ones (high S-B ratio), so pooling can dilute suspicious signals with benign information.

\textbf{Pooling variants.} We use mean pooling by default, but other options are possible, including similarity-weighted pooling (HAP). We analyze its expected signal-preservation behavior in Appendix~\ref{app:theory} (Proposition~\ref{thm:hap}).

\KwIn{$\mathcal{G}_{\text{trans}}$, $\mathbf{X}^{\text{bhv}}$, $\mathbf{X}^{\text{enc}}$, neighbors $k$, epochs $E$, momentum $m$, learning rate $\eta$}
\KwOut{Node embeddings $\{\mathbf{z}_i\}_{i=1}^{N}$}
\BlankLine
\tcc{Stage 1: recovered neighborhood graph (one-shot)}
$\tilde{\mathbf{X}}^{\text{bhv}} \gets \mathrm{L2norm}(\mathrm{StandardScaler}(\mathbf{X}^{\text{bhv}}))$\;
$\mathcal{G}_{\text{bhv}} \gets$ symmetrize($\mathrm{kNN}_k(\tilde{\mathbf{X}}^{\text{bhv}})$) \tcp*[r]{Eqs.~\eqref{eq:l2norm},~\eqref{eq:knn_edge}}
\BlankLine
\tcc{Stage 2: self-supervised training (BYOL-style)}
Initialize online encoder $f_\theta$, shared projector $g$, predictor $q$;\ target $\xi \gets \theta$\;
\For{$\text{epoch}=1$ \KwTo $E$}{
  \ForEach{$t \in \{1,2\}$, $\mathcal{G}^{(1)}{=}\mathcal{G}_{\text{trans}}, \mathcal{G}^{(2)}{=}\mathcal{G}_{\text{bhv}}$}{
    $\mathbf{h}^{(t)} \gets f_\theta(\mathbf{X}^{\text{enc}}, \mathcal{G}^{(t)})$;\quad $\mathbf{h}_\xi^{(t)} \gets f_\xi(\mathbf{X}^{\text{enc}}, \mathcal{G}^{(t)})$\;
    $\mathbf{s}^{(t)} \gets \mathrm{MeanPool}_{\mathcal{G}^{(t)}}(\mathbf{h}^{(t)})$ \tcp*[r]{settings (c),(d); Eq.~\eqref{eq:subgraph_pool}}
  }
  Compute $\mathcal{L}$ via Eq.~\eqref{eq:loss} (stop-grad on target branch)\;
  $\theta \gets \theta - \eta \nabla_\theta \mathcal{L}$;\quad
  $\xi \gets m \xi + (1-m) \theta$\;
}
\BlankLine
\tcc{Stage 3: inference (frozen encoder)}
$\mathbf{z}_i \gets [\mathbf{s}_i^{(1)} \,\Vert\, \mathbf{s}_i^{(2)}]$ \tcp*[r]{Eq.~\eqref{eq:concat}; node-level: $[\mathbf{h}_i^{(1)} \Vert \mathbf{h}_i^{(2)}]$}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Training and Inference}
\label{subsec:contrastive_learning}

Algorithm~\ref{alg:behaview} summarizes \method's three stages: recovered neighborhood construction (Section~\ref{subsec:knn_view}), self-supervised training under a symmetric BYOL-style loss, and inference via per-branch concatenation.

The Stage~2 loss maximizes cosine similarity between the two topology branches:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:loss}
    \mathcal{L} = \sum_{t \in \{1,2\}} \left(2 - 2 \cdot \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N} \frac{q(g(\mathbf{r}_i^{(t)})) \cdot g(\mathbf{r}_{\xi,i}^{(3-t)})}{\|q(g(\mathbf{r}_i^{(t)}))\| \cdot \|g(\mathbf{r}_{\xi,i}^{(3-t)})\|}\right)
\end{equation}
where $g$ is the projector shared between online and target paths (stop-gradient on the target), $q$ is the predictor on the online path, $\mathbf{r}_i^{(t)}{=}\mathbf{s}_i^{(t)}$ ($\mathbf{h}_i^{(t)}$ in the node-level ablation), and $\mathbf{r}_{\xi,i}^{(t)}$ is the target-encoder representation. We use momentum $m{=}0.99$ for the EMA target update.

At inference, embeddings are
\begin{equation}\label{eq:concat}
    \mathbf{z}_i = [\mathbf{s}_i^{(1)} \| \mathbf{s}_i^{(2)}],
\end{equation}
with $\mathbf{z}_i = [\mathbf{h}_i^{(1)} \| \mathbf{h}_i^{(2)}]$ for the node-level ablation. Encoder architectures and remaining hyperparameters are at the anonymized link.

\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}
We evaluate \method in four phases, from mechanism to deployment. First, we test whether the recovered neighborhood graph compensates for missing suspicious-to-suspicious links, restores suspicious-class homophily, and improves downstream performance. Second, we examine when aggregation on the repaired topology (subgraph pooling) helps or hurts, and how normalization affects training stability. Third, we compare the self-supervised representations to supervised baselines under label delay and cold start, reflecting realistic AML conditions. Fourth, we test whether the mechanism and empirical patterns transfer to AML datasets with different prevalence and scale. Together, these experiments answer our research questions.

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{RQ1. Behavioral homophily recovery:} Does the topology-independent recovered neighborhood graph repair the suspicious-class topology relative to the transaction graph?
\item \textbf{RQ2. Signal preservation under aggregation:} When does subgraph pooling help versus hurt, and how does recovered homophily change this trade-off?
\item \textbf{RQ3. Self-supervised AML detection under label scarcity:} Does the self-supervised representation from \method remain competitive with supervised models under label delay and cold start?
\item \textbf{RQ4. Robustness across AML regimes:} Do the same mechanisms and trends apply to AML datasets with different prevalence and scale?
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Experimental Setting}
All experiments use Python 3.12, PyTorch 2.10, CUDA 12.8, and NVIDIA H100 80GB GPUs.

\subsubsection{Datasets}Table~\ref{tab:datasets} summarizes the three AML datasets used in this work. \hofinet contains ~4.7M synthetic transactions from a national interbank settlement network over 40 months of household and corporate transfers. AMLworld~\cite{amlpatterns2024realistic}, the NeurIPS 2023 open synthetic benchmark, models eight transaction patterns (e.g., fan-out, scatter-gather, cycle); we use the HI-Small variant (high illicit ratio, small size). AMLNet~\cite{huda2025amlnet} is a small synthetic benchmark for high-prevalence settings that shares AMLworld’s feature schema. All three datasets include transaction-level suspicious labels. Following standard graph-based AML and fraud detection practice~\cite{dou2020enhancing,liu2021pick}, we max-aggregate to the account level: an account is suspicious (label$=1$) if it appears on either side of any suspicious transaction and normal (label$=0$) otherwise. The resulting suspicious-account ratios are 2.13\% (\hofinet), 1.23\% (AMLworld), and 13.52\% (AMLNet).

\subsubsection{Models} We evaluate \method under four ablation settings that vary both topology and contrastive granularity: (a) transaction graph with node-level contrast, (b) recovered neighborhood graph with node-level contrast, (c) transaction graph with subgraph pooling, and (d) repaired topology with subgraph pooling. We compare nine self-supervised encoders: five with BatchNorm at every layer (GBT, DGI+BN, MVGRL+BN, GRACE+BN, GIN), one with BatchNorm only on the final layer (BGRL), and three without BatchNorm (DGI, MVGRL, GRACE). All models share the same BYOL-style bootstrap objective (Eq.~\eqref{eq:loss}) and hyperparameters (learning rate 5e-4, hidden size 256, $L$=2 layers, dropout 0.2, 200 training epochs). 

For the label-efficiency study (Section~\ref{subsec:rq3}), we additionally include six supervised baselines: tabular models (MLP, XGBoost~\cite{xgboost2016}, LightGBM~\cite{lightgbm2017}), generic GNNs (GCN~\cite{kipf2016semi}, GAT~\cite{velivckovic2017graph}), and a fraud-oriented GNN (CARE-GNN~\cite{dou2020enhancing}). The MLP follows the standard fraud-GNN configuration (stacked Linear+ReLU layers without BatchNorm). All supervised baselines apply class-weighted loss and receive the same encoder input features $\mathbf{X}^{\text{enc}}$ (20 behavioral plus 4 network-derived attributes; Table~\ref{tab:features}). Only the topology-repair component consumes behavioral features $\mathbf{X}^{\text{bhv}}$ (20) and omits any network-derived variables.

\subsubsection{Evaluation metrics} We evaluate self-supervised models by freezing the encoder and training Logistic Regression~\cite{pedregosa2011scikit} on embeddings $\mathbf{z}_i = [\mathbf{s}_i^{(1)} \| \mathbf{s}_i^{(2)}]$. The main metric is suspicious-class F1 ($F1_{\text{susp}}$); AUROC and AUPRC are secondary. We use a 10\%/10\%/80\% train/validation/test split, fixed per seed for paired comparisons. We report mean$\pm$std over four seeds and assess significance with a 4-seed paired $t$-test and Holm--Bonferroni correction. Seed-level results and reproduction scripts are available at an anonymized link.

\subsection{Signal Preservation (RQ2)}
\label{subsec:rq2}
RQ1 validated topology repair in the recovered neighborhood graph. RQ2 examines when aggregation (subgraph pooling) helps or hurts, and how repair alters this trade-off. Pooling is unreliable on the raw transaction graph but, on the repaired topology, it can amplify suspicious evidence, with the largest gains under suspicious-class-oriented scoring.
\textbf{Pooling effects across the 4-setting ablation.} Subgraph pooling effectiveness depends on topology homophily and encoder normalization depth. On \hofinet, the (d)-over-(b) improvement shrinks with deeper normalization: per-layer BN encoders gain only about +0.003--0.005, BGRL gains +0.087, and BN-less encoders gain about +0.012 on average. Pooling on the transaction graph (c) reduces performance by 17--30\% relative to (a) for BN-equipped encoders ($-17.6\%$ for BGRL to $-29.8\%$ for GIN), consistent with the sign-flip analysis in Appendix~\ref{app:theory} (Theorem~\ref{thm:meanpool}).

On \hofinet, topology repair is the main benefit ((b)$>$(a), (d)$>$(c)), while pooling yields a smaller, normalization-dependent gain: (d)$\approx$(b) with per-layer BN, but larger gains for BGRL and BN-less encoders. The (b)$\to$(d) gain is statistically significant for representative encoders (GBT $p=2.1{\times}10^{-2}$, BGRL Holm-adjusted $p=1.8{\times}10^{-3}$). On AMLworld, Table~\ref{tab:rq4} shows that the pooling effect varies by encoder: BGRL and GRACE+BN show (d)$>$(b), while per-layer BN encoders (GBT, DGI+BN) show (b)$\approx$(d) at the lower end of the absolute scale.

Thus, subgraph pooling efficacy is governed by (i) recovered homophily and (ii) encoder normalization depth.

\begin{proposition}[BN as a practical stabilizer under bootstrap objectives]\label{thm:bn_collapse}
Under the BYOL-style bootstrap loss~\eqref{eq:loss}, the trivial constant solution $f_\theta(\cdot) \equiv c$ can attain $\mathcal{L}=0$ when the encoder lacks batch normalization (BN). In our training configuration, adding BN prevents the constant-collapse behavior observed in BN-free encoders by enforcing non-degenerate batch statistics during training. We therefore use BN as a practical stabilizer rather than as a universal guarantee for all bootstrap objectives.
\end{proposition}

\textbf{Empirical check for the stability analysis.} In setting (a), BN-less encoders reach $F1_{\text{susp}} \approx 0.04$, matching the \hofinet random-classifier upper bound $2\rho/(1+\rho) \approx 0.042$ (the maximum $F1_{\text{susp}}$ at positive rate $\rho$), indicating that unstable training can erase suspicious-class signal at low prevalence. When $\rho \leq 2\%$, this bound is already $\approx 0.04$, so instability leaves little usable suspicious-class evidence. On AMLNet ($\rho = 13.52\%$), BN-less DGI still achieves $F1_{\text{susp}} \approx 0.40$ (Section~\ref{subsec:rq4}), so the absolute impact is smaller. The BN-conditional pattern is not specific to the BYOL bootstrap loss: bare DGI, MVGRL, and GRACE under their native JSD and InfoNCE losses also collapse to $F1_{\text{susp}} \approx 0.07$, showing that the stability pattern in Proposition~\ref{thm:bn_collapse} is BN-conditional rather than loss-specific. Overall, the results suggest a single mechanism: homophily recovery yields class-relevant neighborhoods, sign-flip predicts when aggregation dilutes them, and normalization determines whether this mechanism is realized.

\subsection{Label-Efficiency (RQ3)}
\label{subsec:rq3}

\section{Conclusion and Future Work}
We propose \method, a topology-repair method for AML detection on transaction networks with weak homophily. \method contrasts the observed transaction graph with a behavioral homophily recovery graph, preserving transaction-topology information while adding repaired suspicious-class neighborhoods (Eq.~\ref{eq:concat}). The repair raises \hofinet edge homophily from 0.690 to 0.981. With only 1\% labeled nodes, \method achieves the best $F1_{\text{susp}}$ on all three datasets, surpassing the strongest supervised baseline by 0.010--0.016; with 10\% labels, tabular boosting matches \method on all three datasets, while \method continues to outperform every supervised graph baseline.

The results isolate the mechanism. Topology repair creates class-informative neighborhoods; sign-flip analysis explains when aggregation on the original graph erodes this structure; repaired-topology pooling refines the recovered signal conditionally; and normalization mainly stabilizes the bootstrap objective. The supporting BN-stability, homophily-recovery, and aggregation-signal results are stated in Appendix~\ref{app:theory}.

\textbf{Operational interpretation.} \method targets delayed-label, low-prevalence AML settings as a first-stage account-ranking model. An account with benign-dominated transaction neighbors can still be prioritized when its behavior-only recovered neighborhood contains laundering-like peers. Analysts then inspect the original transaction graph for STR preparation, mule-network reconstruction, and fund-flow tracing; thus, detection uses repaired topology, while investigation remains grounded in transaction topology. 

\textbf{Reproducibility.} We will release source code, training scripts, and seed-level raw outputs; \hofinet cannot be shared due to its data-sharing agreement, but AMLworld and AMLNet experiments are reproducible from public benchmarks.

\section{Theory: Statements and Empirical Checks}
\label{app:theory}
\subsection{Setup and Statements}
\label{app:theory_notation}

Let $V=V_S\cup V_B$ with $N:=|V|$, prevalence $\rho:=N_S/N$, and $N_{\min}:=\min(N_S,N_B)$. For a graph $G=(V,E)$,
\[
h(G):=\frac{|\{(u,v)\in E: y_u=y_v\}|}{|E|}.
\]
For a source node with feature $x$ and class $c$, an opposite-class candidate $X''\sim P_{1-c}$, and an independent same-class candidate $X'\sim P_c$, assume
\begin{equation}\label{eq:sep}
\Pr\big[\|x-X'\|<\|x-X''\| \mid x, X''\big]\ge 1-\epsilon .
\end{equation}
Let $G_{\text{knn},k}$ be the directed Euclidean $k$-NN graph before symmetrization.

\begin{theorem}[Probabilistic homophily recovery]
\label{thm:knn_homophily}
If $k < (1-\epsilon)(N_{\min}-1)$, then the directed k-NN graph $G_{\text{knn},k}$ built from behavioral features satisfies
\[
    \mathbb{E}\big[h(G_{\text{knn}, k})\big] \;\geq\; 1 - \tfrac{N - 1}{k} \exp\!\left(-\tfrac{((N_{\min}-1)(1-\epsilon) - k)^2}{2(N_{\min}-1)(1-\epsilon)}\right).
\]
\end{theorem}
\noindent The condition only requires $k$ to be well below the minority-class size; here $N_{\min}\in[1{,}487,9{,}644]$ and $k=10$.

For the transaction graph $G_{\text{tx}}$, we use a DCSBM~\cite{karrer2011stochastic},
\begin{equation}\label{eq:dcsbm}
    \Pr[(u, v) \in E_{\text{tx}}] \propto \theta_u\theta_v\,\omega_{y_u,y_v}.
\end{equation}
With $\Theta_c := \sum_{v:y_v=c}\theta_v$, define
\[
    \bar h_{\text{tx}}:=\frac{\Theta_S^2\omega_{SS}+\Theta_B^2\omega_{BB}}{\Theta_S^2\omega_{SS}+2\Theta_S\Theta_B\omega_{SB}+\Theta_B^2\omega_{BB}}.
\]
The node-averaged ego-heterophily prediction is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:eta_dcsbm}
\eta_h^{(\text{dcsbm})}=\rho\,\frac{\Theta_B\omega_{SB}}{\Theta_S\omega_{SS}+\Theta_B\omega_{SB}}+(1-\rho)\,\frac{\Theta_S\omega_{SB}}{\Theta_B\omega_{BB}+\Theta_S\omega_{SB}}.
\end{equation}

\begin{theorem}[Expected homophily gap]
\label{thm:combined}
Under the DCSBM approximation above, write $\bar h_{\text{tx}}=\alpha$. Then
\[
\mathbb{E}\big[h(G_{\text{knn}, k})\big] - \bar h_{\text{tx}} \geq 1 - \alpha - \delta(N, N_{\min}, k, \epsilon),
\]
where
\[
\delta(N,N_{\min},k,\epsilon)=\tfrac{N - 1}{k}\exp\!\left(-\tfrac{((N_{\min}-1)(1-\epsilon) - k)^2}{2(N_{\min}-1)(1-\epsilon)}\right)
\]
is the full finite-sample penalty from Theorem~\ref{thm:knn_homophily}.
\end{theorem}
\noindent When the separation term is negligible, the ideal homophily-gap reference is $1-\bar h_{\text{tx}}$.

For aggregation, write $z_v=s_v\mu+\eta_v$ with $\|\eta_v\|\le \sigma\|\mu\|$, define ego-heterophily $\eta_h := |\{u\in\mathcal{N}(v): y_u\neq y_v\}|/|\mathcal{N}(v)|$, and let $\varrho := \langle z_v^{(\cdot)}, \mu \rangle / \|\mu\|^2$.

\begin{theorem}[Mean pool signal flip]
\label{thm:meanpool}
Assume the noise projections satisfy $\mathbb{E}[\langle \eta_u,\mu\rangle]=0$. With $s_v=+1$, $\mathbb{E}[\varrho^{(\text{mean})}] = (1 + k(1 - 2\eta_h)) / (k+1)$. The mean-pooled signal flips sign (i.e., $\mathbb{E}[\varrho] < 0$) when $\eta_h > 1/2 + 1/(2k)$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proposition}[HAP signal preservation under small noise]
\label{thm:hap}
Let HAP use $w_{uv}=\max(0,\cos(z_u,z_v))$ and
\[
z_v^{(\mathrm{hap})}=\frac{z_v+\sum_{u\in\mathcal{N}(v)}w_{uv}z_u}{1+\sum_{u\in\mathcal{N}(v)}w_{uv}}.
\]
Assume $\sigma<1$ and cosine similarities separate classes in sign, so heterophilous neighbors receive zero weight and homophilous neighbors receive nonnegative weight. Then, for any $\eta_h$,
\[
\varrho^{(\mathrm{hap})}\ge 1-\sigma .
\]
\end{proposition}

\noindent\textbf{Proof sketch.} Theorem~\ref{thm:knn_homophily} applies a Chernoff lower-tail bound to the number of same-class candidates closer than each opposite-class candidate, then averages the cross-class edge count over the directed $k$-NN graph. Theorem~\ref{thm:combined} subtracts the DCSBM homophily ratio. Theorem~\ref{thm:meanpool} follows by projecting the ego and $k$ neighbors onto $\mu$: $\mathbb{E}[\varrho^{(\text{mean})}]=(1+k(1-2\eta_h))/(k+1)$, so the sign flips when $\eta_h>1/2+1/(2k)$. Proposition~\ref{thm:hap} follows because HAP keeps a convex combination of same-class projections, each at least $1-\sigma$. Full derivations are deferred to the supplementary material.
