\renewcommand{\shortauthors}{\method}


\begin{abstract}
In anti-money laundering (AML), suspicious-transaction labels usually arrive only after lengthy regulatory investigations, making labeled data scarce and delayed. Transaction graphs also place suspicious accounts among many benign counterparties; consequently, standard GNN aggregation can dilute the rare suspicious signal. To address this, we propose \method, a graph contrastive learning framework that builds an auxiliary behavioral k-NN view and learns node representations without labels. This view links accounts by similarity in behavioral transaction patterns (e.g., amount statistics, temporal frequencies, transaction-type entropy). On the Home/Firm banking Network (HOFINET), the suspicious ego-neighborhood ratio improves from Suspicious-Suspicious:Suspicious-Benign $=$ 1:5.7 in the transaction graph to 1:1.4 in the behavioral view, while global edge homophily rises from 0.690 to 0.981. \resultdep{With subgraph-level contrastive learning on this recovered view, \method improves suspicious-account detection across HOFINET, AMLworld, and AMLNet.} On HOFINET, its self-supervised representations are within 0.006 $F1_{\text{susp}}$ of a supervised MLP and outperform five fraud- and anomaly-specific GNNs. In a cold-start setting with only 1\% of labels, \method maintains $F1_{\text{susp}}{=}0.654$, demonstrating effective self-supervised view construction for label-delayed AML.
\end{abstract}

\keywords{anti-money laundering, graph contrastive learning, k-NN graph view, subgraph pooling, low-homophily, transaction networks}

\section{Introduction}
% Korean drafts below are kept inside \iffalse blocks for reference and are not compiled.

Money laundering, accounting for an estimated 2-5\% of global GDP~\cite{teichmann2020recent,unodc_ml}, has become more sophisticated through tactics such as structuring, layering, and mule networks (layered chains that route funds through third-party or otherwise normal accounts). GNN-based methods have thus been explored to learn structural patterns in transaction networks beyond rule-based detection~\cite{drezewski2015application,cheng2025graph}. However, money-laundering networks often violate two assumptions underlying standard GNN pipelines: class-homophilous neighborhoods and timely labels. This paper focuses on the resulting properties—suspicious--class neighborhood heterophily~\cite{zhu2020beyond,dou2020enhancing}, severe class imbalance, and label scarcity~\cite{cardoso2022laundrograph,lu2024graph}—and studies how self-supervised view construction can mitigate the mismatch.

\label{fig:intro_homophily}

\textbf{Challenge 1: suspicious-class neighborhood heterophily.} Money-laundering accounts intentionally interact with benign accounts to evade detection, forming mule networks. In a synthetic transaction graph based on HOFINET, about 85\% of suspicious accounts' neighbors are benign (S-S:S-B $=$ 1:5.7, Figure~\ref{fig:intro_homophily}). Consequently, the standard homophily assumption used by many GNNs~\cite{zhu2020beyond} is weak for the suspicious class: suspicious--suspicious links are rare even when global edge homophily is inflated by benign--benign edges.

\textbf{Challenges 2--3: class imbalance and label scarcity.} Suspicious accounts often constitute less than 2\% of all accounts~\cite{hilal2022financial}, and labels rely on retrospective investigations that often take more than six months~\cite{fatf2013guidance,kofia2024aml}. Fraud-specific supervised models can therefore face cold-start issues in production, because transaction networks contain few labels and weak in-neighborhood suspicious signals.

Graph Contrastive Learning (GCL) is a natural fit because it learns label-free graph representations by enforcing consistency across augmented views~\cite{you2020graph,velivckovic2018deep,thakoor2021large,bielak2022graph}. Standard GCL, however, constructs views by perturbing the original transaction graph; thus, the same neighborhood mismatch can persist across both views~\cite{zhu2020deep,zhu2021graph,hassani2020contrastive}. In particular, view perturbations do not change which accounts are linked by transactions; thus, suspicious accounts can remain embedded among many benign neighbors. Some variants avoid modifying the input graph (e.g., AFGRL~\cite{lee2022augmentation}, SimGRACE~\cite{xia2022simgrace}) but still leave transaction edges unchanged.

In contrast, MLGCL~\cite{shang2023mlgcl} and GCPAL~\cite{lu2024graph} add a k-NN auxiliary graph linking nodes close in feature space. When k-NN is built from all features, including connectivity-derived variables (e.g., degree or centrality), the auxiliary graph can still reflect the original topology and blur behavioral similarity with structural proximity. This raises two design questions: which features should define the auxiliary view, and at which level should contrastive learning be applied?

We answer these questions along two axes: view construction and contrastive level. For view construction, we build a behavioral k-NN auxiliary view from behavioral transaction features, increasing suspicious--suspicious neighborhoods at the view level. For the contrastive level, we move from node-level contrast to subgraph-level contrast by pooling each account with its neighbors in the behavioral k-NN view.

We propose \method (Figure~\ref{fig:framework}), a graph contrastive learning framework that separates topology-derived variables from behavioral features, thus enabling the auxiliary view to capture behavioral similarity rather than graph position. On HOFINET, the behavioral k-NN view improves the S-S:S-B ratio from 1:5.7 to 1:1.4, and edge homophily rises from 0.690 on the transaction graph to 0.981 on the behavioral view; AMLworld~\cite{amlpatterns2024realistic} and AMLNet exhibit the same pattern. Our contributions are summarized below.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Homophily recovery via behavioral view construction.} \method alleviates suspicious-class neighborhood heterophily by constructing the k-NN auxiliary view using only behavioral features. On HOFINET, this raises edge homophily from 0.690 (transaction graph) to 0.981 (behavioral k-NN), with a quantitative lower bound in §\ref{subsec:theory}.

    \item \textbf{Self-supervised learning under label delay and cold start.} Across three datasets with suspicious-account prevalences of 2.13\%, 1.23\%, and 13.52\%, \method remains competitive with fully supervised baselines and exceeds supervised GNN baselines in the low-prevalence AMLworld setting; in a 1\%-label cold-start regime, it achieves $F1_{\text{susp}}{=}0.654$.

    \item \textbf{Disentangling view construction and contrastive level.} Crossing graph view (transaction vs. behavioral k-NN) with contrastive level (node vs. subgraph) into four settings and applying them to ten encoders, we find that behavioral k-NN enables homophily recovery, while \resultdep{subgraph-level scoring on the recovered view strengthens suspicious-class evidence}. A DCSBM-based analysis and a formal characterization of BatchNorm collapse support these mechanisms.
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}\label{sec:related_work}
% Korean drafts below are kept inside \iffalse blocks for reference and are not compiled.
\subsection{Low-homophily/heterophily-aware GNNs}
Heterophily-aware GNN, such as H2GCN~\cite{zhu2020beyond}, Geom-GCN~\cite{pei2020geom}, FAGCN~\cite{bo2021beyond}, GBK-GNN~\cite{du2022gbk}, ACM-GCN~\cite{luan2022acmgcn}, GPR-GNN~\cite{chien2021adaptive}, and LINKX~\cite{zhu2021linkx}, improve message passing under low homophily by separating ego and neighbor embeddings, modifying propagation, or decoupling structure from attributes; however, they still rely on the original graph. In AML transaction networks, suspicious accounts intentionally connect to benign ones; thus, suspicious–suspicious edges are rare, and propagation changes alone cannot recover same-class neighborhoods. We instead build a behavioral k-NN auxiliary graph as a contrastive view that directly links suspicious accounts with similar transaction patterns.

\subsection{GCL and view design}
Graph Contrastive Learning (GCL) is a self-supervised framework that learns graph representations by enforcing consistency across multiple views. Methods such as DGI~\cite{velivckovic2018deep}, GRACE~\cite{zhu2020deep}, GraphCL~\cite{you2020graph}, GCA~\cite{zhu2021graph}, BGRL~\cite{thakoor2021large}, GBT~\cite{bielak2022graph}, and MVGRL~\cite{hassani2020contrastive} typically generate views by stochastically perturbing a single input graph. Augmentation-free methods, including AFGRL~\cite{lee2022augmentation}, SimGRACE~\cite{xia2022simgrace}, CCA-SSG~\cite{zhang2021canonical}, GraphMAE~\cite{hou2022graphmae}, and S2GAE~\cite{li2023s2gae}, avoid explicit graph augmentations but still form views at the representation or model level, without repairing transaction edges. On transaction graphs where suspicious accounts have heterophilous neighborhoods, these views preserve rather than alleviate the neighborhood mismatch. In contrast, we use a behavioral k-NN auxiliary graph as one view, restoring homophily at the graph level while retaining the self-supervised benefits of GCL.

\subsection{Fraud GNNs and label delay}
GNN-based fraud and AML models improve detection on transaction networks via fraud-aware neighbor selection, label-distribution-based sampling, reweighting, temporal modeling, and multi-relation inference~\cite{liu2020alleviating,dou2020enhancing,liu2021pick,peng2021reinforced,zhang2021fraudre,cheng2020graph,rao2020xfraud,wang2023label,cheng2025graph}. These methods, however, rely on abundant labels and assume suspicious labels are available during training. In AML, labels depend on retrospective investigations and often appear months after the transaction; thus, it is crucial to model suspicious patterns before labels arrive. This makes label-free representation learning a practical response to operational delay. We use labels only in the downstream evaluation head, mitigating label-delay issues.

\subsection{Unsupervised graph anomaly detection}
Unsupervised graph anomaly detection methods such as DOMINANT~\cite{ding2019dominant}, CoLA~\cite{liu2021anomaly}, ANEMONE~\cite{jin2021anemone}, BWGNN~\cite{tang2022rethinking}, and BOND~\cite{liu2022bond} detect anomalous nodes via reconstruction errors of node attributes and graph structure or node–subgraph contrastive learning. These methods are mainly evaluated on general graph benchmarks and are not tailored to AML transaction networks, where label delay, low prevalence, mule-network-based evasion, and suspicious-neighborhood imbalance co-occur. We adapt the view to AML by building it from behavioral features and analyze when subgraph pooling amplifies suspicious signals versus when it dilutes them. We also study collapse behavior under severe class imbalance.

\subsection{k-NN-view-based GCL}
MLGCL~\cite{shang2023mlgcl} and GCPAL~\cite{lu2024graph} are the closest prior works, as they also introduce a k-NN view. From an AML perspective, the key issue is which features define this view. We build it from behavioral features (amount, timing, type statistics) rather than graph connectivity; thus, the k-NN edges complement the transaction topology. Using all features would leak connectivity-based quantities (e.g., degree, centrality) into the auxiliary graph and prevent a topology-independent view. Prior k-NN view studies also conflate feature choice with contrastive level. In contrast, we construct the k-NN view from behavioral features only and jointly evaluate both design axes via four settings that cross view construction (transaction graph vs. behavioral k-NN) with contrastive level (node vs. subgraph).

Prior work has advanced heterophilous propagation, label-dependent detection, self-supervised representation learning, unsupervised anomaly detection, and k-NN-based view construction. Yet few methods jointly tackle the three key AML transaction-network challenges: suspicious-class neighborhood heterophily, label delay, and suspicious accounts that are behaviorally similar but transactionally disconnected. We target these by constructing a behavioral k-NN auxiliary view, applying subgraph-level contrastive learning on it, and independently validating the roles of view construction and contrastive level through theory and experiments.

\section{Method: \method}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Problem Formulation}
We model the transaction network as a directed graph $\mathcal{G}_{\text{trans}} = (\mathcal{V}, \mathcal{E}, \mathbf{X})$, where $\mathcal{V}$ is the set of accounts (nodes, $|\mathcal{V}|=N$), $\mathcal{E} \subseteq \mathcal{V} \times \mathcal{V}$ is the set of transfer transactions (edges), and $\mathbf{X} \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times F}$ is the node feature matrix. Each node $v_i$ has a suspicion label $y_i \in \{0, 1\}$, but we use a self-supervised setting that ignores labels during training. Our goal is to learn node representations $\mathbf{Z} \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times D}$ that identify suspicious accounts from the graph structure and node features alone.

\label{subsec:feature_taxonomy}
We group the node features available in a transaction network into behavioral, network-derived, and structural categories (Table~\ref{tab:features}).

The behavioral features capture transaction patterns via amount statistics, time-window frequencies, and transaction-type entropies (Table~\ref{tab:features}). Network-derived features (transaction counts and degrees) are input to the encoder but are excluded from the k-NN view, while structural features (centrality, hub/authority, topology) are used only for analysis and auxiliary comparison. We denote by $\mathbf{X}^{\text{enc}}$ the behavioral and network-derived features given to the GNN encoder, and by $\mathbf{X}^{\text{knn}}$ the 20 behavioral features used to build the k-NN graph. When suspicious accounts transact with benign ones to evade detection, the suspicious class exhibits low neighborhood homophily. We counter this by constructing a behavioral k-NN auxiliary graph as the contrastive view, linking accounts with similar behavior even if they are not directly connected by transactions.

\subsection{Behavioral k-NN View}
\label{subsec:knn_view}
A central component of \method is a behavioral-similarity k-NN graph built separately from the transaction edges. Standard augmentation-based GCL produces contrastive inputs by dropping edges or masking features on the original graph. We instead construct an auxiliary k-NN view independently of the edge structure of $\mathcal{G}_{\text{trans}}$ (Figure~\ref{fig:framework}).

\textbf{k-NN graph construction.} We first standardize the behavioral features $\mathbf{x}_i^{\text{knn}} \in \mathbb{R}^{20}$ to have a zero mean and unit variance to remove scale differences across variables, and then apply L2 normalization, such that similarity depends on the direction of the feature vector:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:l2norm}
    \bar{\mathbf{x}}_i = \text{StandardScaler}(\mathbf{x}_i^{\text{knn}}), \quad
    \tilde{\mathbf{x}}_i = \frac{\bar{\mathbf{x}}_i}{\|\bar{\mathbf{x}}_i\|_2}
\end{equation}

Because the Euclidean distance between two L2-normalized vectors is monotonic in their cosine similarity, we retrieve the $k$ nearest neighbors of each node $v_i$ with a ball-tree search over Euclidean distance. Connecting each node to these neighbors yields the k-NN graph $\mathcal{G}_{\text{knn}} = (\mathcal{V}, \mathcal{E}_{\text{knn}})$:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:knn_edge}
    \mathcal{E}_{\text{knn}} = \{(v_i, v_j) \mid v_j \in \operatorname{kNN}(v_i, k)\}.
\end{equation}

We use the symmetrized (bidirectional) version of this k-NN graph for message passing and pooling; thus, we add $(v_j, v_i)$ whenever $(v_i, v_j) \in \mathcal{E}_{\text{knn}}$.

Here, $\operatorname{kNN}(v_i, k)$ denotes the set of $k$ nodes closest (in Euclidean distance) to $\tilde{\mathbf{x}}_i$, with $k=10$ by default.

Money-laundering accounts that split transactions and layer transfers often show similar patterns in amount distributions, time-window frequencies, and transaction-type entropies. The behavioral k-NN graph thus links suspicious accounts that are not directly connected by transactions, recovering suspicious-to-suspicious neighborhoods missing in the transaction view.

\subsection{Dual-View GNN Encoder}
\label{subsec:dual_view}
Dual-view contrastive learning aligns a transaction view, capturing transfer structure, with an auxiliary k-NN view, capturing behavioral similarity, for the same account. \method forms these views from $\mathcal{G}_{\text{trans}}$ and $\mathcal{G}_{\text{knn}}$, with stochastic edge dropping and feature masking applied during training.

\textbf{Contrastive views.} Using only the transaction view mixes suspicious and benign accounts, while using only the auxiliary k-NN view loses transaction-structure information. \method learns a shared account representation across both views; thus, embeddings capture both transaction structure and behavioral similarity.

\textbf{GNN encoder.} We apply stochastic edge dropping and feature masking ($p_e{=}p_f{=}0.3$) to the transaction and k-NN graphs to form two views. A shared online encoder $f_\theta$ outputs embeddings $\mathbf{H}^{(1)} = f_\theta(\mathbf{X}^{\text{enc}}, \tilde{\mathcal{E}}_{\text{trans}})$ and $\mathbf{H}^{(2)} = f_\theta(\mathbf{X}^{\text{enc}}, \tilde{\mathcal{E}}_{\text{knn}})$. The target encoder $f_\xi$ is an EMA copy that provides stop-gradient representations for the opposite BYOL branch. The online encoder stacks GCNConv~\cite{kipf2016semi} layers with batch normalization, nonlinear activation, and dropout. \method is encoder-agnostic and supports 10 encoders, including GIN~\cite{xu2018powerful}, MVGRL, and GBT; Section~\ref{subsec:rq2} studies the effect of batch normalization.

\subsection{Subgraph Pooling}
\label{subsec:subgraph_pool}
\textbf{Subgraph-level signal aggregation.} \method compares not only individual node representations but also a subgraph representation that bundles each account with its neighbors (Subgraph Pooling in Figure~\ref{fig:framework}). This reflects the idea that whether an account is suspicious often becomes clearer from its relationships with neighboring counterparts than from its own attributes alone.

\textbf{Subgraph representation.} For each view $t \in \{1,2\}$, we form the subgraph representation of node $v_i$ by averaging its own representation with the representations of its neighbors in that view:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:subgraph_pool}
    \mathbf{s}_i^{(t)} = \frac{1}{|\mathcal{N}^{(t)}(v_i)| + 1}
    \left(\mathbf{h}_i^{(t)} + \sum_{v_j \in \mathcal{N}^{(t)}(v_i)} \mathbf{h}_j^{(t)}\right)
\end{equation}

Here $\mathcal{N}^{(1)}(v_i)$ is the neighborhood of $v_i$ in the transaction view, and $\mathcal{N}^{(2)}(v_i)$ is its neighborhood in the auxiliary k-NN view. For both views, we perform message passing and pooling on a bidirected graph; hence, $\mathcal{N}^{(t)}(v_i)$ can be interpreted as the union of in- and out-neighbors. The operation is implemented efficiently with \texttt{scatter\_add}. The mean pooling in Eq.~\eqref{eq:subgraph_pool} is the default in \method.

\textbf{Pooling effect by view.} Subgraph pooling is applied separately to each view's neighborhood: transaction-graph neighbors for the transaction view and k-NN neighbors for the auxiliary view. Because pooling aggregates neighbor information, the choice of view shapes the outcome. The behavioral k-NN graph is designed to place behaviorally similar suspicious accounts closer together; therefore, pooling on this view can preserve or strengthen suspicious signals. In the transaction graph, suspicious accounts are often linked to benign ones (high S-B ratio); consequently, pooling can dilute suspicious signals with benign information.

\textbf{Conditional amplification and alternative pooling.} The pooling effect depends on view homophily and encoder normalization depth. With per-layer batch normalization, ego-neighborhoods are partly normalized, leaving little room for additional pooling gains. With only final batch normalization (BGRL) or none (DGI, MVGRL, GRACE, GCA), pooling can have a larger marginal effect. When most neighbors belong to another class, averaging can weaken or even invert the suspicious signal (Theorem~\ref{thm:meanpool}; Section~\ref{subsec:theory}). We also define heterophily-aware pooling (HAP, cosine-similarity weights) to characterize when similarity weighting preserves the signal better than mean pooling (Proposition~\ref{thm:hap}); the main experiments use mean pooling for simplicity.

\subsection{Training and Inference}
\label{subsec:contrastive_learning}
\textbf{Projector and BYOL loss.} Subgraph-pooled representations pass through a projector $g$ and a predictor $q$ and are trained with a BYOL-style loss~\cite{grill2020bootstrap} (Projector and BYOL Loss in Figure~\ref{fig:framework}). Instead of pushing other accounts as negatives, the loss pulls together representations of the same account across the two views. Both views are encoded by the online encoder $f_\theta$ and the target encoder $f_\xi$, and the loss sums the $1{\to}2$ and $2{\to}1$ directions. The target encoder is a momentum copy of the online encoder ($\xi \leftarrow m\xi + (1-m)\theta$, $m=0.99$), retaining most of the previous target while slowly incorporating new information.

We denote the loss input representation by $\mathbf{r}_i^{(t)}$. In our setting, $\mathbf{r}_i^{(t)}=\mathbf{s}_i^{(t)}$; in the node-level ablation, $\mathbf{r}_i^{(t)}=\mathbf{h}_i^{(t)}$. Let $\mathbf{r}_{\xi,i}^{(t)}$ be the corresponding target-branch representation, and $g_\xi$ the target-branch projector. The BYOL loss maximizes cosine similarity between representations from the two views:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:loss}
    \mathcal{L} = \sum_{t \in \{1,2\}} \left(2 - 2 \cdot \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N} \frac{q(g(\mathbf{r}_i^{(t)})) \cdot g_\xi(\mathbf{r}_{\xi,i}^{(3-t)})}{\|q(g(\mathbf{r}_i^{(t)}))\| \cdot \|g_\xi(\mathbf{r}_{\xi,i}^{(3-t)})\|}\right)
\end{equation}

Here $g$ and $q$ are the projection and predictor heads, and $\mathbf{r}_{\xi,i}^{(\cdot)}$ is the input representation obtained from the target encoder branch.

\textbf{Inference.} At inference, we freeze the encoder and concatenate the two view representations to obtain the final embedding (right side of Figure~\ref{fig:framework}). In the subgraph-level setting, we concatenate subgraph representations:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:concat}
    \mathbf{z}_i = [\mathbf{s}_i^{(1)} \| \mathbf{s}_i^{(2)}].
\end{equation}
For node-level ablations, we use $\mathbf{z}_i = [\mathbf{h}_i^{(1)} \| \mathbf{h}_i^{(2)}]$.

Implementation details and seed-level scripts will be released with the code.

\section{Theoretical Analysis: Homophily Recovery and Signal Preservation}
\label{subsec:theory}

We now analyze how each \method component behaves when suspicious accounts have low-homophily neighborhoods in the transaction graph. Two central issues arise: (1) a view derived directly from transaction edges rarely links suspicious accounts, resulting in weak suspicious-class homophily; and (2) aggregating information from such heterophilous neighbors can dilute or even invert the suspicious-class signal. Using theorems and a proposition, we identify when a behavioral k-NN view can restore homophily and how different pooling mechanisms affect signal preservation.

We model the transaction graph with a degree-corrected stochastic block model (DCSBM)~\cite{karrer2011stochastic} and assume behavioral features are i.i.d. with class-conditional distributions $x_v \mid y_v = c \sim P_c$. We define homophily $h(G) := |\{(u,v) \in E : y_u = y_v\}| / |E|$, set $|V_S| = \rho N$, and let $N_{\min} = \min(|V_S|, |V_B|)$. In behavioral space, we assume probabilistic class separability: a same-class node is closer to a query than a different-class node with a probability of at least $1 - \epsilon$.

\subsection{View-level Homophily Recovery}
\label{subsec:theory_view}

\begin{theorem}[Probabilistic homophily recovery]
\label{thm:knn_homophily}
For $k \leq (1-\epsilon)(N_{\min}-1) - 1$, the behavioral k-NN graph $G_{\text{knn},k}$ satisfies the homophily lower bound
\[
    h(G_{\text{knn}, k}) \;\geq\; 1 - \tfrac{N - 1}{k} \exp\!\left(-\tfrac{((N_{\min}-1)(1-\epsilon) - k)^2}{2(N_{\min}-1)(1-\epsilon)}\right).
\]
\end{theorem}

\begin{theorem}[Combined homophily gap]\label{thm:combined}
Let $h(G_{\text{tx}}) = \alpha$ under the DCSBM, and let $\delta$ denote the error term on the right-hand side of Theorem~\ref{thm:knn_homophily}. Then
\[
    h(G_{\text{knn}, k}) - h(G_{\text{tx}})
    \geq 1 - \alpha - \delta(N, N_{\min}, k, \epsilon).
\]
\end{theorem}

\subsection{Aggregation-level Signal Preservation}
\label{subsec:theory_pool}

We now focus on the aggregation step. We decompose the hidden representation of node $v$ as $z_v = s_v \mu + \eta_v$, where $s_v \in \{\pm 1\}$ denotes the class signal, $\mu$ is the discriminative direction separating suspicious from benign nodes, and $\eta_v$ is a bounded noise term with $\|\eta_v\| \leq \epsilon \|\mu\|$. We quantify the heterophily of the 1-hop ego neighborhood of $v$ by
\[
\eta_h := \frac{|\{u \in \mathcal{N}(v) : y_u \neq y_v\}|}{|\mathcal{N}(v)|}.
\]
The $\eta_h$ values reported in the main text are dataset-level averages, obtained by computing this fraction for every node in the transaction graph.

To assess how effectively pooling retains the discriminative direction $\mu$, we compare the post-pooling projection
\[
\rho := \frac{\langle z_v^{(\cdot)}, \mu \rangle}{\|\mu\|^2}
\]
under mean pooling and heterophily-aware pooling (HAP, using cosine-similarity weights). The expectation is taken with the ego node $v$ and its heterophily $\eta_h$ fixed, while the class labels of its neighbors are treated as random. Without loss of generality, we set $s_v = +1$; hence, any neighbor $u$ satisfies
\[
\mathbb{E}[s_u \mid u \in \mathcal{N}(v)] = (1-\eta_h)(+1) + \eta_h(-1) = 1 - 2\eta_h.
\]

\begin{theorem}[Mean-pool signal reversal]
\label{thm:meanpool}
With mean pooling, the expected post-pooling projection is $\mathbb{E}[\rho^{(\text{mean})}] = \frac{1 + k(1 - 2\eta_h)}{k+1}$. Consequently, the expected signal flips sign once $\eta_h > \tfrac{1}{2} + \tfrac{1}{2k}$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proposition}[HAP signal preservation under small-noise regime]
\label{thm:hap}
Assume a small-noise regime with $\epsilon < 1/\sqrt{2}$ and preservation of the cosine similarity sign. Then, for any $\eta_h$, the HAP post-pooling expectation satisfies
\[
\mathbb{E}[\rho^{(\text{hap})}] \geq 1 - 2\epsilon^2 - 4\epsilon^2 \eta_h.
\]
\end{proposition}

Figure~\ref{fig:theory_inset} shows the sign-flip threshold from Theorem~\ref{thm:meanpool} and the measured $\eta_h$ on the three datasets. All three exceed $\eta_h^* = 0.55$ at $k{=}10$; therefore, mean pooling on the transaction graph places the suspicious signal in the sign-flip regime, explaining why setting (c) (transaction graph plus mean pool) can degrade performance.

\subsection{Setting-level Performance Prediction}
\label{subsec:theory_pred}

When $\eta_h^{\text{tx}} > 1/2 + 1/(2 \bar{d}_{\text{tx}})$, with $\bar{d}_{\text{tx}}$ the average ego-neighborhood size in the transaction graph, and the encoder uses batch normalization, the analysis predicts $F1(b) \approx F1(d) > F1(a) > F1(c)$, combining view-level homophily recovery with mean-pool signal reversal. On HOFINET, all six BN-equipped encoders follow this ordering ($\Delta(b{-}a) \in [+0.255, +0.405]$, $\Delta(c{-}a) \in [-0.070, -0.052]$).

Theorems~\ref{thm:knn_homophily}--\ref{thm:combined} specify when a behavioral k-NN view can offset scarce suspicious--suspicious transaction edges, while Theorem~\ref{thm:meanpool} and Proposition~\ref{thm:hap} compare pooling choices under high neighborhood heterophily. Together, they motivate the four-setting ablation used in the experiments below.

\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}
We assess \method in four phases, moving from mechanism to practical deployment. First, we examine whether the behavioral k-NN auxiliary view can make up for missing links between suspicious accounts and restore view-level homophily, thereby boosting downstream performance. Second, we investigate whether applying subgraph pooling on this reconstructed view can further strengthen the suspicious-class signal and how this effect depends on encoder normalization, with particular attention to BatchNorm. Third, we benchmark the resulting self-supervised representations against supervised baselines under label delay and cold-start conditions, reflecting realistic AML settings. Fourth, we test whether the same mechanism and empirical patterns carry over to AML datasets that differ in prevalence and scale. Together, these experiments address the following research questions.

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{RQ1. Effectiveness of behavioral k-NN view:} Does the behavioral k-NN auxiliary view compensate for missing edges between suspicious accounts in the transaction graph and restore homophily at the view level?
\item \textbf{RQ2. Subgraph aggregation and encoder stability:} Does subgraph pooling on the recovered view further strengthen suspicious signals, and how does this depend on encoder normalization, particularly BatchNorm?
\item \textbf{RQ3. Self-supervised AML detection under label scarcity:} Does the self-supervised representation from \method remain competitive with supervised models under label delay and cold start?
\item \textbf{RQ4. Robustness across AML regimes:} Do the same mechanisms and trends apply to AML datasets with different prevalence and scale?
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Experimental Setting}
\textbf{Datasets.} We use three AML datasets (Table~\ref{tab:datasets}). HOFINET includes about 4.7M synthetic transactions derived from real activity on the Korean interbank settlement network, modeling 40 months of household and corporate transfers (2021Q3--2024Q4). AMLworld~\cite{amlpatterns2024realistic}, the NeurIPS 2023 open synthetic benchmark, models eight transaction patterns (e.g., fan-out, scatter-gather, cycle); we use the HI-Small variant (high illicit ratio, small size). AMLNet~\cite{huda2025amlnet}, a small synthetic benchmark for the high-prevalence regime, shares AMLworld’s feature schema.

\textbf{Account-level labels.} All three datasets provide transaction-level suspicious labels. Following standard graph-based AML and fraud detection practice~\cite{dou2020enhancing,liu2021pick}, we max-aggregate to the account level: an account is suspicious (label$=1$) if it appears on either side of any suspicious transaction, and normal (label$=0$) otherwise. The resulting suspicious-account ratios are 2.13\% (HOFINET), 1.23\% (AMLworld), and 13.52\% (AMLNet) (Table~\ref{tab:datasets}).

\textbf{Ablation settings and encoders.} We evaluate four settings that cross graph view and contrastive level: (a) transaction graph + node contrast, (b) behavioral k-NN + node contrast, (c) transaction graph + subgraph pooling, and (d) behavioral k-NN + subgraph pooling. All encoders use the same BYOL-style bootstrap loss (Eq.~\eqref{eq:loss}) and hyperparameters (lr=5e-4, hidden=256, $L$=2, dropout=0.2, 200 epochs).

\textbf{Supervised baselines.} We apply class-weight correction to 11 supervised models: generic GNN (GCN~\cite{kipf2016semi}, GAT~\cite{velivckovic2017graph}, GraphSAGE~\cite{hamilton2017inductive}), tabular methods (MLP, LightGBM~\cite{lightgbm2017}, XGBoost~\cite{xgboost2016}), and fraud- and anomaly-specific GNNs (CARE-GNN~\cite{dou2020enhancing}, PC-GNN~\cite{liu2021pick}, BWGNN~\cite{tang2022rethinking}, GAGA~\cite{wang2023label}, ConsisGAD~\cite{chen2024consisgad}). All three datasets are homogeneous single-relation graphs of account-to-account transfers; therefore, the multi-relation components of the fraud-specific GNNs do not apply; we retain only their single-relation core mechanisms (see Section~\ref{subsec:rq3}).

\textbf{Evaluation protocol.} The primary metric is suspicious-class F1 ($F1_{\text{susp}}$); AUROC and AUPRC are secondary. We use a 10\%/10\%/80\% train/val/test split (encoders are trained self-supervised; labels only train the LogisticRegression head; therefore, 10\% labeled data suffices). For paired comparisons, the split is fixed per seed. We report mean$\pm$std over four seeds (2024--2027). Statistical significance uses a 4-seed paired $t$-test with Holm--Bonferroni correction; seed-level raw results are in the public repository.

\textbf{Evaluation environment.} We evaluate self-supervised models by freezing the encoder and training LogisticRegression~\cite{pedregosa2011scikit} on embeddings $\mathbf{z}_i = [\mathbf{s}_i^{(1)} \| \mathbf{s}_i^{(2)}]$. All experiments use Python 3.12, PyTorch 2.10, CUDA 12.8, and NVIDIA H100 80GB GPUs.

\begin{theorem}[BN-conditional collapse]\label{thm:bn_collapse}
Under the BYOL-style bootstrap loss~\eqref{eq:loss}, the trivial constant solution $f_\theta(\cdot) \equiv c$ of an encoder $f_\theta$ is an attainable global minimum at $\mathcal{L} = 0$ only when $f_\theta$ contains no BN layer. An encoder with at least one BN layer blocks the trivial constant solution by the definition of batch normalization.
\end{theorem}

\textbf{Empirical confirmation of BN-conditional collapse.} At setting (a), BN-less encoders achieve $F1_{\text{susp}} \approx 0.04$, matching the HOFINET random-classifier upper bound $2\rho/(1+\rho) \approx 0.042$ (the maximum $F1_{\text{susp}}$ at positive rate $\rho$), thereby empirically confirming structural collapse.
The practical severity depends on prevalence: at low prevalence ($\rho \leq 2\%$), the bound is already $\approx 0.04$; hence, collapse leaves little usable suspicious-class signal. On AMLNet ($\rho = 13.52\%$), BN-less DGI still reaches $F1_{\text{susp}} \approx 0.40$ (Section~\ref{subsec:rq4}); consequently, the absolute effect is smaller. RQ2 thus indicates that \method's gains arise from combining the homophily-recovered view, appropriate normalization, and conditional subgraph amplification, rather than from any single pooling ablation.

\section{Conclusion and Future Work}

We introduced \method, a graph contrastive learning framework for AML detection in low-homophily transaction networks. Its central design is to build an auxiliary behavioral k-NN view from transaction-behavior features rather than from the original topology, raising HOFINET edge homophily from 0.690 to 0.981. The resulting self-supervised representations are competitive with supervised models: they are within 0.006 and 0.035 $F1_{\text{susp}}$ of the best supervised baselines on HOFINET and AMLNet, respectively, and above all supervised GNN baselines on AMLworld. Under cold-start label scarcity, \method maintains $F1_{\text{susp}}=0.654$ with only 1\% downstream labels. \resultdep{The experiments also clarify the role of the second design axis: on the recovered behavioral view, subgraph-level contrast strengthens suspicious-class evidence}, while BatchNorm prevents trivial collapse under the BYOL-style objective. These findings are supported by the homophily-recovery, aggregation-signal, and BN-collapse analyses in Theorems~\ref{thm:knn_homophily}--\ref{thm:bn_collapse}.

\method is designed for the operational regimes common in AML: delayed labels, low prevalence, and transaction neighborhoods that are anti-homophilous for the suspicious class. When abundant labels are available, high-prevalence supervised models such as MLP, XGBoost, or LightGBM can be marginally stronger. In settings with a six-month label delay and a 0.5\%--2\% suspicious rate, self-supervised behavioral view construction provides a trainable alternative before reliable labels exist.

Several limitations remain. The evaluation is transductive and does not yet use a temporal hold-out split. The DCSBM analysis assumes i.i.d. class-conditional behavioral features, an approximation that may be violated by deliberate evasion patterns such as mule networks. Finally, all three benchmarks used here are synthetic; thus, validation on real bank transaction data remains necessary. Future work will extend \method to dynamic and inductive graphs, evaluate temporal splits, generalize the collapse analysis beyond BYOL-style objectives, run shadow deployments in operational AML workflows, and refine cross-domain transfer.

\paragraph{Reproducibility.} We will release the source code, training scripts, and seed-level raw results. HOFINET cannot be redistributed due to regulatory restrictions; however, the AMLworld and AMLNet experiments are reproducible from public benchmarks.

\section{Extended Related Work}\label{app:rw_extended}

\subsection{GNN-based AML and Fraud Detection}\label{app:rw_aml}

Early fraud-aware GNN such as GraphConsis~\cite{liu2020alleviating} and CARE-GNN~\cite{dou2020enhancing} exploit the observation that fraudsters often form intentionally inconsistent links to benign users, introducing label-distribution-based neighbor filtering through relabeling or similarity thresholds. PC-GNN~\cite{liu2021pick} generalizes this idea with prior sampling, while RioGNN~\cite{peng2021reinforced} learns multi-relation neighbor selection through reinforcement learning. Temporal fraud models include GTAN~\cite{cheng2020graph}, which applies attentive temporal aggregation on transaction graphs, and xFraud~\cite{rao2020xfraud}, which provides explainable detection on heterogeneous account-device-IP graphs. FraudRE~\cite{zhang2021fraudre} addresses class imbalance through reweighting, and LHFD~\cite{wang2023label} studies label-aware multi-class fraud detection. These methods generally assume that labeled fraud examples are available during training. In AML operations, however, labels depend on retrospective investigations and can lag by six months or more~\cite{hilal2022financial}; \method targets this cold-start gap by learning the representation without labels.

\subsection{Heterophily-aware GNNs}\label{app:rw_hetero}

H2GCN~\cite{zhu2020beyond} separates ego and neighbor embeddings and combines higher-order neighborhoods, while Geom-GCN~\cite{pei2020geom} redefines neighborhoods through latent distances. From a spectral perspective, FAGCN~\cite{bo2021beyond} separates low- and high-frequency signals, and GPR-GNN~\cite{chien2021adaptive} uses adaptive PageRank propagation. GBK-GNN~\cite{du2022gbk} gates homophilous and heterophilous neighbors, ACM-GCN~\cite{luan2022acmgcn} uses multi-channel propagation, and LINKX~\cite{zhu2021linkx} decouples graph structure from node features to handle strong heterophily. These models modify message passing while keeping the original graph fixed. That is limiting for transaction networks in which the observed neighbors themselves often lack suspicious-class signals. \method takes a complementary route: it keeps the encoder simple and changes the auxiliary graph view.

\subsection{Augmentation-free and Feature-view GCL}\label{app:rw_gcl_aug}

AFGRL~\cite{lee2022augmentation} finds homophilous anchors in representation space, and SimGRACE~\cite{xia2022simgrace} creates views through model perturbation. CCA-SSG~\cite{zhang2021canonical} reduces redundancy through canonical correlation, while GraphMAE~\cite{hou2022graphmae} and S2GAE~\cite{li2023s2gae} replace augmentation with masking and reconstruction. Because these approaches do not introduce a separate graph structure, the anti-homophily of the transaction graph remains embedded in the contrastive views. MLGCL~\cite{shang2023mlgcl} and GCPAL~\cite{lu2024graph} are the closest baselines because they explicitly use a k-NN view. Their limitations are that they build k-NN graphs from all available features, which weakens orthogonality to the transaction topology; they do not isolate the effect of k-NN feature choice; and they do not separate node-level from subgraph-level contrast. \method addresses these points by separating behavioral from structural features and by evaluating the four view/level settings directly.

\subsection{Graph Anomaly Detection}\label{app:rw_gad}

DOMINANT~\cite{ding2019dominant} derives unsupervised anomaly scores by jointly reconstructing attributes and structure. CoLA~\cite{liu2021anomaly} uses node-subgraph contrast, and ANEMONE~\cite{jin2021anemone} extends this idea to multi-scale patch-context objectives. BWGNN~\cite{tang2022rethinking} analyzes anomalies in the spectral domain and introduces band-pass filtering. The BOND benchmark~\cite{liu2022bond} shows that such unsupervised methods can be competitive on standard anomaly datasets. Most of these benchmarks, however, are citation or co-author graphs such as Cora, Citeseer, and ACM. Their graph statistics differ sharply from AML networks, where suspicious accounts deliberately interact with benign accounts to evade detection. \method closes this domain gap through transaction-specific view construction and an explicit analysis of BatchNorm-conditional collapse.

\section{Theory: Proofs and Empirical Checks}\label{app:theory}

This appendix gives the setup, proof sketches, DCSBM derivations, HOFINET numerical checks, and cross-dataset empirical checks for the theory in Section~\ref{subsec:theory}.

\subsection{Setup and Notation}\label{app:theory_notation}

Let $V = V_S \cup V_B$ denote the suspicious ($S$) and benign ($B$) node sets. Each node $v$ has behavioral features $x_v \in \mathbb{R}^d$ drawn from a class-conditional distribution $x_v \mid y_v = c \sim P_c$ for $c \in \{S,B\}$, independently across nodes. For any $x \in \text{supp}(P_y)$, $y' \neq y$, and independent samples $X' \sim P_y$, $X'' \sim P_{y'}$, assume
\begin{equation}\label{eq:sep}
    \Pr\big[\|x - X'\| < \|x - X''\|\big] \;\geq\; 1 - \epsilon.
\end{equation}
Thus a same-class sample is closer than a cross-class sample with probability at least $1-\epsilon$; $\epsilon=0$ gives deterministic separation. $G_{\text{knn},k}$ is the Euclidean k-NN graph in which each node connects to its $k$ nearest neighbors.

\subsection{View-level Homophily Recovery}\label{app:theory_view}

\begin{equation}\label{eq:knn_bound}
    h(G_{\text{knn}, k}) \;\geq\; 1 - \frac{N - 1}{k} \exp\!\left(-\frac{\big((N_{\min}-1)(1-\epsilon) - k\big)^2}{2(N_{\min}-1)(1-\epsilon)}\right).
\end{equation}

For a cross-class candidate $B_j$ to enter the top-$k$ list of a suspicious source $u$, it must satisfy $Z_j := |\{i : \|x_u - x_{S_i}\| < \|x_u - x_{B_j}\|\}| < k$. By assumption~\eqref{eq:sep}, $Z_j$ stochastically dominates $\text{Binom}(N_S{-}1,1{-}\epsilon)$. Applying a Chernoff lower-tail bound and a union bound gives Eq.~\eqref{eq:knn_bound}. When $\epsilon=0$, the deterministic limit is $h(G_{\text{knn},k})=1$.

For the transaction graph $G_{\text{tx}}$, we use a degree-corrected stochastic block model (DCSBM)~\cite{karrer2011stochastic}. With activity factor $\theta_v>0$ for node $v$ and class-pair coupling $\omega_{y_u,y_v}$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:dcsbm}
    \Pr[(u, v) \in E_{\text{tx}}] \;\propto\; \theta_u \theta_v \cdot \omega_{y_u, y_v}.
\end{equation}
Let $\Theta_c := \sum_{v: y_v=c}\theta_v$. Then
\[
    h(G_{\text{tx}}) = \frac{\Theta_S^2 \omega_{SS} + \Theta_B^2 \omega_{BB}}{\Theta_S^2 \omega_{SS} + 2\Theta_S\Theta_B \omega_{SB} + \Theta_B^2 \omega_{BB}}.
\]
In HOFINET, suspicious accounts often act as high-activity hubs, inflating $\Theta_S/\Theta_B$ and yielding $h(G_{\text{tx}}) < \rho^2 + (1-\rho)^2$, the random-mixing baseline.

Theorem~\ref{thm:knn_homophily} gives $h(G_{\text{knn},k}) \geq 1-\delta(N,N_{\min},k,\epsilon)$. Setting $h(G_{\text{tx}})=\alpha$ under the DCSBM and subtracting it from both sides gives the following lower bound on the homophily gap:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:gap}
    h(G_{\text{knn}, k}) - h(G_{\text{tx}}) \;\geq\; 1 - \alpha - \tfrac{N - 1}{k} \exp\!\left(-\tfrac{((N_{\min}-1)(1-\epsilon) - k)^2}{2(N_{\min}-1)(1-\epsilon)}\right).
\end{equation}
For HOFINET ($N=452{,}816$, $N_{\min}=9{,}644$, $k=10$, $\alpha=0.690$), taking $\epsilon=0.05$ gives the bound $1-0.690-10^{-1986}\approx0.310$. The measured gap is $0.981-0.690=0.291$, about 0.019 below the bound. This difference is consistent with imperfect separability near class boundaries and with structural dependencies (e.g., mule networks) that deviate from the DCSBM assumptions. Accordingly, we interpret the bound as a sufficient condition for substantial homophily recovery, rather than as a point-accurate predictor for every dataset detail.

\subsection{Aggregation-level Signal Preservation}\label{app:theory_pool}

Write the hidden representation of node $v$ as $z_v=s_v\mu+\eta_v \in \mathbb{R}^d$. Here $s_v\in\{+1,-1\}$ is the signed suspicious/benign signal, $\mu\in\mathbb{R}^d$ is the class-discriminative direction, and $\eta_v\perp\mu$ is i.i.d.\ zero-mean noise with $\|\eta_v\|\leq\epsilon\|\mu\|$. We compare mean pooling (Eq.~\eqref{eq:subgraph_pool}) with heterophily-aware pooling, $z_v^{(\text{hap})}=(z_v+\sum_u w_{uv}z_u)/(1+\sum_u w_{uv})$, where $w_{uv}=\max(\cos(z_u,z_v),0)$. The post-pool signal is $\rho^{(\cdot)}:=\langle z_v^{(\cdot)},\mu\rangle/\|\mu\|^2$.

Without loss of generality, take the ego node $v$ to be suspicious; hence, $s_v=+1$. If neighbor $u$ has the same class with probability $1-\eta_h$ and the opposite class with probability $\eta_h$, then
\[
    \mathbb{E}[s_u \mid u \in \mathcal{N}(v)] = (1-\eta_h)(+1) + \eta_h(-1) = 1 - 2\eta_h .
\]
The expectation is over the class composition of the neighborhood, conditioning on the ego node and $\eta_h$. The expected projection after mean pooling is therefore
\[
    \mathbb{E}[\rho^{(\text{mean})}]
    = \frac{s_v + \sum_{u \in \mathcal{N}(v)} \mathbb{E}[s_u]}{k+1}
    = \frac{1 + k(1 - 2\eta_h)}{k+1}
\]
The variance is at most $\epsilon^2/(k+1)$ by the i.i.d.\ noise assumption and $\|\eta_v\|^2\leq\epsilon^2\|\mu\|^2$.

Under the small-noise approximation, a cross-class neighbor satisfies $\cos(z_u,z_v)=-1+O(\epsilon^2)<0$; therefore, $w_{uv}=0$. A same-class neighbor has $\cos(z_u,z_v)=1-O(\epsilon^2)$. HAP therefore removes the cross-class contribution to first order, yielding the conservative $\mu$-projection lower bound in Proposition~\ref{thm:hap}.

\paragraph{Approximate pool-level implication.}
For $\eta_h \in [1/2,1]$, $\mathbb{E}[\rho^{(\text{hap})}-\rho^{(\text{mean})}] \geq 2\eta_h k/(k+1)-O(\epsilon^2)$.

Under the DCSBM in Eq.~\eqref{eq:dcsbm}, the ego-neighborhood heterophily of a suspicious source $v$ can be written as
\begin{equation}\label{eq:eta_dcsbm}
    \eta_h^{(v)} \;=\; \frac{1}{1 + (\Theta_S' / \Theta_B)(\omega_{SS} / \omega_{SB})}, \quad \Theta_S' = \Theta_S - \theta_v.
\end{equation}
With HOFINET parameters ($\Theta_S \approx 6.94\times10^4$, $\Theta_B \approx 6.20\times10^5$, $\omega_{SS}/\omega_{SB}\approx3.45$), a typical suspicious source has $\eta_h\approx0.72$, while a high-activity hub reaches about 0.95. The measured aggregate value, $\eta_h\approx0.85$ (S-S:S-B = 1:5.7), falls within this range.

Plugging in $\eta_h=0.85$, $k=10$, and $\epsilon=0.2$ gives $\mathbb{E}[\rho^{(\text{mean})}]\approx-0.545$; therefore, mean pooling can flip the signal. Proposition~\ref{thm:hap}, in contrast, gives the conservative bound $\mathbb{E}[\rho^{(\text{hap})}]\geq0.784$. Thus setting (c), transaction graph plus mean pooling, can invert the suspicious direction, while HAP preserves a positive projection.

\subsection{Cross-Dataset Empirical Check}\label{app:theory_verify}

Table~\ref{tab:theory_verify} compares the idealized bound from Theorem~\ref{thm:combined} and the DCSBM prediction in Eq.~\eqref{eq:eta_dcsbm} with measured values on all three datasets. HOFINET and AMLworld are within about 0.02 of the $\epsilon=0.05$ homophily-gap bound. AMLNet has a smaller measured gap (0.109) than the bound (0.209), which is plausible given its much smaller graph size (11K nodes) and higher prevalence (13.5\%), both of which weaken the class-separability approximation. The measured ego-heterophily exceeds the mean-pool flip threshold $1/2+1/(2k)=0.55$ for $k=10$ on all datasets. The DCSBM underestimates aggregate $\eta_h$ by 5--11\%, suggesting additional anti-homophily mechanisms beyond degree heterogeneity, such as deliberate layering and mule-network behavior.

\section{Datasets: Detailed Description}\label{app:datasets}

\subsection{HOFINET}\label{app:dataset_hofinet}

HOFINET is a synthetic transaction dataset derived from activity on Korea's Home and Firm Banking Network, an inter-bank transfer network operated by the Korea Financial Telecommunications \& Clearings Institute (KFTC). The network integrates household channels such as internet and mobile banking with corporate channels such as firm banking. The snapshot used in this study contains 452,816 accounts and 4,732,130 directed multi-edges, with a suspicious-account ratio of 2.13\% (9,644 accounts). The simulator models six AML typologies, including structuring, layering, and mule-network patterns, over 40 months from 2021Q3 to 2024Q4.

\subsection{AMLworld}\label{app:dataset_amlworld}

AMLworld~\cite{amlpatterns2024realistic} is a public synthetic AML benchmark from the NeurIPS 2023 Datasets \& Benchmarks Track. We use the HI-Small variant, which contains 515,088 accounts and 5,078,345 directed multi-edges. Its transaction-level laundering rate is 0.10\%, and its account-level suspicious ratio is 1.23\%, lower than HOFINET's 2.13\%. The simulator covers eight AML patterns: fan-out, fan-in, scatter-gather, gather-scatter, cycle, bipartite, stack, and random.

\subsection{AMLNet}\label{app:dataset_amlnet}

AMLNet~\cite{huda2025amlnet} is a smaller public synthetic AML benchmark with 11,000 accounts and 1,090,172 directed multi-edges. Its account-level suspicious ratio is 13.52\% (1,487 accounts), more than an order of magnitude above HOFINET and AMLworld, making it useful for high-prevalence evaluation. AMLNet shares the same 22 behavioral feature schema as AMLworld, which enables the cross-dataset transfer study in Appendix~\ref{app:transfer}.

\subsection{Transaction-to-Account Label Aggregation}\label{app:dataset_label}

All three datasets provide labels at the transaction level (HOFINET: suspicious-transaction flag; AMLworld: Is Laundering; AMLNet: isMoneyLaundering). Our node-classification task requires account-level labels; therefore, we aggregate them as follows. For transaction $e=(u,v,t)$ with label $\ell(e)\in\{0,1\}$, the account label for $a$ is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:label_agg}
    y_a \;=\; \max_{e \in \mathcal{E}_a^{\text{out}} \cup \mathcal{E}_a^{\text{in}}} \ell(e),
\end{equation}
Thus $y_a=1$ if account $a$ participates in at least one suspicious transaction as either sender or receiver, and $y_a=0$ otherwise. This max-aggregation follows standard graph-based fraud detection practice~\cite{dou2020enhancing,liu2021pick} and gives a conservative suspicious-account definition that minimizes false negatives. The resulting label distribution matches the prevalence $\rho$ in Table~\ref{tab:datasets}.

In operational AML, suspicious activity reports are filed at the transaction or case level, but analysts first identify risky accounts and then inspect their transaction sequences before making a SAR decision. The account-level risk score studied here directly supports that analyst workflow and is complementary to isolated transaction-level review.

\section{BN-Conditional Collapse: Formalization}\label{app:bn_proof}

This section gives the proof sketch for Theorem~\ref{thm:bn_collapse} in Section~\ref{subsec:rq2}.

The BYOL-style bootstrap loss in Eq.~\eqref{eq:loss} uses no negative samples; therefore, a trivial constant solution $f_\theta(\cdot)\equiv c$ can achieve $\mathcal{L}=0$ when online and target representations coincide and the cosine similarity is 1~\cite{tian2021understanding,chen2021exploring}. BatchNorm~\cite{ioffe2015batch} imposes batch-level zero-mean/unit-variance normalization, $\hat{x}=(x-\mu_B)/\sqrt{\sigma_B^2+\epsilon}$, where $\mu_B$ and $\sigma_B$ are computed from the current batch. If every batch element equals the same constant $c$, the input has zero variance and the normalization becomes numerically ill-conditioned as $\sigma_B^2\to0$. A BN-equipped encoder therefore cannot attain the trivial constant solution as a stable global minimum.

The theorem explains why both BN-equipped families in the paper, per-layer BN and final-only BN in BGRL, avoid collapse, matching the measured pattern: per-layer BN reaches about 0.27 in setting (a), BGRL about 0.31, and BN-less encoders about 0.04. On AMLNet, whose prevalence is 13.5\%, the BN-less DGI encoder also escapes the collapse region and reaches $F1_{\text{susp}}\approx0.40$ in setting (a). This is consistent with the random-classifier upper bound $2\rho/(1+\rho)$ rising to about 0.24 at higher prevalence, reducing the absolute impact of collapse.

\section{Implementation Details}\label{app:impl}

The default GNN encoder is an $L$-layer GCNConv~\cite{kipf2016semi} stack with BatchNorm, activation, and dropout at each layer:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:gnn_layer}
    \mathbf{H}^{(l)} = \text{Dropout}\left(\sigma\left(\text{BN}\left(\hat{\mathbf{A}}\mathbf{H}^{(l-1)}\mathbf{W}^{(l)}\right)\right)\right)
\end{equation}
Here $\hat{\mathbf{A}}$ is the normalized adjacency matrix, $\mathbf{W}^{(l)}$ is the learnable weight matrix at layer $l$, $\sigma$ is PReLU or ReLU, and BN denotes BatchNorm. Real transaction networks combine power-law degree distributions with extreme class imbalance (2.13\% suspicious accounts in HOFINET and 1.23\% in AMLworld). Without normalization, high-degree benign accounts dominate the gradients and dilute the suspicious-account signal. Per-layer BatchNorm normalizes the batch-level activation distribution, allowing suspicious accounts to contribute proportionally to the contrastive objective; this is the same mechanism that blocks trivial constant collapse in Theorem~\ref{thm:bn_collapse}.

At inference time, we freeze the encoder and concatenate the subgraph representations from the two views to form the final embedding, $\mathbf{z}_i=[\mathbf{s}_i^{(1)}\|\mathbf{s}_i^{(2)}]$. Appendix~\ref{app:algorithm} provides the training and inference pseudocode.

\section{Training and Inference Algorithm}\label{app:algorithm}

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{\method: Training and Inference}\label{alg:becon}
\KwIn{Transaction graph $\mathcal{G}_{\text{trans}}$, encoder features $\mathbf{X}^{\text{enc}}$, k-NN features $\mathbf{X}^{\text{knn}}$, $k$, epochs $T$, momentum $m$}
\KwOut{Node embeddings $\mathbf{Z}$}
\tcc{Preprocessing}
Build $\mathcal{G}_{\text{knn}}$ from $\mathbf{X}^{\text{knn}}$ via Eq.~\eqref{eq:l2norm}--\eqref{eq:knn_edge}\;
Initialize online encoder $f_\theta$, target encoder $f_\xi \leftarrow f_\theta$\;
\tcc{Training}
\For{$t = 1$ \KwTo $T$}{
  $\tilde{\mathcal{G}}_{\text{trans}}, \tilde{\mathcal{G}}_{\text{knn}} \leftarrow \text{Augment}(\mathcal{G}_{\text{trans}}, \mathcal{G}_{\text{knn}})$\;
  $\mathbf{H}^{(1)}, \mathbf{H}^{(2)} \leftarrow f_\theta(\mathbf{X}^{\text{enc}}, \tilde{\mathcal{E}}_{\text{trans}}), f_\theta(\mathbf{X}^{\text{enc}}, \tilde{\mathcal{E}}_{\text{knn}})$\;
  $\mathbf{S}^{(1)}, \mathbf{S}^{(2)} \leftarrow \text{SubgraphPool}(\mathbf{H}^{(1)}), \text{SubgraphPool}(\mathbf{H}^{(2)})$\;
  Compute $\mathcal{L}$ via Eq.~\eqref{eq:loss}, update $\theta$\;
  $\xi \leftarrow m\xi + (1-m)\theta$\;
}
\tcc{Inference}
$\mathbf{S}^{(1)}, \mathbf{S}^{(2)} \leftarrow$ encode and pool with frozen $f_\theta$\;
$\mathbf{Z} \leftarrow [\mathbf{S}^{(1)} \| \mathbf{S}^{(2)}]$\;
\Return $\mathbf{Z}$
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Pool Variant Comparison}\label{app:pool_variants}

Because only HOFINET provides triangle features, the cycle-aware comparison is limited to HOFINET.

Four patterns emerge. First, on the transaction view with per-layer BN encoders (GBT and GIN), HAP and Cycle recover substantial performance over mean pooling: +0.046 for GBT and +0.059 for GIN. This is the regime where the HAP signal-preservation bound is most relevant because mean pooling lies in the sign-flip region ($\eta_h>0.55$). Second, with final-only BN (BGRL), all three variants are within noise (mean 0.253, HAP 0.240, Cycle 0.243, all with std $\approx0.02$), suggesting that the final normalization already absorbs much of the heterophily noise after pooling. Overall, HAP is most useful for robustness in the mean-pool sign-flip regime, especially with per-layer normalization on the transaction view. Outside that regime, mean pooling is sufficient and remains the default design for \method.
