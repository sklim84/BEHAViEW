\begin{abstract}
In anti-money laundering (AML) transaction graphs, suspicious accounts have predominantly benign neighbors, so graph neural network (GNN) aggregation dilutes an already rare suspicious signal. We trace this to a topology-behavior mismatch: the manipulated transaction network fails to reflect homophily within the suspicious class. To address this mismatch, we present \method, a graph contrastive learning framework that performs topology repair through behavioral homophily recovery. \method builds a topology-independent recovered neighborhood graph from behavioral similarity, such as amount distributions. Contrastive learning then aligns the repaired topology with the transaction graph. On \hofinet, a retail inter-bank transfer network, the repair raises the suspicious ego-neighborhood ratio from 1:5.7 to 1:1.4. Across three AML datasets, \method outperforms supervised GNN and tabular baselines; subgraph-level pooling on the repaired topology adds further gains. At 1\% labels, \method achieves the highest suspicious-class F1 on all three datasets, with a 0.010–0.021 gap over the strongest supervised baseline. These findings position \method as a label-light building block for money laundering detection under realistic label-delay constraints.
\end{abstract}

\keywords{topology repair, behavioral homophily recovery, anti-money laundering, transaction networks, heterophily, self-supervised learning, graph contrastive learning}

Money laundering, accounting for an estimated 2--5\% of global GDP~\cite{unodc_ml}, has become more sophisticated through tactics such as structuring, layering, and mule networks. GNN-based methods have thus been explored to learn structural patterns in transaction networks beyond rule-based detection~\cite{cheng2025graph}. In AML, however, neighborhood aggregation is only useful when a node's neighbors carry class-relevant evidence. For the suspicious class, this evidence is often weak because suspicious accounts intentionally mix with benign counterparties. We focus on three recurring challenges in AML transaction networks: suspicious-class neighborhood heterophily~\cite{zhu2020beyond}, severe class imbalance, and delayed or limited labels~\cite{fatf2013guidance}. To address these jointly, we adopt a topology-repair perspective on the underlying topology--behavior mismatch.

\textbf{Challenge 1: weak suspicious-class neighborhood signal.}

Money-laundering accounts deliberately mix with benign accounts, forming mule networks. In \hofinet's transaction graph, about 85\% of suspicious-incident edges connect to benign accounts (S-S:S-B $=$ 1:5.7), and many suspicious accounts lie even deeper in benign neighborhoods (Figure~\ref{fig:intro_topology_repair}). Message passing thus struggles to accumulate suspicious evidence. Standard graph contrastive learning (GCL)~\cite{you2020graph,velivckovic2018deep} also fails: common graph augmentations perturb the transaction graph but preserve account linkages, so suspicious accounts stay embedded among benign neighbors in both contrastive branches~\cite{zhu2021graph}.

\textbf{Challenges 2, 3: class imbalance and delayed labels.} Suspicious accounts typically represent under 2\% of all accounts~\cite{hilal2022financial}. Labels are only available after retrospective investigations that can take over six months~\cite{fatf2013guidance,kofia2024aml}. Combined with weak neighborhood signals for the suspicious class, these constraints cause cold-start problems for supervised fraud models in production.
We propose \method, a graph contrastive learning framework that repairs adversarial topologies by restoring behavioral homophily. We study: (i) when restoring behavioral homophily corrects the suspicious-class topology and (ii) how to aggregate on the repaired topology without diluting suspicious signals. These form a causal chain: homophily recovery determines whether class-relevant neighborhoods exist in the recovered topology (Theorems~\ref{thm:knn_homophily}--\ref{thm:combined}); within such neighborhoods, a sign-flip threshold decides whether pooling preserves or erases suspicious signals (Theorem~\ref{thm:meanpool}). Both effects require normalization to prevent collapse under the BYOL-style objective (Theorem~\ref{thm:bn_collapse}).

Our approach differs from prior k-NN auxiliary-graph methods (e.g., MLGCL) in two ways. First, earlier work uses the feature-space affinity graph to complement the original topology and assumes the input graph encodes class structure—an assumption violated under adversarial manipulation. Second, we use the affinity graph as a replacement: the recovered neighborhood graph substitutes for corrupted suspicious-class neighborhoods before contrastive learning. This yields two predictions: only behavioral features can repair the topology, since connectivity features inherit adversarial bias; and aggregation should use the repaired, not the original, transaction graph. We validate these in Section~\ref{sec:experiments} and support them theoretically in Appendix~\ref{app:theory}.

On \hofinet, the recovered neighborhood graph improves the S-S:S-B ratio from 1:5.7 to 1:1.4, with similar gains on AMLworld~\cite{amlpatterns2024realistic} and AMLNet. Our contributions are:

\section{Related Work}\label{sec:related_work}
We survey three research areas related to \method: heterophily-aware GNNs, graph contrastive learning with topology-repaired graphs, and fraud or anomaly detection with limited labels.

\subsection{Heterophily-aware GNNs}
Prior work improves message passing under low label homophily by separating ego and neighbor embeddings, modifying propagation, or decoupling structure from attributes (e.g., H2GCN~\cite{zhu2020beyond}, Geom-GCN~\cite{pei2020geom}, LINKX~\cite{zhu2021linkx}). Yet these models still depend on the observed transaction topology; in AML, suspicious accounts intentionally mix with benign counterparties, so suspicious--suspicious edges are rare. \method instead repairs the topology, complementing heterophily-aware propagation by building a class-relevant neighborhood from a topology-independent behavioral homophily recovery graph.

\subsection{GCL with Topology-Repair Graphs}
Self-supervised GCL enforces consistency across multiple graph branches~\cite{velivckovic2018deep,you2020graph}. Most methods perturb one branch at the representation level, keeping neighborhoods fixed. Auxiliary-graph methods add a separate graph, such as a k-NN affinity graph~\cite{shang2023mlgcl}, but when built from all features, this graph can mix behavioral similarity with topology-correlated variables. \method instead builds a topology-independent recovered neighborhood graph from behavioral features alone, uses it to repair topology, and studies when aggregation on the repaired topology preserves or dilutes suspicious evidence. Unlike prior k-NN auxiliary graphs that mainly enrich an existing topology, we use it for topology repair: when the observed graph is adversarially manipulated to be heterophilous (e.g., mule networks), the recovered graph replaces broken neighborhoods so downstream learning relies on class-relevant structure.

\subsection{Money Laundering and Fraud Detection}
In AML and fraud detection, supervised GNNs can mitigate class imbalance via neighborhood selection and reweighting~\cite{dou2020enhancing}, but require timely, abundant labels that AML rarely provides. Unsupervised anomaly detection avoids labels~\cite{ding2019dominant}, yet is mostly tested on generic graph benchmarks and does not address suspicious-class neighborhood mismatch in transaction networks. \method overcomes this by combining topology-independent behavioral homophily recovery with self-supervised learning and by analyzing aggregation-level signal preservation on the repaired topology.
\section{Method: \method}
\label{sec:method}

\method comprises a behavioral homophily recovery graph, a topology-repair contrastive encoder with subgraph pooling, and BYOL-style training.

\subsection{Problem Formulation}
We model the transaction network as a directed graph $\mathcal{G}_{\text{trans}} = (\mathcal{V}, \mathcal{E}, \mathbf{X})$, where $\mathcal{V}$ is the set of accounts (nodes, $|\mathcal{V}|=N$), $\mathcal{E} \subseteq \mathcal{V} \times \mathcal{V}$ is the set of transfer transactions (edges), and $\mathbf{X} \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times F}$ is the node feature matrix. Each node $v_i$ has a suspicion label $y_i \in \{0, 1\}$, but we use a self-supervised setting that ignores labels during training. Our goal is to learn node representations $\mathbf{Z} \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times D}$ that identify suspicious accounts based purely on the graph structure and node features.

\subsection{Feature Taxonomy}
\label{subsec:feature_taxonomy}
We group node features into behavioral, network-derived, and structural categories (Table~\ref{tab:features}). Behavioral features describe transaction patterns via amount statistics, time-window frequencies, and transaction-type entropies. Network-derived features (transaction counts and degrees) are fed to the encoder but excluded from recovered-neighborhood construction, while structural features (centrality, hub/authority, topology) are used only for analysis and auxiliary comparison. We denote by $\mathbf{X}^{\text{enc}}$ the behavioral and network-derived features used by the GNN encoder, and by $\mathbf{X}^{\text{bhv}}$ the 20 behavioral features used to build a behavioral homophily recovery graph. Because suspicious accounts often transact with benign ones to evade detection, they show low neighborhood homophily in the observed topology. We address this by recovering topology-independent behavioral homophily, linking behaviorally similar accounts even when they are not directly connected.

\subsection{Recovered Neighborhood Graph}
\label{subsec:knn_view}
\method repairs topology by constructing a concrete, topology-independent recovered neighborhood graph $\mathcal{G}_{\text{bhv}}$ that replaces heterophilous suspicious-class neighborhoods in the transaction graph. Unlike standard augmentation-based GCL, which perturbs the original graph by dropping edges or masking features and thus preserves original neighborhoods, we build $\mathcal{G}_{\text{bhv}}$ solely from behavioral features, independent of the edge structure of $\mathcal{G}_{\text{trans}}$ (Figure~\ref{fig:framework}), preventing any connectivity-derived information from affecting the repair.

\textbf{Recovered-neighborhood construction.} We standardize the behavioral features $\mathbf{x}_i^{\text{bhv}} \in \mathbb{R}^{20}$ to zero mean and unit variance to remove scale differences, then apply L2 normalization so that similarity depends only on the feature vector direction:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:l2norm}
    \bar{\mathbf{x}}_i = \text{StandardScaler}(\mathbf{x}_i^{\text{bhv}}), \quad
    \tilde{\mathbf{x}}_i = \frac{\bar{\mathbf{x}}_i}{\|\bar{\mathbf{x}}_i\|_2}
\end{equation}

Because the Euclidean distance between two L2-normalized vectors is monotonic in their cosine similarity, we retrieve the $k$ nearest neighbors of each node $v_i$ using a ball-tree search over Euclidean distance. Connecting each node to these neighbors yields the recovered neighborhood graph $\mathcal{G}_{\text{bhv}} = (\mathcal{V}, \mathcal{E}_{\text{bhv}})$:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:knn_edge}
    \mathcal{E}_{\text{bhv}} = \{(v_i, v_j) \mid v_j \in \operatorname{kNN}(v_i, k)\}.
\end{equation}

We use the symmetrized (bidirectional) version of this recovered neighborhood graph for message passing and pooling, adding $(v_j, v_i)$ whenever $(v_i, v_j) \in \mathcal{E}_{\text{bhv}}$.

Here, $\operatorname{kNN}(v_i, k)$ is the set of $k$ nodes closest in Euclidean distance to $\tilde{\mathbf{x}}_i$, with $k=10$ by default.

Money-laundering accounts that split transactions and layer transfers tend to share similar amount distributions, time-window frequencies, and transaction-type entropies. The recovered neighborhood graph therefore links suspicious accounts not directly connected by transactions, restoring suspicious-to-suspicious neighborhoods missing from the transaction topology.

\subsection{Topology-Repair GNN Encoder}
\label{subsec:dual_view}
\textbf{Topology-repair contrastive branches.} Contrastive learning aligns the observed transaction topology, which captures transfer structure, with the recovered neighborhood topology, which captures behavioral homophily of the same account. \method builds these two branches from $\mathcal{G}_{\text{trans}}$ and $\mathcal{G}_{\text{bhv}}$.

\textbf{Observed and repaired topologies.} Using only the transaction topology mixes suspicious and benign accounts, while using only the recovered neighborhood graph discards transaction-structure information. \method jointly learns account representations from both branches so embeddings capture both transaction structure and behavioral homophily.

\textbf{GNN encoder.} A shared online encoder $f_\theta$ produces embeddings $\mathbf{H}^{(1)} = f_\theta(\mathbf{X}^{\text{enc}}, \mathcal{E}_{\text{trans}})$ and $\mathbf{H}^{(2)} = f_\theta(\mathbf{X}^{\text{enc}}, \mathcal{E}_{\text{bhv}})$. The target encoder $f_\xi$ is an EMA copy that provides stop-gradient representations for the opposite BYOL branch. The online encoder stacks GCNConv~\cite{kipf2016semi} layers with batch normalization, nonlinearity, and dropout. \method is encoder-agnostic and supports nine encoders, including GIN~\cite{xu2018powerful}, MVGRL, and GBT; Section~\ref{subsec:rq2} studies the impact of batch normalization.

\subsection{Subgraph Pooling}
\label{subsec:subgraph_pool}
\textbf{Subgraph-level signal aggregation.} \method compares both individual node representations and a subgraph representation that pools each account with its neighbors (Subgraph Pooling in Figure~\ref{fig:framework}). This captures the idea that an account’s suspiciousness is often clearer from its relationships with neighbors than from its own attributes alone.

\textbf{Subgraph representation.} For each topology branch $t \in \{1,2\}$, we define the subgraph representation of node $v_i$ as the average of its own representation and those of its neighbors in that branch:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:subgraph_pool}
    \mathbf{s}_i^{(t)} = \frac{1}{|\mathcal{N}^{(t)}(v_i)| + 1}
    \left(\mathbf{h}_i^{(t)} + \sum_{v_j \in \mathcal{N}^{(t)}(v_i)} \mathbf{h}_j^{(t)}\right)
\end{equation}

Here $\mathcal{N}^{(1)}(v_i)$ is the neighborhood of $v_i$ in the transaction topology, and $\mathcal{N}^{(2)}(v_i)$ is its recovered neighborhood. For both topology branches, we perform message passing and pooling on a bidirected graph, so $\mathcal{N}^{(t)}(v_i)$ is the union of in- and out-neighbors. The mean pooling in Eq.~\eqref{eq:subgraph_pool} is the default in \method.

\textbf{Repair-aware pooling effect.} Subgraph pooling is applied separately to each topology branch: transaction-graph neighbors for the observed topology and behaviorally similar neighbors for the repaired topology. Because pooling aggregates neighbor information, the chosen topology shapes the result. The repaired neighborhood brings behaviorally similar suspicious accounts closer, so pooling on it tends to preserve or amplify suspicious signals. In the transaction graph, suspicious accounts often connect to benign ones (high S-B ratio), so pooling can dilute suspicious signals with benign information.

\textbf{Pooling variants.} We use mean pooling by default, but other options are possible, including similarity-weighted pooling (HAP). We analyze its expected signal-preservation behavior in Appendix~\ref{app:theory} (Proposition~\ref{thm:hap}).

\KwIn{$\mathcal{G}_{\text{trans}}$, $\mathbf{X}^{\text{bhv}}$, $\mathbf{X}^{\text{enc}}$, neighbors $k$, epochs $E$, momentum $m$, learning rate $\eta$}
\KwOut{Node embeddings $\{\mathbf{z}_i\}_{i=1}^{N}$}
\BlankLine
\tcc{Stage 1: recovered neighborhood graph (one-shot)}
$\tilde{\mathbf{X}}^{\text{bhv}} \gets \mathrm{L2norm}(\mathrm{StandardScaler}(\mathbf{X}^{\text{bhv}}))$\;
$\mathcal{G}_{\text{bhv}} \gets$ symmetrize($\mathrm{kNN}_k(\tilde{\mathbf{X}}^{\text{bhv}})$) \tcp*[r]{Eqs.~\eqref{eq:l2norm},~\eqref{eq:knn_edge}}
\BlankLine

\For{$\text{epoch}=1$ \KwTo $E$}{
  \ForEach{$t \in \{1,2\}$, $\mathcal{G}^{(1)}{=}\mathcal{G}_{\text{trans}}, \mathcal{G}^{(2)}{=}\mathcal{G}_{\text{bhv}}$}{
    $\mathbf{h}^{(t)} \gets f_\theta(\mathbf{X}^{\text{enc}}, \mathcal{G}^{(t)})$;\quad $\mathbf{h}_\xi^{(t)} \gets f_\xi(\mathbf{X}^{\text{enc}}, \mathcal{G}^{(t)})$\;

  }
  Compute $\mathcal{L}$ via Eq.~\eqref{eq:loss} (stop-grad on target branch)\;

  $\xi \gets m \xi + (1-m) \theta$\;
}
\BlankLine

$\mathbf{z}_i \gets [\mathbf{s}_i^{(1)} \,\Vert\, \mathbf{s}_i^{(2)}]$ \tcp*[r]{Eq.~\eqref{eq:concat}; node-level: $[\mathbf{h}_i^{(1)} \Vert \mathbf{h}_i^{(2)}]$}

\subsection{Training and Inference}
\label{subsec:contrastive_learning}

Algorithm~\ref{alg:behaview} summarizes \method's three stages: recovered-neighborhood construction (Section~\ref{subsec:knn_view}), self-supervised training under a symmetric BYOL-style loss, and inference via per-branch concatenation.

The Stage~2 loss maximizes cosine similarity between the two topology branches:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:loss}
    \mathcal{L} = \sum_{t \in \{1,2\}} \left(2 - 2 \cdot \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N} \frac{q(g(\mathbf{r}_i^{(t)})) \cdot g(\mathbf{r}_{\xi,i}^{(3-t)})}{\|q(g(\mathbf{r}_i^{(t)}))\| \cdot \|g(\mathbf{r}_{\xi,i}^{(3-t)})\|}\right)
\end{equation}
where $g$ is the projector shared between online and target paths (stop-gradient on the target), $q$ is the predictor on the online path, $\mathbf{r}_i^{(t)}{=}\mathbf{s}_i^{(t)}$ ($\mathbf{h}_i^{(t)}$ in the node-level ablation), and $\mathbf{r}_{\xi,i}^{(t)}$ is the target-encoder representation. We use momentum $m{=}0.99$ for the EMA target update.

At inference, embeddings are
\begin{equation}\label{eq:concat}
    \mathbf{z}_i = [\mathbf{s}_i^{(1)} \| \mathbf{s}_i^{(2)}],
\end{equation}
with $\mathbf{z}_i = [\mathbf{h}_i^{(1)} \| \mathbf{h}_i^{(2)}]$ for the node-level ablation. Encoder architectures and remaining hyperparameters are at the anonymized link.

\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}
We evaluate \method in four phases, from mechanism to deployment. First, we test whether the recovered neighborhood graph compensates for missing suspicious-to-suspicious links, restores suspicious-class homophily, and improves downstream performance. Second, we examine when aggregation on the repaired topology (subgraph pooling) helps or hurts, and how normalization affects training stability. Third, we compare the self-supervised representations to supervised baselines under label delay and cold start, reflecting realistic AML conditions. Fourth, we test whether the mechanism and empirical patterns transfer to AML datasets with different prevalence and scale. Together, these experiments answer our research questions.

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{RQ1. Behavioral homophily recovery:} Does the topology-independent recovered neighborhood graph repair the suspicious-class topology relative to the transaction graph?
\item \textbf{RQ2. Signal preservation under aggregation:} When does subgraph pooling help versus hurt, and how does recovered homophily change this trade-off?
\item \textbf{RQ3. Self-supervised AML detection under label scarcity:} Does the self-supervised representation from \method remain competitive with supervised models under label delay and cold start?
\item \textbf{RQ4. Robustness across AML regimes:} Do the same mechanisms and trends apply to AML datasets with different prevalence and scale?
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Experimental Setting}
All experiments use Python 3.12, PyTorch 2.10, CUDA 12.8, and NVIDIA H100 80GB GPUs.

\subsubsection{Datasets}We use three AML datasets (Table~\ref{tab:datasets}). \hofinet contains ~4.7M synthetic transactions from a national interbank settlement network over 40 months of household and corporate transfers. AMLworld~\cite{amlpatterns2024realistic}, the NeurIPS 2023 open synthetic benchmark, models eight transaction patterns (e.g., fan-out, scatter-gather, cycle); we use the HI-Small variant (high illicit ratio, small size). AMLNet~\cite{huda2025amlnet} is a small synthetic benchmark for high-prevalence settings that shares AMLworld’s feature schema. All three datasets include transaction-level suspicious labels. Following standard graph-based AML and fraud detection practice~\cite{dou2020enhancing,liu2021pick}, we max-aggregate to the account level: an account is suspicious (label$=1$) if it appears on either side of any suspicious transaction and normal (label$=0$) otherwise. The resulting suspicious-account ratios are 2.13\% (\hofinet), 1.23\% (AMLworld), and 13.52\% (AMLNet) (Table~\ref{tab:datasets}).

\subsubsection{Models} We evaluate \method under four ablation settings that vary both topology and contrastive granularity: (a) transaction graph with node-level contrast, (b) recovered neighborhood graph with node-level contrast, (c) transaction graph with subgraph pooling, and (d) repaired topology with subgraph pooling. We compare nine self-supervised encoders: five with BatchNorm at every layer (GBT, DGI+BN, MVGRL+BN, GRACE+BN, GIN), one with BatchNorm only on the final layer (BGRL), and three without BatchNorm (DGI, MVGRL, GRACE). All models share the same BYOL-style bootstrap objective (Eq.~\eqref{eq:loss}) and hyperparameters (learning rate 5e-4, hidden size 256, $L$=2 layers, dropout 0.2, 200 training epochs). 

For the label-efficiency study (Section~\ref{subsec:rq3}), we additionally include six supervised baselines: tabular models (MLP, XGBoost~\cite{xgboost2016}, LightGBM~\cite{lightgbm2017}), generic GNNs (GCN~\cite{kipf2016semi}, GAT~\cite{velivckovic2017graph}), and a fraud-oriented GNN (CARE-GNN~\cite{dou2020enhancing}). The MLP follows the standard fraud-GNN configuration (stacked Linear+ReLU layers without BatchNorm). All supervised baselines apply class-weighted loss and receive the same encoder input features $\mathbf{X}^{\text{enc}}$ (20 behavioral plus 4 network-derived attributes; Table~\ref{tab:features}). Only the topology-repair component consumes behavioral features $\mathbf{X}^{\text{bhv}}$ (20) and omits any network-derived variables.

\subsubsection{Evaluation metrics} We evaluate self-supervised models by freezing the encoder and training Logistic Regression~\cite{pedregosa2011scikit} on embeddings $\mathbf{z}_i = [\mathbf{s}_i^{(1)} \| \mathbf{s}_i^{(2)}]$. The main metric is suspicious-class F1 ($F1_{\text{susp}}$); AUROC and AUPRC are secondary. We use a 10\%/10\%/80\% train/validation/test split, fixed per seed for paired comparisons. We report mean$\pm$std over four seeds and assess significance with a 4-seed paired $t$-test and Holm--Bonferroni correction. Seed-level results and reproduction scripts are available at an anonymized link.

\begin{theorem}[Normalization-conditional stability under bootstrap objectives]\label{thm:bn_collapse}
Under the BYOL-style bootstrap loss~\eqref{eq:loss}, the trivial constant solution $f_\theta(\cdot) \equiv c$ attains $\mathcal{L}=0$ when the encoder lacks batch normalization (BN). In contrast, the presence of at least one BN layer rules out this trivial constant mapping under the definition of batch normalization, acting as a sufficient condition to avoid the constant-solution pathology.
\end{theorem}

\textbf{Empirical check for the stability analysis.} In setting (a), BN-less encoders reach $F1_{\text{susp}} \approx 0.04$, matching the \hofinet random-classifier upper bound $2\rho/(1+\rho) \approx 0.042$ (the maximum $F1_{\text{susp}}$ at positive rate $\rho$), indicating that unstable training can erase suspicious-class signal at low prevalence. When $\rho \leq 2\%$, this bound is already $\approx 0.04$, so instability leaves little usable suspicious-class evidence. On AMLNet ($\rho = 13.52\%$), BN-less DGI still achieves $F1_{\text{susp}} \approx 0.40$ (Section~\ref{subsec:rq4}), so the absolute impact is smaller. The BN-conditional pattern is not specific to the BYOL bootstrap loss: bare DGI, MVGRL, and GRACE under their native JSD and InfoNCE losses also collapse to $F1_{\text{susp}} \approx 0.07$, showing that the mechanism in Theorem~\ref{thm:bn_collapse} is BN-conditional rather than loss-specific. Overall, the results suggest a single mechanism: homophily recovery yields class-relevant neighborhoods, sign-flip predicts when aggregation dilutes them, and normalization determines whether this mechanism is realized.

\subsection{Label-Efficiency (RQ3)}

The MLP uses a standard stacked Linear+ReLU architecture without BatchNorm, as in prior fraud-GNN work. All models share the same encoder inputs $\mathbf{X}^{\text{enc}}$ (20 behavioral + 4 network-derived features; Table~\ref{tab:features}). \method uses setting (d) with the strongest encoder per dataset (GBT on \hofinet and AMLNet, BGRL on AMLworld).

\textbf{Tabular boosting catches up at 10\% labels.} As label fraction increases, XGBoost and LightGBM monotonically approach \method. This matches the BN-free self-supervised behavior in Theorem~\ref{thm:bn_collapse}: avoiding collapse—via BatchNorm or sufficient prevalence—is crucial in low-prevalence regimes for both supervised and self-supervised training. \method's encoder uses per-layer BatchNorm and avoids collapse on all three datasets.

\textbf{Graph baselines remain consistently weaker than \method.} GCN, GAT, and CARE-GNN trail \method by $0.13$–$0.57$ $F1_{\text{susp}}$ at 10\% labels on \hofinet, by $0.02$ on AMLworld, and by $0.16$–$0.24$ on AMLNet. These models aggregate on the original anti-homophilous transaction graph, whereas \method first repairs this topology before contrastive training. The consistent gap across label fractions shows that topology repair, not message-passing capacity, is the main bottleneck for graph-based AML detection.

\textbf{Operational utility with a limited analyst budget.} \method with GBT attains \hofinet precision@$k$ of 0.875, 0.853, 0.834, 0.791, and 0.746 for $k \in \{50, 100, 200, 400, 800\}$ (4 seeds), detecting approximately 317 true suspicious accounts within a top-400 budget.

\section{Conclusion and Future Work}
We propose \method, a topology-repair method for AML detection on transaction networks with weak homophily. \method defines its contrastive objective on a behavioral homophily recovery graph that reconstructs neighborhoods of suspicious entities, while test-time prediction still uses the original transaction graph (Eq.~\ref{eq:concat}), preserving fidelity for fund-flow analysis. This repair step raises \hofinet edge homophily from 0.690 to 0.981. With only 1\% labeled nodes, \method achieves the best $F1_{\text{susp}}$ on all three datasets (e.g., 0.654 on \hofinet), surpassing the strongest supervised baseline by 0.010--0.021. With 10\% labels, tabular boosting matches \method on AMLworld and AMLNet, but \method remains ahead on \hofinet and beats all supervised graph models.

These results are consistent with a mechanism in which topology repair yields class-informative neighborhoods; sign-flip analysis pinpoints when aggregation erodes this structure; subgraph pooling on the repaired graph amplifies it; and normalization mainly stabilizes the bootstrap objective. The stability theorem (Theorem~\ref{thm:bn_collapse}) and the homophily-recovery and aggregation-signal theorems (Theorems~\ref{thm:knn_homophily}--\ref{thm:meanpool}), stated in Appendix~\ref{app:theory}, support these claims.

\method targets AML settings with delayed labels, low suspicious-activity prevalence, and anti-homophilous neighborhoods. In high-prevalence, label-rich scenarios, stronger supervised methods are available; \method is intended for the delayed, low-prevalence regime common in operational AML.

\textbf{Detection vs.\ investigation.} \method is tuned for detection accuracy: the recovered neighborhood graph underlies the improvements in Table~\ref{tab:rq1}. The transaction graph remains the basis for inference but is not optimized for investigative tasks. STR filing, mule-network reconstruction, and fund-flow tracing must still rely on the original transaction topology; the recovered graph is not a post-detection replacement. We view \method as a first-stage behavioral filter in a two-stage pipeline, followed by transaction-graph-based investigation.

\textbf{Reproducibility.} We will release source code, training scripts, and seed-level raw outputs. \hofinet cannot be shared due to its data-sharing agreement, but AMLworld and AMLNet experiments are fully reproducible from public benchmarks.

\section{Theory: Proofs and Empirical Checks}
\label{app:theory}
\subsection{Setup and Notation}
\label{app:theory_notation}

Let $V=V_S\cup V_B$ with $N:=|V|$, prevalence $\rho:=N_S/N$, and $N_{\min}:=\min(N_S,N_B)$. For a graph $G=(V,E)$,
\[
h(G):=\frac{|\{(u,v)\in E: y_u=y_v\}|}{|E|}.
\]
Assume class-conditional features $x\mid y=c\sim P_c$ satisfy
\begin{equation}\label{eq:sep}
\Pr\big[\|x-X'\|<\|x-X''\|\big]\ge 1-\epsilon,
\end{equation}
for independent $X'\sim P_y$ and $X''\sim P_{y'}$ with $y'\neq y$. Let $G_{\text{knn},k}$ be the Euclidean $k$-NN graph.

\subsection{Recovered Homophily Analysis}
\label{app:theory_view}

\begin{theorem}[Probabilistic homophily recovery]
\label{thm:knn_homophily}
If $k \leq (1-\epsilon)(N_{\min}-1) - 1$, then the k-NN graph $G_{\text{knn},k}$ built from behavioral features satisfies
\[
    h(G_{\text{knn}, k}) \;\geq\; 1 - \tfrac{N - 1}{k} \exp\!\left(-\tfrac{((N_{\min}-1)(1-\epsilon) - k)^2}{2(N_{\min}-1)(1-\epsilon)}\right).
\]
\end{theorem}
\noindent\textbf{Remark.} The condition $k \leq (1-\epsilon)(N_{\min}-1) - 1$ only requires $k$ to be well below the minority-class size (here $N_{\min}\in[1{,}487,9{,}644]$ and $k=10$).

\noindent\textbf{Proof sketch.} A benign node enters the top-$k$ list of a suspicious node $u$ only if $Z_j := |\{i : \|x_u-x_{S_i}\| < \|x_u-x_{B_j}\|\}| < k$. By Assumption~\eqref{eq:sep}, $Z_j \succeq \text{Binom}(N_S{-}1,1{-}\epsilon)$; Chernoff + union bound yields Theorem~\ref{thm:knn_homophily} (and $h(G_{\text{knn},k})=1$ when $\epsilon=0$).

For the transaction graph $G_{\text{tx}}$, we use a DCSBM~\cite{karrer2011stochastic}:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:dcsbm}
    \Pr[(u, v) \in E_{\text{tx}}] \propto \theta_u\theta_v\,\omega_{y_u,y_v}.
\end{equation}
With $\Theta_c := \sum_{v:y_v=c}\theta_v$,
\[
    h(G_{\text{tx}})=\frac{\Theta_S^2\omega_{SS}+\Theta_B^2\omega_{BB}}{\Theta_S^2\omega_{SS}+2\Theta_S\Theta_B\omega_{SB}+\Theta_B^2\omega_{BB}}.
\]
The node-averaged ego-heterophily prediction is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:eta_dcsbm}
\eta_h^{(\text{dcsbm})}=\rho\,\frac{\Theta_B\omega_{SB}}{\Theta_S\omega_{SS}+\Theta_B\omega_{SB}}+(1-\rho)\,\frac{\Theta_S\omega_{SB}}{\Theta_B\omega_{BB}+\Theta_S\omega_{SB}}.
\end{equation}

\begin{theorem}[Combined homophily gap]
\label{thm:combined}
Under the DCSBM, if $h(G_{\text{tx}}) = \alpha$, then
\[
h(G_{\text{knn}, k}) - h(G_{\text{tx}}) \geq 1 - \alpha - \delta(N, N_{\min}, k, \epsilon),
\]
where $\delta$ is the exponential term in Theorem~\ref{thm:knn_homophily}.
\end{theorem}

Combining Theorem~\ref{thm:knn_homophily} with $h(G_{\text{tx}})=\alpha$ gives
\begin{equation}\label{eq:gap}
    h(G_{\text{knn}, k}) - h(G_{\text{tx}}) \geq 1 - \alpha - \tfrac{N - 1}{k} \exp\!\left(-\tfrac{((N_{\min}-1)(1-\epsilon) - k)^2}{2(N_{\min}-1)(1-\epsilon)}\right).
\end{equation}
On \hofinet ($N=452{,}816$, $N_{\min}=9{,}644$, $k=10$, $\alpha=0.690$, $\epsilon=0.05$), the bound is $\approx 0.310$ and the measured gap is $0.291$.

\subsection{Aggregation-level Signal Preservation}
Write $z_v=s_v\mu+\eta_v$ with bounded noise $\|\eta_v\|\le \sigma\|\mu\|$ and define ego-heterophily $\eta_h := |\{u\in\mathcal{N}(v): y_u\neq y_v\}|/|\mathcal{N}(v)|$. We study the post-pooling projection on $\mu$,
$\varrho := \langle z_v^{(\cdot)}, \mu \rangle / \|\mu\|^2$,
for mean pooling and heterophily-aware pooling (HAP) with cosine-similarity weights, and set $s_v=+1$ WLOG.

\begin{theorem}[Mean pool signal flip]
\label{thm:meanpool}
With $s_v=+1$, $\mathbb{E}[\varrho^{(\text{mean})}] = (1 + k(1 - 2\eta_h)) / (k+1)$. The mean-pooled signal flips sign (i.e., $\mathbb{E}[\varrho] < 0$) when $\eta_h > 1/2 + 1/(2k)$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proposition}[HAP signal preservation under small noise]
\label{thm:hap}
Assume $\sigma < 1/\sqrt{2}$ and cosine similarities have the correct sign. Then, for any $\eta_h$,
\[
\mathbb{E}[\varrho^{(\text{hap})}] \geq 1 - 2\sigma^2 - 4\sigma^2 \eta_h.
\]
\end{proposition}

\paragraph{Predicted 4-setting ablation pattern.}\label{cor:ablation}
For an encoder with BN and $\eta_h^{\text{tx}} > 1/2 + 1/(2k)$, the predicted $F1_{\text{susp}}$ ranking is $F1(b) \approx F1(d) > F1(a) > F1(c)$. On \hofinet, all six BN-equipped encoders follow this order ($\Delta(b{-}a) \in [+0.255, +0.405]$, $\Delta(c{-}a) \in [-0.070, -0.052]$).
