In anti-money laundering (AML) transaction graphs, suspicious accounts have predominantly benign neighbors. This heterophilous neighborhood structure causes graph neural network (GNN) aggregation to dilute an already rare suspicious signal. We trace this to a mismatch between topology and behavior: the manipulated transaction network fails to reflect homophily within the suspicious class. To address this mismatch, we present \method, a graph contrastive learning framework that performs topology repair through behavioral homophily recovery. \method builds a behavioral, topology-independent view that connects accounts by similarities in their transaction patterns, such as amount distributions. By design, the view uses no features derived from graph connectivity. Our account transfer network, called \hofinet, comprises retail inter-bank account transfers via mobile or internet banking. On \hofinet, the repair raises the suspicious ego-neighborhood ratio from 1:5.7 to 1:1.4. \method outperforms standard transaction-view baselines across three AML datasets, with subgraph-level pooling on the recovered view adding further gains. At 1\% labels, \method achieves the highest $F1_{\text{susp}}$ on all three datasets, exceeding the strongest supervised baseline by 0.010--0.021. These findings position \method as a label-light building block for money laundering detection under realistic label-delay constraints.
\end{abstract}

\keywords{topology repair, behavioral homophily recovery, anti-money laundering, transaction networks, low-homophily, self-supervised learning, graph contrastive learning}

Money laundering, accounting for an estimated 2--5\% of global GDP~\cite{unodc_ml}, has become more sophisticated through tactics such as structuring, layering, and mule networks (layered chains that route funds through third-party or otherwise normal accounts). GNN-based methods have thus been explored to learn structural patterns in transaction networks beyond rule-based detection~\cite{cheng2025graph}. In AML, however, neighborhood aggregation is only useful when a node's neighbors carry class-relevant evidence; for the suspicious class, this evidence is often weak because suspicious accounts intentionally mix with benign counterparties. Independently, AML data are also highly imbalanced and labeled with substantial delays. Accordingly, we focus on three recurring challenges in AML transaction networks: suspicious-class neighborhood heterophily~\cite{zhu2020beyond}, severe class imbalance, and delayed or limited labels~\cite{fatf2013guidance}. We study how a topology-repair perspective can mitigate the underlying topology--behavior mismatch.


\textbf{Challenge 1: weak suspicious-class neighborhood signal.} Money-laundering accounts intentionally interact with benign accounts to evade detection, forming mule networks. In a synthetic transaction graph based on \hofinet, about 85\% of suspicious-incident edges connect to benign accounts (S-S:S-B $=$ 1:5.7), and many individual suspicious accounts are even more deeply embedded in benign neighborhoods (Figure~\ref{fig:intro_topology_repair} shows one such account with 25 of its 26 1-hop counterparts benign). As a result, even expressive message passing can struggle to accumulate suspicious evidence from local neighborhoods because suspicious--suspicious links are scarce, despite high global homophily dominated by benign--benign edges.

Standard graph contrastive learning (GCL) cannot resolve this mismatch either: typical view-augmentation strategies perturb the original transaction graph, leaving the underlying neighborhood structure unchanged~\cite{zhu2021graph}. Because view perturbations do not alter which accounts are linked by transactions, suspicious accounts remain embedded among many benign neighbors across both views.

\textbf{Challenges 2--3: class imbalance and delayed or limited labels.} Suspicious accounts often constitute less than 2\% of all accounts~\cite{hilal2022financial}. Moreover, suspicious labels are confirmed only after retrospective investigations that can take more than six months~\cite{fatf2013guidance,kofia2024aml}; therefore, at training time, reliable suspicious labels are often limited. Supervised fraud models can therefore face cold-start issues in production, especially when both label availability and neighborhood suspicious signals are weak.

Graph contrastive learning (GCL) is a natural label-free starting point because it enforces consistency across augmented views~\cite{you2020graph,velivckovic2018deep}. The challenge above motivates moving beyond standard GCL augmentations toward an auxiliary view that no longer inherits the transaction topology. We identify the main challenge as a topology--behavior mismatch: in AML transaction networks, the observed graph structure is strategically manipulated (via mule chains and mixing with legitimate parties), making the transaction graph a weak surrogate for homophily within the suspicious class. Accordingly, we cast the problem as topology repair: we replace the heterophilous transaction neighborhoods of suspicious accounts with a recovered neighborhood graph built from behavioral similarity, without leaking connectivity-derived variables into the construction.

We study \method along two linked questions: (i) when can behavioral homophily recovery repair the suspicious-class topology, and (ii) how should aggregation operate on the repaired topology without diluting the suspicious signal. These analyses form a single causal chain: homophily recovery first determines whether class-relevant neighborhoods exist in the contrastive view (Theorems~\ref{thm:knn_homophily}--\ref{thm:combined}); on those neighborhoods, a sign-flip threshold governs whether pooling preserves or destroys the suspicious signal (Theorem~\ref{thm:meanpool}); and both mechanisms are observable only when normalization keeps the BYOL-style objective from collapsing (Theorem~\ref{thm:bn_collapse}). We test the chain by varying view and pooling in the four-setting ablation, and by varying encoder families in the normalization ablation.

Our framing departs from prior k-NN auxiliary views (e.g., MLGCL) in two ways. First, prior designs treat a feature-space affinity graph as a complement to the existing topology, implicitly assuming the input graph carries useful (if noisy) class structure. This assumption is violated when the topology is adversarially manipulated through mule networks. Second, we frame the affinity graph as a replacement: the recovered neighborhood graph replaces broken suspicious-class neighborhoods before contrastive learning. Two testable consequences follow: (i) only behavioral features can repair the topology, since connectivity-derived ones inherit the adversarial structure; and (ii) aggregation on the repaired topology, not the original transaction graph, is the valid signal-extraction operation. We validate both in Section~\ref{sec:experiments} and support them theoretically in Appendix~\ref{app:theory}.

We propose \method (Figure~\ref{fig:framework}), a graph contrastive learning framework that operationalizes topology repair through behavioral homophily recovery. On \hofinet, the recovered neighborhood graph improves the S-S:S-B ratio from 1:5.7 to 1:1.4, and edge homophily rises from 0.690 on the transaction graph to 0.981 on the repaired topology; AMLworld~\cite{amlpatterns2024realistic} and AMLNet exhibit the same pattern. Our contributions are summarized below.

\section{Related Work}\label{sec:related_work}
% Korean drafts below are kept inside \iffalse blocks for reference and are not compiled.
\subsection{Heterophily-aware GNNs}
A line of work improves message passing when label homophily is low by separating ego and neighbor embeddings, modifying propagation, or decoupling structure from attributes (e.g., H2GCN~\cite{zhu2020beyond}, Geom-GCN~\cite{pei2020geom}, LINKX~\cite{zhu2021linkx}). These models still operate on the observed transaction topology; in AML, suspicious accounts intentionally mix with benign counterparties, which can make suspicious--suspicious edges structurally scarce. \method complements heterophily-aware propagation by repairing the topology itself: it builds a class-relevant neighborhood structure from a topology-independent behavioral homophily recovery graph.

\subsection{Self-supervised GCL and auxiliary-view design}
Self-supervised GCL learns representations by enforcing consistency across multiple graph views~\cite{velivckovic2018deep,you2020graph}. Most existing approaches either perturb a single input graph or form views at the model and representation level, leaving the underlying neighborhood structure essentially unchanged. Auxiliary-graph approaches introduce a separate view, such as a k-NN affinity graph~\cite{shang2023mlgcl}; however, when built from all available features, this view can entangle behavioral similarity with topology-correlated variables. \method constructs a topology-independent affinity graph from behavioral features only and uses it for topology repair, then studies when aggregation on the repaired topology preserves versus dilutes suspicious evidence. Unlike prior k-NN auxiliary views that mainly enrich an existing topology, we motivate the construction as topology repair: when the observed graph is adversarially manipulated to be heterophilous (e.g., mule networks), the recovered neighborhood graph replaces broken neighborhood structure so downstream learning can operate on class-relevant neighborhoods.

\subsection{AML and fraud detection, and unsupervised anomaly detection under label delay}
In AML and fraud detection, supervised GNNs can improve detection under class imbalance via neighborhood selection and reweighting~\cite{dou2020enhancing}. However, they typically require training-time labels that are delayed and limited in AML operations. Unsupervised anomaly detection provides label-free alternatives~\cite{ding2019dominant}, but is mostly evaluated on general graph benchmarks and does not directly address the suspicious-class neighborhood mismatch in transaction networks. \method targets this gap by combining topology-independent topology repair with self-supervised learning, and by analyzing aggregation-level signal preservation on the repaired topology.

\section{Method: \method}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Problem Formulation}
We model the transaction network as a directed graph $\mathcal{G}_{\text{trans}} = (\mathcal{V}, \mathcal{E}, \mathbf{X})$, where $\mathcal{V}$ is the set of accounts (nodes, $|\mathcal{V}|=N$), $\mathcal{E} \subseteq \mathcal{V} \times \mathcal{V}$ is the set of transfer transactions (edges), and $\mathbf{X} \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times F}$ is the node feature matrix. Each node $v_i$ has a suspicion label $y_i \in \{0, 1\}$, but we use a self-supervised setting that ignores labels during training. Our goal is to learn node representations $\mathbf{Z} \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times D}$ that identify suspicious accounts based purely on the graph structure and node features.

\subsection{Feature Taxonomy}
\label{subsec:feature_taxonomy}
We group the node features available in a transaction network into behavioral, network-derived, and structural categories (Table~\ref{tab:features}).

The behavioral features capture transaction patterns via amount statistics, time-window frequencies, and transaction-type entropies (Table~\ref{tab:features}). Network-derived features (transaction counts and degrees) are input to the encoder but are excluded from the auxiliary view, while structural features (centrality, hub/authority, topology) are used only for analysis and auxiliary comparison. We denote by $\mathbf{X}^{\text{enc}}$ the behavioral and network-derived features given to the GNN encoder, and by $\mathbf{X}^{\text{bhv}}$ the 20 behavioral features used to construct a behavioral affinity graph. When suspicious accounts transact with benign ones to evade detection, the suspicious class exhibits low neighborhood homophily in the observed transaction topology. We counter this by recovering a topology-independent behavioral view (topology repair), linking accounts with similar behavior even if they are not directly connected by transactions.

\subsection{Recovered Neighborhood Graph for Topology Repair}
\label{subsec:knn_view}
\method realizes topology repair as a concrete graph object: a topology-independent recovered neighborhood graph $\mathcal{G}_{\text{bhv}}$ that replaces the heterophilous suspicious-class neighborhoods of the transaction topology. Standard augmentation-based GCL produces contrastive inputs by dropping edges or masking features on the original graph, so the underlying transaction neighborhoods are left intact. We instead build $\mathcal{G}_{\text{bhv}}$ independently of the edge structure of $\mathcal{G}_{\text{trans}}$ (Figure~\ref{fig:framework}) and use only behavioral features, so that the repair does not leak connectivity-derived information back into the construction.

\textbf{View construction.} We first standardize the behavioral features $\mathbf{x}_i^{\text{bhv}} \in \mathbb{R}^{20}$ to have a zero mean and unit variance to remove scale differences across variables, and then apply L2 normalization, such that similarity depends on the direction of the feature vector:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:l2norm}
    \bar{\mathbf{x}}_i = \text{StandardScaler}(\mathbf{x}_i^{\text{bhv}}), \quad
    \tilde{\mathbf{x}}_i = \frac{\bar{\mathbf{x}}_i}{\|\bar{\mathbf{x}}_i\|_2}
\end{equation}

Because the Euclidean distance between two L2-normalized vectors is monotonic in their cosine similarity, we retrieve the $k$ nearest neighbors of each node $v_i$ using a ball-tree search over Euclidean distance. Connecting each node to these neighbors yields the behavioral view graph $\mathcal{G}_{\text{bhv}} = (\mathcal{V}, \mathcal{E}_{\text{bhv}})$:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:knn_edge}
    \mathcal{E}_{\text{bhv}} = \{(v_i, v_j) \mid v_j \in \operatorname{kNN}(v_i, k)\}.
\end{equation}

We use the symmetrized (bidirectional) version of this behavioral view graph for message passing and pooling; accordingly, we add $(v_j, v_i)$ whenever $(v_i, v_j) \in \mathcal{E}_{\text{bhv}}$.

Here, $\operatorname{kNN}(v_i, k)$ denotes the set of $k$ nodes closest (in Euclidean distance) to $\tilde{\mathbf{x}}_i$, with $k=10$ by default.

Money-laundering accounts that split transactions and layer transfers often show similar patterns in amount distributions, time-window frequencies, and transaction-type entropies. The behavioral view graph thus links suspicious accounts that are not directly connected by transactions, recovering suspicious-to-suspicious neighborhoods missing in the transaction view.

\subsection{Dual-View GNN Encoder}
\label{subsec:dual_view}
Dual-view contrastive learning aligns a transaction view, which captures transfer structure, with a behavioral view that captures behavioral similarity for the same account. \method forms these views from $\mathcal{G}_{\text{trans}}$ and $\mathcal{G}_{\text{bhv}}$, with stochastic edge dropping and feature masking applied during training.

\textbf{Contrastive views.} Using only the transaction view mixes suspicious and benign accounts, while using only the behavioral view loses transaction-structure information. \method learns a shared account representation across both views, so embeddings capture both transaction structure and behavioral similarity.

\textbf{GNN encoder.} We apply stochastic edge dropping and feature masking ($p_e{=}p_f{=}0.3$) to the transaction and behavioral view graphs to form two views. A shared online encoder $f_\theta$ outputs embeddings $\mathbf{H}^{(1)} = f_\theta(\mathbf{X}^{\text{enc}}, \tilde{\mathcal{E}}_{\text{trans}})$ and $\mathbf{H}^{(2)} = f_\theta(\mathbf{X}^{\text{enc}}, \tilde{\mathcal{E}}_{\text{bhv}})$. The target encoder $f_\xi$ is an EMA copy that provides stop-gradient representations for the opposite BYOL branch. The online encoder stacks GCNConv~\cite{kipf2016semi} layers with batch normalization, nonlinear activation, and dropout. \method is encoder-agnostic and supports nine encoders, including GIN~\cite{xu2018powerful}, MVGRL, and GBT; Section~\ref{subsec:rq2} studies the effect of batch normalization.

\subsection{Subgraph Pooling}
\label{subsec:subgraph_pool}
\textbf{Subgraph-level signal aggregation.} \method compares not only individual node representations but also a subgraph representation that bundles each account with its neighbors (Subgraph Pooling in Figure~\ref{fig:framework}). This reflects the idea that whether an account is suspicious often becomes clearer from its relationships with neighboring counterparts than from its own attributes alone.

\textbf{Subgraph representation.} For each view $t \in \{1,2\}$, we form the subgraph representation of node $v_i$ by averaging its own representation with the representations of its neighbors in that view:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:subgraph_pool}
    \mathbf{s}_i^{(t)} = \frac{1}{|\mathcal{N}^{(t)}(v_i)| + 1}
    \left(\mathbf{h}_i^{(t)} + \sum_{v_j \in \mathcal{N}^{(t)}(v_i)} \mathbf{h}_j^{(t)}\right)
\end{equation}

Here $\mathcal{N}^{(1)}(v_i)$ is the neighborhood of $v_i$ in the transaction view, and $\mathcal{N}^{(2)}(v_i)$ is its neighborhood in the behavioral view. For both views, we perform message passing and pooling on a bidirected graph; hence, $\mathcal{N}^{(t)}(v_i)$ can be interpreted as the union of in- and out-neighbors. The operation is implemented efficiently with \texttt{scatter\_add}. The mean pooling in Eq.~\eqref{eq:subgraph_pool} is the default in \method.

\textbf{View-wise pooling effect.} Subgraph pooling is conducted independently for each view’s neighborhood: transaction-graph neighbors are pooled for the transaction view, while behaviorally similar neighbors are pooled for the behavioral view. Because pooling aggregates neighbor information, the choice of view shapes the outcome. The behavioral view graph is designed to place behaviorally similar suspicious accounts closer together; therefore, pooling on this view can preserve or strengthen suspicious signals. In the transaction graph, suspicious accounts are often linked to benign ones (high S-B ratio); consequently, pooling can dilute suspicious signals with benign information.

\textbf{Pooling variants.} We use mean pooling by default. Other pooling choices are possible, including similarity-weighted pooling (HAP). We report its expected signal-preservation behavior in Appendix~\ref{app:theory} (Proposition~\ref{thm:hap}).

\subsection{Training and Inference}
\label{subsec:contrastive_learning}
\textbf{Projector and BYOL loss.} Subgraph-pooled representations pass through a projector $g$ and a predictor $q$ and are trained with a BYOL-style loss~\cite{grill2020bootstrap} (Projector and BYOL Loss in Figure~\ref{fig:framework}). Instead of pushing other accounts as negatives, the loss pulls together representations of the same account across the two views. Both views are encoded by the online encoder $f_\theta$ and the target encoder $f_\xi$, and the loss sums the $1{\to}2$ and $2{\to}1$ directions. The target encoder is a momentum copy of the online encoder ($\xi \leftarrow m\xi + (1-m)\theta$, $m=0.99$), retaining most of the previous target while slowly incorporating new information.

We denote the loss input representation by $\mathbf{r}_i^{(t)}$. In our setting, $\mathbf{r}_i^{(t)}=\mathbf{s}_i^{(t)}$; in the node-level ablation, $\mathbf{r}_i^{(t)}=\mathbf{h}_i^{(t)}$. Let $\mathbf{r}_{\xi,i}^{(t)}$ be the corresponding target-branch representation, and $g_\xi$ the target-branch projector. The BYOL loss maximizes cosine similarity between representations from the two views:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:loss}
    \mathcal{L} = \sum_{t \in \{1,2\}} \left(2 - 2 \cdot \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N} \frac{q(g(\mathbf{r}_i^{(t)})) \cdot g_\xi(\mathbf{r}_{\xi,i}^{(3-t)})}{\|q(g(\mathbf{r}_i^{(t)}))\| \cdot \|g_\xi(\mathbf{r}_{\xi,i}^{(3-t)})\|}\right)
\end{equation}

Here $g$ and $q$ are the projection and predictor heads, and $\mathbf{r}_{\xi,i}^{(\cdot)}$ is the input representation obtained from the target encoder branch.

\textbf{Inference.} At inference, we freeze the encoder and concatenate the two view representations to obtain the final embedding (right side of Figure~\ref{fig:framework}). In the subgraph-level setting, we concatenate subgraph representations:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:concat}
    \mathbf{z}_i = [\mathbf{s}_i^{(1)} \| \mathbf{s}_i^{(2)}].
\end{equation}
For node-level ablations, we use $\mathbf{z}_i = [\mathbf{h}_i^{(1)} \| \mathbf{h}_i^{(2)}]$.

Encoder architectures and training hyperparameters are available via an anonymized link for double-blind review.

\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}
We assess \method in four phases, moving from mechanism to practical deployment. First, we examine whether the recovered neighborhood graph can make up for missing suspicious-to-suspicious links and restore view-level homophily, thereby boosting downstream performance. Second, we test when aggregating on the repaired topology (subgraph pooling) helps versus hurts, and how normalization affects training stability. Third, we benchmark the resulting self-supervised representations against supervised baselines under label delay and cold-start conditions, reflecting realistic AML settings. Fourth, we test whether the same mechanism and empirical patterns carry over to AML datasets that differ in prevalence and scale. Together, these experiments address the following research questions.

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{RQ1. Behavioral homophily recovery:} Does the topology-independent recovered neighborhood graph repair the suspicious-class topology relative to the transaction graph?
\item \textbf{RQ2. Signal preservation under aggregation:} When does subgraph pooling help versus hurt, and how does recovered homophily change this trade-off?
\item \textbf{RQ3. Self-supervised AML detection under label scarcity:} Does the self-supervised representation from \method remain competitive with supervised models under label delay and cold start?
\item \textbf{RQ4. Robustness across AML regimes:} Do the same mechanisms and trends apply to AML datasets with different prevalence and scale?
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Experimental Setting}
\textbf{Datasets.} We use three AML datasets (Table~\ref{tab:datasets}). \hofinet includes about 4.7M synthetic transactions derived from real activity on a national interbank settlement network, modeling 40 months of household and corporate transfers. AMLworld~\cite{amlpatterns2024realistic}, the NeurIPS 2023 open synthetic benchmark, models eight transaction patterns (e.g., fan-out, scatter-gather, cycle); we use the HI-Small variant (high illicit ratio, small size). AMLNet~\cite{huda2025amlnet}, a small synthetic benchmark for the high-prevalence regime, shares AMLworld’s feature schema.

\textbf{Account-level labels.} All three datasets provide transaction-level suspicious labels. Following standard graph-based AML and fraud detection practices~\cite{dou2020enhancing,liu2021pick}, we max-aggregate to the account level: an account is suspicious (label$=1$) if it appears on either side of any suspicious transaction, and normal (label$=0$) otherwise. The resulting suspicious-account ratios are 2.13\% (\hofinet), 1.23\% (AMLworld), and 13.52\% (AMLNet) (Table~\ref{tab:datasets}).

\textbf{Topology repair via behavioral similarity.} The transaction graph's S-S:S-B ratio of 1:5.7 (Table~\ref{tab:ss_sb}) improves to 1:1.4 under the recovered neighborhood graph (Figure~\ref{fig:intro_topology_repair}), and edge homophily $h$ (the fraction of edges whose endpoints share the same label) increases from 0.690 to 0.981. Because money-laundering accounts share transaction patterns such as split transfers and multi-hop routing, behavioral similarity tends to reconnect them in the recovered graph. The structural view, in contrast, worsens the ratio to 1:9.7, which supports our design choice to exclude structural features from the construction and to use them only for analysis.

\textbf{From the case study to Table~\ref{tab:rq1}.} For the Figure~\ref{fig:intro_topology_repair} account, the encoder's aggregation neighborhood is $3.8\%$ suspicious in (a), $7.4\%$ in (c), $90.0\%$ in (b), and $90.9\%$ in (d). The $\le 7.4\%$ signals in (a)/(c) push the account into the benign region (false negative, contributing to the GBT scores $0.266$ and $0.197$), while the $\ge 90\%$ signals in (b)/(d) classify it correctly ($0.671$ and $0.673$); pooling (c$\to$d) sharpens this gap because the suspicious ego reinforces a suspicious-peer cluster but not a benign-dominated star. Overall, the effect of subgraph pooling depends on (i) view homophily and (ii) encoder normalization depth.

\begin{theorem}[Normalization-conditional stability under bootstrap objectives]\label{thm:bn_collapse}
Under the BYOL-style bootstrap loss~\eqref{eq:loss}, the trivial constant solution $f_\theta(\cdot) \equiv c$ attains $\mathcal{L}=0$ when the encoder lacks batch normalization (BN). In contrast, the presence of at least one BN layer rules out this trivial constant mapping under the definition of batch normalization, acting as a sufficient condition to avoid the constant-solution pathology.
\end{theorem}

\textbf{Empirical check for the stability analysis.} At setting (a), BN-less encoders achieve $F1_{\text{susp}} \approx 0.04$, close to the \hofinet random-classifier upper bound $2\rho/(1+\rho) \approx 0.042$ (the maximum $F1_{\text{susp}}$ at a positive rate $\rho$), indicating that unstable training can wash out the suspicious-class signal in low-prevalence regimes.
The practical severity depends on prevalence: at low prevalence ($\rho \leq 2\%$), the bound is already $\approx 0.04$; hence, instability leaves little usable suspicious-class evidence. On AMLNet ($\rho = 13.52\%$), BN-less DGI still reaches $F1_{\text{susp}} \approx 0.40$ (Section~\ref{subsec:rq4}); consequently, the absolute impact is smaller. Overall, the results align with a single mechanism: homophily recovery provides class-relevant neighborhoods, sign-flip explains when aggregation can dilute them, and normalization mainly modulates whether this mechanism is realized under the bootstrap objective.

\subsection{Self-Supervised AML Detection under Label Scarcity (RQ3)}
\label{subsec:rq3}

RQ3 asks whether \method's self-supervised representation remains competitive with supervised baselines as labels become scarce. We vary the train label fraction in $\{1\%, 5\%, 10\%\}$ and compare \method against six supervised baselines spanning three families: tabular (MLP, XGBoost~\cite{xgboost2016}, LightGBM~\cite{lightgbm2017}), generic GNN (GCN~\cite{kipf2016semi}, GAT~\cite{velivckovic2017graph}), and fraud-specific GNN (CARE-GNN~\cite{dou2020enhancing}). MLP follows the standard convention (stacked Linear$+$ReLU; no BatchNorm) common in fraud-GNN literature. All models share the same 22 behavioral features; \method uses setting (d) with the strongest encoder per dataset (GBT on \hofinet and AMLNet, BGRL on AMLworld).

\textbf{\method dominates the 1\%-label cold-start regime.} At 1\% labels, \method attains the highest $F1_{\text{susp}}$ on every dataset: 0.654 on \hofinet, 0.062 on AMLworld, and 0.632 on AMLNet. The margin over the next-best supervised baseline is $+0.021$ (\hofinet, vs.\ XGBoost), $+0.017$ (AMLworld, vs.\ MLP), and $+0.010$ (AMLNet, vs.\ MLP). Among graph baselines, \method exceeds the strongest (GAT) by $+0.25$ on \hofinet, $+0.02$ on AMLworld, and $+0.26$ on AMLNet at 1\% labels.

\textbf{Tabular boosting catches up at 10\% labels.} XGBoost and LightGBM approach \method monotonically as labels increase. On AMLworld, XGBoost reaches $F1_{\text{susp}}=0.073$ at 10\% labels versus \method's 0.066; on AMLNet, XGBoost reaches 0.699 versus \method's 0.679. The gap to \method shrinks as the label budget grows, supporting the operational claim that \method's main value lies in the label-delayed regime characteristic of AML operations~\cite{hilal2022financial}.

\textbf{BN-conditional collapse generalizes from self-supervised to supervised models.} On the low-prevalence \hofinet ($\rho{=}2.13\%$) and AMLworld ($\rho{=}1.23\%$), bare MLP collapses to near-random performance ($F1_{\text{susp}}\approx 0.05$, close to the random-classifier upper bound $2\rho/(1+\rho)$). The same model reaches $F1_{\text{susp}}=0.62$--$0.68$ on AMLNet ($\rho{=}13.52\%$), where the random-classifier ceiling is much higher ($\approx 0.24$). This mirrors the BN-free self-supervised encoders analyzed in Theorem~\ref{thm:bn_collapse}: collapse-prevention—via BatchNorm or sufficient prevalence—becomes critical in low-prevalence regimes, regardless of whether training is supervised or self-supervised. \method's encoder uses per-layer BatchNorm and avoids this collapse on all three datasets.

\textbf{Graph baselines remain consistently weaker than \method.} GCN, GAT, and CARE-GNN trail \method by $0.13$--$0.57$ $F1_{\text{susp}}$ at 10\% labels on \hofinet, $0.02$ on AMLworld, and $0.16$--$0.24$ on AMLNet. These baselines aggregate on the original transaction topology and inherit its anti-homophilous neighborhood structure; \method first repairs that topology before contrastive training. The gap is stable across all label fractions, indicating that topology repair, not message-passing capacity, is the binding constraint for graph-based AML detection.

\textbf{Operational utility with a limited analyst budget.} \method with GBT attains \hofinet precision@$k$ of 0.875, 0.853, 0.834, 0.791, and 0.746 for $k \in \{50, 100, 200, 400, 800\}$ (4 seeds), detecting approximately 317 true suspicious accounts within a top-400 budget.

\subsection{Robustness across AML Regimes (RQ4)}\label{subsec:rq4}

Finally, RQ4 asks whether the patterns established in RQ1–RQ3 transfer to AML regimes with different prevalence and scale. We therefore repeat the same ablation on two structurally distinct datasets: AMLworld, a large low-prevalence simulator dataset ($\rho{=}1.23\%$), and AMLNet, a smaller high-prevalence benchmark ($\rho{=}13.52\%$). Table~\ref{tab:rq4} reports $F1_{\text{susp}}$ across view-by-level settings for five encoders.

The cross-regime trend is consistent: topology repair provides most of the gains, and subgraph-level evaluation sharpens suspicious-class decisions once the topology has been repaired.

\textbf{AMLworld: amplification in the low-prevalence regime.} On AMLworld, BGRL reaches $F1_{\text{susp}}=0.066$ in (d), a 73\% gain over (a), and all encoders satisfy (b) $>$ (a). In this low-prevalence regime ($\rho{=}1.23\%$), subgraph pooling on the repaired topology (d) beats node-level evaluation on the same graph (b): once topology repair restores neighborhood class context, subgraph pooling aggregates it into stronger suspicious-account evidence, including for per-layer BN encoders.

\textbf{AMLNet: high prevalence reduces instability in BN-free encoders.} On AMLNet, the baseline (a) already attains $F1_{\text{susp}}=0.40$--$0.45$, far above \hofinet (0.04--0.27) and AMLworld (0.038--0.042). The high suspicious-account ratio (13.52\%) keeps even the BN-free DGI encoder outside the unstable regime described in Theorem~\ref{thm:bn_collapse}. Collapse prevention is most critical at low prevalence and weakens as the random-classifier ceiling rises. View and level effects still match \hofinet; for example, GBT rises from 0.426 in (a) to 0.679 in (d), a relative gain of over 50\%. All three datasets show the same topology-repair pattern reported in Table~\ref{tab:ss_sb}.

\section{Conclusion and Future Work}
We introduced \method, a graph contrastive learning framework that performs topology repair for AML detection in low-homophily transaction networks. \method anchors its contrastive objective to a behavioral homophily recovery graph that replaces the broken suspicious-class neighborhoods of the transaction topology, while retaining the transaction graph itself as the co-view at inference (Eq.~\ref{eq:concat}) so that downstream fund-flow tracing can still operate on real transactions. This repair raises \hofinet edge homophily from 0.690 to 0.981. Under realistic label scarcity (1\% labels), \method achieves the highest $F1_{\text{susp}}$ on all three datasets (e.g., 0.654 on \hofinet), exceeding the strongest supervised baseline by 0.010--0.021. At 10\% labels, tabular boosting models catch up on AMLworld and AMLNet, while \method retains the lead on \hofinet and surpasses all supervised graph baselines across the board.

The results support one mechanism: topology repair yields class-relevant neighborhoods; sign-flip analysis shows when aggregation can dilute them; subgraph pooling on the repaired topology refines them conditionally; and normalization mainly stabilizes the bootstrap objective. These claims follow from the stability analysis (Theorem~\ref{thm:bn_collapse}) and the homophily-recovery and aggregation-signal analyses (Theorems~\ref{thm:knn_homophily}--\ref{thm:meanpool}), with detailed statements in Appendix~\ref{app:theory}.

\method targets AML regimes with delayed labels, low prevalence, and anti-homophilous suspicious neighborhoods. In high-prevalence regimes with abundant labels, stronger supervised baselines exist; \method is designed for the label-delayed, low-prevalence regime that characterizes operational AML. 

Limitations remain. Our evaluation is transductive and does not include temporal hold-out splits; all three benchmarks are synthetic. Real-world labeled AML transaction graphs are typically protected by banking secrecy and confidential reporting frameworks, which restrict redistribution even for anonymized snapshots. Future work includes evaluation with temporal splits and validation on real transaction data when possible.

\textbf{Detection vs.\ investigation.} \method optimizes detection accuracy: the recovered neighborhood graph drives the gains in Table~\ref{tab:rq1}, and the transaction graph is retained at inference but is not specifically optimized for the investigation workflow. Suspicious Transaction Report filing, mule-network reconstruction, and fund-flow tracing still operate on the original transaction topology; the recovered graph cannot stand in for it post-detection. We position \method as the first-stage screen in a two-stage pipeline (behavioral screening followed by transaction-graph investigation), not as a single-view replacement for the transaction graph.

\textbf{Reproducibility.} We will release the source code, training scripts, and seed-level raw results. \hofinet cannot be redistributed due to its data-sharing agreement, but the AMLworld and AMLNet experiments are reproducible from public benchmarks.

\section{Theory: Proofs and Empirical Checks}
\label{app:theory}

This appendix presents the assumptions and main derivations used in the theoretical part of the paper, and it also reports simple numerical checks.

\subsection{Setup and Notation}
\label{app:theory_notation}

Let $V = V_S \cup V_B$ be the node set, where $V_S$ are suspicious ($S$) nodes and $V_B$ are benign ($B$) nodes. Let $N:=|V|$, $N_S:=|V_S|$, and $N_B:=|V_B|$. Define the prevalence $\rho:=N_S/N$ and $N_{\min}:=\min(N_S,N_B)$. For a graph $G=(V,E)$, define edge homophily as $h(G):=|\{(u,v)\in E: y_u=y_v\}|/|E|$.

Each node $v$ has behavioral features $x_v \in \mathbb{R}^d$. We assume i.i.d. class-conditional features: $x_v \mid y_v = c \sim P_c$ for $c \in \{S,B\}$. For any $x \in \text{supp}(P_y)$, $y' \neq y$, and independent samples $X' \sim P_y$, $X'' \sim P_{y'}$, we assume
\begin{equation}\label{eq:sep}
    \Pr\big[\|x - X'\| < \|x - X''\|\big] \;\geq\; 1 - \epsilon.
\end{equation}
That is, a same-class node is closer than a different-class node with probability at least $1-\epsilon$. When $\epsilon=0$, this becomes deterministic. Let $G_{\text{knn},k}$ be the Euclidean k-NN graph where each node connects to its $k$ nearest neighbors.

\subsection{View-level Homophily Recovery}
\label{app:theory_view}

\begin{theorem}[Probabilistic homophily recovery]
\label{thm:knn_homophily}
Under $k \leq (1-\epsilon)(N_{\min}-1) - 1$, the k-NN graph $G_{\text{knn},k}$ constructed from behavioral features satisfies
\[
    h(G_{\text{knn}, k}) \;\geq\; 1 - \tfrac{N - 1}{k} \exp\!\left(-\tfrac{((N_{\min}-1)(1-\epsilon) - k)^2}{2(N_{\min}-1)(1-\epsilon)}\right).
\]
\end{theorem}
\noindent\textbf{Remark.} The condition $k \leq (1-\epsilon)(N_{\min}-1) - 1$ is mild in our setting: it requires $k$ to be much smaller than the minority-class size. In our datasets, $N_{\min}$ ranges from 1{,}487 (AMLNet) to 9{,}644 (\hofinet), and we use $k=10$ throughout.

For a suspicious node $u$, a benign node $B_j$ enters the top-$k$ neighbor list only if
$Z_j := |\{i : \|x_u - x_{S_i}\| < \|x_u - x_{B_j}\|\}| < k$.
By Assumption~\eqref{eq:sep}, $Z_j$ stochastically dominates $\text{Binom}(N_S{-}1,1{-}\epsilon)$. A Chernoff lower-tail bound plus a union bound gives Theorem~\ref{thm:knn_homophily}; in the deterministic limit $\epsilon=0$, the bound becomes $h(G_{\text{knn},k})=1$.

For the transaction graph $G_{\text{tx}}$, we use a degree-corrected stochastic block model (DCSBM)~\cite{karrer2011stochastic}. Each node $v$ has an activity parameter $\theta_v>0$, and $\omega_{y_u,y_v}$ controls the connection strength between classes. We assume
\begin{equation}\label{eq:dcsbm}
    \Pr[(u, v) \in E_{\text{tx}}] \;\propto\; \theta_u \theta_v \cdot \omega_{y_u, y_v}.
\end{equation}
Let $\Theta_c := \sum_{v: y_v=c}\theta_v$. Then
\[
    h(G_{\text{tx}}) = \frac{\Theta_S^2 \omega_{SS} + \Theta_B^2 \omega_{BB}}{\Theta_S^2 \omega_{SS} + 2\Theta_S\Theta_B \omega_{SB} + \Theta_B^2 \omega_{BB}}.
\]

To estimate the (node-averaged) ego-heterophily under the DCSBM, we sample a node $v$ uniformly at random from $V$, compute the expected cross-class neighbor fraction conditional on $y_v\in\{S,B\}$, and then average over the classes:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:eta_dcsbm}
\eta_h^{(\text{dcsbm})}
\;=\;
\rho\,\frac{\Theta_B\omega_{SB}}{\Theta_S\omega_{SS}+\Theta_B\omega_{SB}}
\;+
(1-\rho)\,\frac{\Theta_S\omega_{SB}}{\Theta_B\omega_{BB}+\Theta_S\omega_{SB}}.
\end{equation}

\begin{theorem}[Combined homophily gap]
\label{thm:combined}
Under the DCSBM, if $h(G_{\text{tx}}) = \alpha$, then
\[
h(G_{\text{knn}, k}) - h(G_{\text{tx}}) \geq 1 - \alpha - \delta(N, N_{\min}, k, \epsilon),
\]
where $\delta$ is the exponential term in Theorem~\ref{thm:knn_homophily}.
\end{theorem}

Using Theorem~\ref{thm:knn_homophily} (i.e., $h(G_{\text{knn},k}) \geq 1-\delta$) and subtracting $h(G_{\text{tx}})=\alpha$, we obtain the homophily-gap bound:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:gap}
    h(G_{\text{knn}, k}) - h(G_{\text{tx}}) \;\geq\; 1 - \alpha - \tfrac{N - 1}{k} \exp\!\left(-\tfrac{((N_{\min}-1)(1-\epsilon) - k)^2}{2(N_{\min}-1)(1-\epsilon)}\right).
\end{equation}
On \hofinet ($N=452{,}816$, $N_{\min}=9{,}644$, $k=10$, $\alpha=0.690$) with $\epsilon=0.05$, the exponential term is negligible (below $10^{-1000}$), giving a bound of $\approx 0.310$. The measured gap is $0.981-0.690=0.291$, which is 0.019 below the bound. We treat this as a sufficient condition, not an exact predictor.

\subsection{Aggregation-level Signal Preservation}
\label{subsec:theory_pool}

We now study the aggregation step. We write the hidden representation of node $v$ as $z_v = s_v \mu + \eta_v$. Here $s_v \in \{\pm 1\}$ is the class signal, $\mu$ is the direction that separates suspicious and benign nodes, and $\eta_v$ is a bounded noise term with $\|\eta_v\| \leq \sigma \|\mu\|$. (We use $\sigma$ here to avoid confusion with the separability parameter $\epsilon$ in Assumption~\eqref{eq:sep}.) We define the ego-neighborhood heterophily as
$\eta_h := |\{u \in \mathcal{N}(v) : y_u \neq y_v\}| / |\mathcal{N}(v)|$.
We compare the post-pooling projection on $\mu$,
$\varrho := \langle z_v^{(\cdot)}, \mu \rangle / \|\mu\|^2$,
for mean pooling and for heterophily-aware pooling (HAP) with cosine-similarity weights. Without loss of generality, we set $s_v=+1$.

\begin{theorem}[Mean pool signal flip]
\label{thm:meanpool}
Assuming $s_v=+1$ without loss of generality, $\mathbb{E}[\varrho^{(\text{mean})}] = (1 + k(1 - 2\eta_h)) / (k+1)$. The mean-pooled signal flips sign (i.e., $\mathbb{E}[\varrho] < 0$) when $\eta_h > 1/2 + 1/(2k)$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proposition}[HAP signal preservation under small-noise regime]
\label{thm:hap}
Assume $\sigma < 1/\sqrt{2}$ and that cosine similarities maintain the correct sign. Then, for any $\eta_h$, the HAP post-pooling expectation satisfies
\[
\mathbb{E}[\varrho^{(\text{hap})}] \geq 1 - 2\sigma^2 - 4\sigma^2 \eta_h.
\]
\end{proposition}

\paragraph{Predicted 4-setting ablation pattern.}\label{cor:ablation}
Assume an encoder with BN and $\eta_h^{\text{tx}} > 1/2 + 1/(2k)$. Then the predicted ranking of $F1_{\text{susp}}$ is $F1(b) \approx F1(d) > F1(a) > F1(c)$. On \hofinet, all six BN-equipped encoders follow this order ($\Delta(b{-}a) \in [+0.255, +0.405]$, $\Delta(c{-}a) \in [-0.070, -0.052]$).

\subsection{Cross-Dataset Empirical Check}\label{app:theory_verify}

Table~\ref{tab:theory_verify} compares the theory with the measured values. The homophily-gap lower bound in Theorem~\ref{thm:combined} is close to the measured gap on \hofinet and AMLworld (difference $\approx 0.02$ for $\epsilon=0.05$), but the gap is less tight on AMLNet, likely because AMLNet is smaller and has higher prevalence.
