\begin{abstract}
In anti-money laundering (AML), suspicious-transaction labels usually arrive only after lengthy regulatory investigations, making labeled data scarce and delayed. Transaction graphs also place suspicious accounts among many benign counterparties; consequently, standard GNN aggregation can dilute the rare suspicious signal. We argue that this stems from a topology–behavior mismatch: the observed transaction topology is adversarially shaped and can be a poor proxy for suspicious-class homophily. To address this, we propose \method, a graph contrastive learning framework whose primary contribution is \emph{topology repair via behavioral homophily recovery}: we construct a topology-independent behavioral view (a behavioral affinity graph) that links accounts by similarity in behavioral transaction patterns (e.g., amount statistics, temporal frequencies, transaction-type entropy) without leaking connectivity-derived variables into the view. On the Home and Firm banking Network (HOFINET), the suspicious ego-neighborhood ratio improves from Suspicious-Suspicious:Suspicious-Benign $=$ 1:5.7 in the transaction graph to 1:1.4 in the recovered behavioral view, while global edge homophily rises from 0.690 to 0.981. \method improves suspicious-account detection across HOFINET, AMLworld, and AMLNet; subgraph-level pooling on the recovered view serves as a conditional refinement with modest additional gains. On HOFINET, its self-supervised representations are within 0.006 $F1_{\text{susp}}$ of a supervised MLP and outperform five fraud- and anomaly-specific GNNs. In a cold-start setting with only 1\% of labels, \method maintains $F1_{\text{susp}}{=}0.654$, demonstrating effective self-supervised learning for label-delayed AML.
\end{abstract}

\keywords{anti-money laundering, self-supervised learning, graph contrastive learning, behavioral auxiliary view, homophily recovery, topology repair, low-homophily, transaction networks}

Money laundering, accounting for an estimated 2-5\% of global GDP~\cite{teichmann2020recent,unodc_ml}, has become more sophisticated through tactics such as structuring, layering, and mule networks (layered chains that route funds through third-party or otherwise normal accounts). GNN-based methods have thus been explored to learn structural patterns in transaction networks beyond rule-based detection~\cite{drezewski2015application,cheng2025graph}. In AML, however, neighborhood aggregation is only useful when a node’s neighbors carry class-relevant evidence; for the suspicious class, this evidence is often weak because suspicious accounts intentionally mix with benign counterparties. Independently, AML data are also highly imbalanced and labeled with substantial delays. Accordingly, we focus on three recurring challenges in AML transaction networks—suspicious-class neighborhood heterophily~\cite{zhu2020beyond,dou2020enhancing}, severe class imbalance, and delayed/limited labels~\cite{cardoso2022laundrograph,lu2024graph}—and study how self-supervised view construction can mitigate the topology–behavior mismatch.

\textbf{Challenge 1: weak suspicious-class neighborhood signal.} Money-laundering accounts intentionally interact with benign accounts to evade detection, forming mule networks. In a synthetic transaction graph based on HOFINET, about 85\% of suspicious accounts' neighbors are benign (S-S:S-B $=$ 1:5.7, Figure~\ref{fig:intro_homophily}). As a result, even expressive message passing can struggle to accumulate suspicious evidence from local neighborhoods because suspicious–suspicious links are scarce, despite high global homophily dominated by benign–benign edges.

\textbf{Challenges 2–3: class imbalance and delayed/limited labels.} Suspicious accounts often constitute less than 2\% of all accounts~\cite{hilal2022financial}. Moreover, suspicious labels are confirmed only after retrospective investigations that can take more than six months~\cite{fatf2013guidance,kofia2024aml}; thus, at training time, reliable suspicious labels are often limited. Supervised fraud models can therefore face cold-start issues in production, especially when both label availability and neighborhood suspicious signals are weak.

Graph Contrastive Learning (GCL) is a natural fit because it learns label-free graph representations by enforcing consistency across augmented views~\cite{you2020graph,velivckovic2018deep,thakoor2021large,bielak2022graph}. Standard GCL, however, constructs views by perturbing the original transaction graph; thus, the same neighborhood mismatch can persist across both views~\cite{zhu2020deep,zhu2021graph,hassani2020contrastive}. In particular, view perturbations do not change which accounts are linked by transactions; thus, suspicious accounts can remain embedded among many benign neighbors.

We posit that the main challenge is a \emph{topology–behavior mismatch}: in AML transaction networks, the observed graph structure is strategically manipulated (via mule chains and mixing with legitimate parties), making the transaction graph a weak surrogate for homophily within the suspicious class. Accordingly, constructing a behavioral view is not just feature engineering, but a form of \emph{graph topology repair} (or \emph{homophily recovery}) that replaces heterophilous transaction neighborhoods with behaviorally similar ones, without leaking connectivity-derived variables into the view.

We study \method along two linked questions: (i) \emph{when} can a behavioral-only view recover suspicious-class homophily, and (ii) \emph{how} should aggregation operate on the recovered view without diluting the suspicious signal.

We propose \method (Figure~\ref{fig:framework}), a graph contrastive learning framework centered on the recovery of behavioral homophily. On HOFINET, the behavioral view improves the S-S:S-B ratio from 1:5.7 to 1:1.4, and edge homophily rises from 0.690 on the transaction graph to 0.981 on the behavioral view; AMLworld~\cite{amlpatterns2024realistic} and AMLNet exhibit the same pattern.

Our contributions are summarized below.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Behavioral homophily recovery as topology repair.} We construct a topology-independent behavioral view (behavioral-only features) that recovers suspicious-class neighborhoods. We quantify this effect empirically (homophily, S-S:S-B ratio) and theoretically via a DCSBM-based homophily recovery bound (§\ref{subsec:theory}).

    \item \textbf{From recovered homophily to usable representations under label delay.} \method acquires representations that remain competitive on three AML datasets with BYOL-style self-supervision, even in a cold-start scenario with under 1\% labeled data ($F1_{\text{susp}}{=}0.654$).

    \item \textbf{Signal preservation when aggregating on the recovered view.} We analyze when neighborhood aggregation helps versus hurts: a sign-flip characterization explains why pooling can dilute suspicious evidence in heterophilous transaction neighborhoods, while recovered behavioral neighborhoods enable pooling as a conditional refinement. BatchNorm-related collapse is treated as a supporting analysis for stability rather than the primary novelty.
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}\label{sec:related_work}
% Korean drafts below are kept inside \iffalse blocks for reference and are not compiled.
\subsection{Heterophily-aware GNNs}
A line of work improves message passing when label homophily is low by separating ego and neighbor embeddings, modifying propagation, or decoupling structure from attributes (e.g., H2GCN~\cite{zhu2020beyond}, Geom-GCN~\cite{pei2020geom}, FAGCN~\cite{bo2021beyond}, GBK-GNN~\cite{du2022gbk}, ACM-GCN~\cite{luan2022acmgcn}, GPR-GNN~\cite{chien2021adaptive}, LINKX~\cite{zhu2021linkx}). These models still operate on the observed transaction topology; in AML, suspicious accounts intentionally mix with benign counterparties; thus, suspicious–suspicious edges can be structurally scarce. \method complements heterophily-aware propagation by recovering a class-relevant neighborhood structure through a topology-independent behavioral auxiliary view.

\subsection{Self-supervised GCL and auxiliary-view design}
Self-supervised GCL learns representations by enforcing consistency across multiple graph views~\cite{velivckovic2018deep,zhu2020deep,you2020graph,zhu2021graph,thakoor2021large,bielak2022graph,hassani2020contrastive}. Most existing approaches either perturb a single input graph or form views at the model/representation level (e.g., AFGRL~\cite{lee2022augmentation}, GraphMAE~\cite{hou2022graphmae}), leaving the underlying neighborhood structure essentially unchanged. Auxiliary-graph approaches instead introduce a separate view, such as a k-NN affinity graph (e.g., MLGCL~\cite{shang2023mlgcl}, GCPAL~\cite{lu2024graph}); however, when built from all available features, this view can entangle behavioral similarity with topology-correlated variables. \method frames auxiliary-view construction as \emph{topology repair}: we build a topology-independent behavioral affinity graph (behavioral-only), quantify suspicious-class homophily recovery, and then study when aggregation on the recovered view preserves versus dilutes suspicious evidence.

\subsection{AML/fraud detection and unsupervised anomaly detection under label delay}
In AML and fraud detection, supervised GNNs leverage neighbor selection, sampling, reweighting, temporal modeling, and multi-relation inference~\cite{liu2020alleviating,dou2020enhancing,liu2021pick,peng2021reinforced,zhang2021fraudre,cheng2020graph,rao2020xfraud,wang2023label,cheng2025graph}, but they typically require training-time labels that are delayed and limited in AML operations. Unsupervised anomaly detection and contrastive objectives provide label-free alternatives (e.g., DOMINANT~\cite{ding2019dominant}, CoLA~\cite{liu2021anomaly}, ANEMONE~\cite{jin2021anemone}, BWGNN~\cite{tang2022rethinking}, BOND~\cite{liu2022bond}); however, they are mostly evaluated on general graph benchmarks and do not directly address the suspicious-class neighborhood mismatch in transaction networks. \method targets this gap by combining topology-independent view recovery with self-supervised learning and by analyzing aggregation-level signal preservation in the recovered neighborhoods.

\section{Method: \method}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Problem Formulation}
We model the transaction network as a directed graph $\mathcal{G}_{\text{trans}} = (\mathcal{V}, \mathcal{E}, \mathbf{X})$, where $\mathcal{V}$ is the set of accounts (nodes, $|\mathcal{V}|=N$), $\mathcal{E} \subseteq \mathcal{V} \times \mathcal{V}$ is the set of transfer transactions (edges), and $\mathbf{X} \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times F}$ is the node feature matrix. Each node $v_i$ has a suspicion label $y_i \in \{0, 1\}$, but we use a self-supervised setting that ignores labels during training. Our goal is to learn node representations $\mathbf{Z} \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times D}$ that identify suspicious accounts based purely on the graph structure and node features.

\subsection{Feature Taxonomy}
\label{subsec:feature_taxonomy}
We group the node features available in a transaction network into behavioral, network-derived, and structural categories (Table~\ref{tab:features}).

The behavioral features capture transaction patterns via amount statistics, time-window frequencies, and transaction-type entropies (Table~\ref{tab:features}). Network-derived features (transaction counts and degrees) are input to the encoder but are excluded from the auxiliary view, while structural features (centrality, hub/authority, topology) are used only for analysis and auxiliary comparison. We denote by $\mathbf{X}^{\text{enc}}$ the behavioral and network-derived features given to the GNN encoder, and by $\mathbf{X}^{\text{bhv}}$ the 20 behavioral features used to construct a behavioral affinity graph. When suspicious accounts transact with benign ones to evade detection, the suspicious class exhibits low neighborhood homophily in the observed transaction topology. We counter this by recovering a topology-independent behavioral view (topology repair), linking accounts with similar behavior even if they are not directly connected by transactions.

\subsection{Behavioral Auxiliary View for Homophily Recovery}
\label{subsec:knn_view}
A central component of \method is a topology-independent behavioral auxiliary view that acts as a recovered neighborhood graph for AML. Standard augmentation-based GCL produces contrastive inputs by dropping edges or masking features on the original graph, which cannot change heterophilous transaction neighborhoods. We instead construct a behavioral affinity graph independently of the edge structure of $\mathcal{G}_{\text{trans}}$ (Figure~\ref{fig:framework}), using behavioral-only features to avoid topology leakage.

\textbf{View construction.} We first standardize the behavioral features $\mathbf{x}_i^{\text{bev}} \in \mathbb{R}^{20}$ to have a zero mean and unit variance to remove scale differences across variables, and then apply L2 normalization, such that similarity depends on the direction of the feature vector:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:l2norm}
    \bar{\mathbf{x}}_i = \text{StandardScaler}(\mathbf{x}_i^{\text{bhv}}), \quad
    \tilde{\mathbf{x}}_i = \frac{\bar{\mathbf{x}}_i}{\|\bar{\mathbf{x}}_i\|_2}
\end{equation}

Because the Euclidean distance between two L2-normalized vectors is monotonic in their cosine similarity, we retrieve the $k$ nearest neighbors of each node $v_i$ using a ball-tree search over Euclidean distance. Connecting each node to these neighbors yields the behavioral view graph $\mathcal{G}_{\text{bhv}} = (\mathcal{V}, \mathcal{E}_{\text{bhv}})$:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:knn_edge}
    \mathcal{E}_{\text{bhv}} = \{(v_i, v_j) \mid v_j \in \operatorname{kNN}(v_i, k)\}.
\end{equation}

We use the symmetrized (bidirectional) version of this behavioral view graph for message passing and pooling; thus, we add $(v_j, v_i)$ whenever $(v_i, v_j) \in \mathcal{E}_{\text{bhv}}$.

Here, $\operatorname{kNN}(v_i, k)$ denotes the set of $k$ nodes closest (in Euclidean distance) to $\tilde{\mathbf{x}}_i$, with $k=10$ by default.

Money-laundering accounts that split transactions and layer transfers often show similar patterns in amount distributions, time-window frequencies, and transaction-type entropies. The behavioral view graph thus links suspicious accounts that are not directly connected by transactions, recovering suspicious-to-suspicious neighborhoods missing in the transaction view.

\subsection{Dual-View GNN Encoder}
\label{subsec:dual_view}
Dual-view contrastive learning aligns a transaction view, which captures transfer structure, with a behavioral view that captures behavioral similarity for the same account. \method forms these views from $\mathcal{G}_{\text{trans}}$ and $\mathcal{G}_{\text{bhv}}$, with stochastic edge dropping and feature masking applied during training.

\textbf{Contrastive views.} Using only the transaction view mixes suspicious and benign accounts, while using only the behavioral view loses transaction-structure information. \method learns a shared account representation across both views; thus, embeddings capture both transaction structure and behavioral similarity.

\textbf{GNN encoder.} We apply stochastic edge dropping and feature masking ($p_e{=}p_f{=}0.3$) to the transaction and behavioral view graphs to form two views. A shared online encoder $f_\theta$ outputs embeddings $\mathbf{H}^{(1)} = f_\theta(\mathbf{X}^{\text{enc}}, \tilde{\mathcal{E}}_{\text{trans}})$ and $\mathbf{H}^{(2)} = f_\theta(\mathbf{X}^{\text{enc}}, \tilde{\mathcal{E}}_{\text{bhv}})$. The target encoder $f_\xi$ is an EMA copy that provides stop-gradient representations for the opposite BYOL branch. The online encoder stacks GCNConv~\cite{kipf2016semi} layers with batch normalization, nonlinear activation, and dropout. \method is encoder-agnostic and supports 10 encoders, including GIN~\cite{xu2018powerful}, MVGRL, and GBT; Section~\ref{subsec:rq2} studies the effect of batch normalization.

\subsection{Subgraph Pooling}
\label{subsec:subgraph_pool}
\textbf{Subgraph-level signal aggregation.} \method compares not only individual node representations but also a subgraph representation that bundles each account with its neighbors (Subgraph Pooling in Figure~\ref{fig:framework}). This reflects the idea that whether an account is suspicious often becomes clearer from its relationships with neighboring counterparts than from its own attributes alone.

\textbf{Subgraph representation.} For each view $t \in \{1,2\}$, we form the subgraph representation of node $v_i$ by averaging its own representation with the representations of its neighbors in that view:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:subgraph_pool}
    \mathbf{s}_i^{(t)} = \frac{1}{|\mathcal{N}^{(t)}(v_i)| + 1}
    \left(\mathbf{h}_i^{(t)} + \sum_{v_j \in \mathcal{N}^{(t)}(v_i)} \mathbf{h}_j^{(t)}\right)
\end{equation}

Here $\mathcal{N}^{(1)}(v_i)$ is the neighborhood of $v_i$ in the transaction view, and $\mathcal{N}^{(2)}(v_i)$ is its neighborhood in the behavioral view. For both views, we perform message passing and pooling on a bidirected graph; hence, $\mathcal{N}^{(t)}(v_i)$ can be interpreted as the union of in- and out-neighbors. The operation is implemented efficiently with \texttt{scatter\_add}. The mean pooling in Eq.~\eqref{eq:subgraph_pool} is the default in \method.

\textbf{View-wise pooling effect.} Subgraph pooling is conducted independently for each view’s neighborhood: transaction-graph neighbors are pooled for the transaction view, while behaviorally similar neighbors are pooled for the behavioral view. Because pooling aggregates neighbor information, the choice of view shapes the outcome. The behavioral view graph is designed to place behaviorally similar suspicious accounts closer together; therefore, pooling on this view can preserve or strengthen suspicious signals. In the transaction graph, suspicious accounts are often linked to benign ones (high S-B ratio); consequently, pooling can dilute suspicious signals with benign information.

\textbf{Conditional amplification and alternative pooling.} The pooling effect depends on view homophily and encoder normalization depth. With per-layer batch normalization, ego-neighborhoods are partly normalized, leaving little room for additional pooling gains. With only final batch normalization (BGRL) or none (DGI, MVGRL, GRACE, GCA), pooling can have a larger marginal effect. When most neighbors belong to another class, averaging can weaken or even invert the suspicious signal (Theorem~\ref{thm:meanpool}; Section~\ref{subsec:theory}). We also define heterophily-aware pooling (HAP, cosine-similarity weights) to characterize when similarity weighting preserves the signal better than mean pooling (Proposition~\ref{thm:hap}); the main experiments use mean pooling for simplicity.

\subsection{Training and Inference}
\label{subsec:contrastive_learning}
\textbf{Projector and BYOL loss.} Subgraph-pooled representations pass through a projector $g$ and a predictor $q$ and are trained with a BYOL-style loss~\cite{grill2020bootstrap} (Projector and BYOL Loss in Figure~\ref{fig:framework}). Instead of pushing other accounts as negatives, the loss pulls together representations of the same account across the two views. Both views are encoded by the online encoder $f_\theta$ and the target encoder $f_\xi$, and the loss sums the $1{\to}2$ and $2{\to}1$ directions. The target encoder is a momentum copy of the online encoder ($\xi \leftarrow m\xi + (1-m)\theta$, $m=0.99$), retaining most of the previous target while slowly incorporating new information.

We denote the loss input representation by $\mathbf{r}_i^{(t)}$. In our setting, $\mathbf{r}_i^{(t)}=\mathbf{s}_i^{(t)}$; in the node-level ablation, $\mathbf{r}_i^{(t)}=\mathbf{h}_i^{(t)}$. Let $\mathbf{r}_{\xi,i}^{(t)}$ be the corresponding target-branch representation, and $g_\xi$ the target-branch projector. The BYOL loss maximizes cosine similarity between representations from the two views:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:loss}
    \mathcal{L} = \sum_{t \in \{1,2\}} \left(2 - 2 \cdot \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N} \frac{q(g(\mathbf{r}_i^{(t)})) \cdot g_\xi(\mathbf{r}_{\xi,i}^{(3-t)})}{\|q(g(\mathbf{r}_i^{(t)}))\| \cdot \|g_\xi(\mathbf{r}_{\xi,i}^{(3-t)})\|}\right)
\end{equation}

Here $g$ and $q$ are the projection and predictor heads, and $\mathbf{r}_{\xi,i}^{(\cdot)}$ is the input representation obtained from the target encoder branch.

\textbf{Inference.} At inference, we freeze the encoder and concatenate the two view representations to obtain the final embedding (right side of Figure~\ref{fig:framework}). In the subgraph-level setting, we concatenate subgraph representations:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:concat}
    \mathbf{z}_i = [\mathbf{s}_i^{(1)} \| \mathbf{s}_i^{(2)}].
\end{equation}
For node-level ablations, we use $\mathbf{z}_i = [\mathbf{h}_i^{(1)} \| \mathbf{h}_i^{(2)}]$.

Implementation details and seed-level scripts will be released with the code.

\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}
We assess \method in four phases, moving from mechanism to practical deployment. First, we examine whether the behavioral auxiliary view can make up for missing links between suspicious accounts and restore view-level homophily, thereby boosting downstream performance. Second, we test whether aggregating on the recovered view (subgraph pooling) provides additional benefits \emph{without reintroducing heterophilous dilution}, and we treat normalization effects as a stability/implementation factor that modulates this refinement rather than as a separate core contribution. Third, we benchmark the resulting self-supervised representations against supervised baselines under label delay and cold-start conditions, reflecting realistic AML settings. Fourth, we test whether the same mechanism and empirical patterns carry over to AML datasets that differ in prevalence and scale. Together, these experiments address the following research questions.

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{RQ1. Behavioral homophily recovery:} Does the topology-independent behavioral auxiliary view restore suspicious-class homophily relative to the transaction graph?
\item \textbf{RQ2. Signal preservation under aggregation:} When does subgraph pooling help versus hurt, and how does recovered homophily change this trade-off?
\item \textbf{RQ3. Self-supervised AML detection under label scarcity:} Does the self-supervised representation from \method remain competitive with supervised models under label delay and cold start?
\item \textbf{RQ4. Robustness across AML regimes:} Do the same mechanisms and trends apply to AML datasets with different prevalence and scale?
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Experimental Setting}
\textbf{Datasets.} We use three AML datasets (Table~\ref{tab:datasets}). HOFINET includes about 4.7M synthetic transactions derived from real activity on the Korean interbank settlement network, modeling 40 months of household and corporate transfers (2021Q3--2024Q4). AMLworld~\cite{amlpatterns2024realistic}, the NeurIPS 2023 open synthetic benchmark, models eight transaction patterns (e.g., fan-out, scatter-gather, cycle); we use the HI-Small variant (high illicit ratio, small size). AMLNet~\cite{huda2025amlnet}, a small synthetic benchmark for the high-prevalence regime, shares AMLworld’s feature schema.

\textbf{Account-level labels.} All three datasets provide transaction-level suspicious labels. Following standard graph-based AML and fraud detection practices~\cite{dou2020enhancing,liu2021pick}, we max-aggregate to the account level: an account is suspicious (label$=1$) if it appears on either side of any suspicious transaction, and normal (label$=0$) otherwise. The resulting suspicious-account ratios are 2.13\% (HOFINET), 1.23\% (AMLworld), and 13.52\% (AMLNet) (Table~\ref{tab:datasets}).

\textbf{Ablation settings and encoders.} We evaluate four settings that cross graph view and contrastive level: (a) transaction graph + node contrast, (b) behavioral view + node contrast, (c) transaction graph + subgraph pooling, and (d) behavioral view + subgraph pooling. All encoders use the same BYOL-style bootstrap loss (Eq.~\eqref{eq:loss}) and hyperparameters (lr=5e-4, hidden=256, $L$=2, dropout=0.2, 200 epochs).

\textbf{Supervised baselines.} We apply class-weight correction to 11 supervised models: generic GNN (GCN~\cite{kipf2016semi}, GAT~\cite{velivckovic2017graph}, GraphSAGE~\cite{hamilton2017inductive}), tabular methods (MLP, LightGBM~\cite{lightgbm2017}, XGBoost~\cite{xgboost2016}), and fraud- and anomaly-specific GNNs (CARE-GNN~\cite{dou2020enhancing}, PC-GNN~\cite{liu2021pick}, BWGNN~\cite{tang2022rethinking}, GAGA~\cite{wang2023label}, ConsisGAD~\cite{chen2024consisgad}). All three datasets are homogeneous single-relation graphs of account-to-account transfers; therefore, the multi-relation components of the fraud-specific GNNs do not apply; we retain only their single-relation core mechanisms (see Section~\ref{subsec:rq3}).

\textbf{Evaluation protocol.} The primary metric is suspicious-class F1 ($F1_{\text{susp}}$); AUROC and AUPRC are secondary. We use a 10\%/10\%/80\% train/val/test split (encoders are trained self-supervised; labels only train the Logistic Regression head; therefore, 10\% of labeled data suffices). For paired comparisons, the split is fixed per seed. We report mean$\pm$std over four seeds (2024--2027). Statistical significance uses a 4-seed paired $t$-test with Holm--Bonferroni correction; seed-level raw results are in the public repository.

\textbf{Evaluation environment.} We evaluate self-supervised models by freezing the encoder and training Logistic Regression~\cite{pedregosa2011scikit} on embeddings $\mathbf{z}_i = [\mathbf{s}_i^{(1)} \| \mathbf{s}_i^{(2)}]$. All experiments use Python 3.12, PyTorch 2.10, CUDA 12.8, and NVIDIA H100 80GB GPUs.

\subsection{Signal Preservation under Aggregation (RQ2)}
\label{subsec:rq2}
RQ1 verified homophily recovery in the behavioral auxiliary view; RQ2 asks when aggregation (subgraph pooling) helps versus hurts, and how recovered homophily changes this trade-off. Pooling is not uniformly beneficial on the raw transaction graph; however, on the recovered behavioral view, it can amplify suspicious evidence, with the clearest gains under suspicious-class-oriented downstream scoring.

\textbf{Conditional amplification across the 4-setting ablation.} The effect of subgraph pooling depends on both the view and the encoder's normalization depth (Figure~\ref{fig:rq2_ablation}). On HOFINET, the (d)-over-(b) gain shrinks as normalization deepens: four per-layer BN encoders gain only +0.002--0.003, BGRL gains +0.078, and BN-less encoders gain +0.018 on average. In contrast, setting (c), pooling on the transaction graph, reduces performance by 17--30\% relative to (a) for BN-equipped encoders ($-17.1\%$ for BGRL to $-29.9\%$ for GIN; (c) bars in Figure~\ref{fig:rq2_ablation}), consistent with the noise-amplification mechanism in Section~\ref{subsec:subgraph_pool} (Theorem~\ref{thm:meanpool}).

On HOFINET, view recovery produces the dominant jump, with (b)$>$(a) and (d)$>$(c); pooling then adds a smaller, normalization-dependent increment, with (d)$\approx$(b) for per-layer BN and larger gains for BGRL and BN-less encoders. The (b)$\to$(d) improvement is significant under paired $t$-tests for representative encoders (GBT $p=1.83{\times}10^{-3}$, BGRL Holm-adjusted $p=2.66{\times}10^{-4}$). On AMLworld, Table~\ref{tab:rq4} shows that the pooled behavioral view setting (d) improves over the node-level behavioral view (b), suggesting that the recovered behaviorally similar neighborhoods provide additional class context that pooling can accumulate.

Overall, subgraph pooling acts as a conditional amplifier whose effect depends on (i) view homophily, (ii) encoder normalization depth, and (iii) the downstream decision rule.

\begin{theorem}[Normalization-conditional stability under bootstrap objectives]\label{thm:bn_collapse}
Under the BYOL-style bootstrap loss~\eqref{eq:loss}, the trivial constant solution $f_\theta(\cdot) \equiv c$ attains $\mathcal{L}=0$ when the encoder lacks batch normalization (BN). In contrast, the presence of at least one BN layer rules out this trivial constant mapping under the definition of batch normalization, acting as a sufficient condition for avoiding the constant-solution pathology.
\end{theorem}

\textbf{Empirical check for the stability analysis.} At setting (a), BN-less encoders achieve $F1_{\text{susp}} \approx 0.04$, close to the HOFINET random-classifier upper bound $2\rho/(1+\rho) \approx 0.042$ (the maximum $F1_{\text{susp}}$ at a positive rate $\rho$), indicating that unstable training can wash out the suspicious-class signal in low-prevalence regimes.
The practical severity depends on prevalence: at low prevalence ($\rho \leq 2\%$), the bound is already $\approx 0.04$; hence, instability leaves little usable suspicious-class evidence. On AMLNet ($\rho = 13.52\%$), BN-less DGI still reaches $F1_{\text{susp}} \approx 0.40$ (Section~\ref{subsec:rq4}); consequently, the absolute impact is smaller. Overall, the results align with a single mechanism: homophily recovery provides class-relevant neighborhoods, sign-flip explains when aggregation can dilute them, and normalization mainly modulates whether this mechanism is realized under the bootstrap objective.

\section{Conclusion and Future Work}

We introduced \method, a graph contrastive learning framework for AML detection in low-homophily transaction networks. Its key idea is to construct a topology-independent behavioral auxiliary view from transaction features, raising HOFINET edge homophily from 0.690 to 0.981. The resulting self-supervised representations rival supervised models: they are within 0.006 and 0.035 $F1_{\text{susp}}$ of the best supervised baselines on HOFINET and AMLNet, and outperform all supervised GNN baselines on AMLworld. Under cold-start label scarcity, \method achieves $F1_{\text{susp}}=0.654$ with only 1\% downstream labels. 

Overall, the results support a single mechanism: recovered homophily yields class-relevant neighborhoods; sign-flip analysis shows when aggregation can dilute them; subgraph pooling on the recovered view provides conditional refinement; and normalization mainly stabilizes the bootstrap objective. These claims are supported by the stability analysis (Theorem~\ref{thm:bn_collapse}) and the homophily-recovery and aggregation-signal analyses (Theorems~\ref{thm:knn_homophily}--\ref{thm:meanpool}).

\method is designed for the operational regimes common in AML: delayed labels, low prevalence, and transaction neighborhoods that are anti-homophilous for the suspicious class. When abundant labels are available, high-prevalence supervised models such as MLP, XGBoost, or LightGBM can be marginally stronger. In settings with a six-month label delay and a 0.5\%--2\% suspicious rate, self-supervised behavioral view construction provides a trainable alternative before reliable labels exist.

Several limitations remain. The evaluation is transductive and does not yet use a temporal hold-out split. The DCSBM analysis assumes i.i.d. class-conditional behavioral features, an approximation that may be violated by deliberate evasion patterns such as mule networks. Finally, all three benchmarks used here are synthetic; thus, validation on real bank transaction data remains necessary. Future work will extend \method to dynamic and inductive graphs, evaluate temporal splits, generalize the collapse analysis beyond BYOL-style objectives, run shadow deployments in operational AML workflows, and refine cross-domain transfer.

\paragraph{Reproducibility.} We will release the source code, training scripts, and seed-level raw results. HOFINET cannot be redistributed due to regulatory restrictions; however, the AMLworld and AMLNet experiments are reproducible from public benchmarks.

\section{Extended Related Work}\label{app:rw_extended}

\subsection{GNN-based AML and Fraud Detection}\label{app:rw_aml}

Early fraud-aware GNN such as GraphConsis~\cite{liu2020alleviating} and CARE-GNN~\cite{dou2020enhancing} exploit the observation that fraudsters often form intentionally inconsistent links to benign users, introducing label-distribution-based neighbor filtering through relabeling or similarity thresholds. PC-GNN~\cite{liu2021pick} generalizes this idea with prior sampling, while RioGNN~\cite{peng2021reinforced} learns multi-relation neighbor selection through reinforcement learning. Temporal fraud models include GTAN~\cite{cheng2020graph}, which applies attentive temporal aggregation on transaction graphs, and xFraud~\cite{rao2020xfraud}, which provides explainable detection on heterogeneous account-device-IP graphs. FraudRE~\cite{zhang2021fraudre} addresses class imbalance through reweighting, and LHFD~\cite{wang2023label} studies label-aware multi-class fraud detection. These methods generally assume that labeled fraud examples are available during training. In AML operations, however, labels depend on retrospective investigations and can lag by six months or more~\cite{hilal2022financial}; \method targets this cold-start gap by learning the representation without labels.

\subsection{Heterophily-aware GNNs}\label{app:rw_hetero}

H2GCN~\cite{zhu2020beyond} separates ego and neighbor embeddings and combines higher-order neighborhoods, while Geom-GCN~\cite{pei2020geom} redefines neighborhoods through latent distances. From a spectral perspective, FAGCN~\cite{bo2021beyond} separates low- and high-frequency signals, and GPR-GNN~\cite{chien2021adaptive} uses adaptive PageRank propagation. GBK-GNN~\cite{du2022gbk} gates homophilous and heterophilous neighbors, ACM-GCN~\cite{luan2022acmgcn} uses multi-channel propagation, and LINKX~\cite{zhu2021linkx} decouples graph structure from node features to handle strong heterophily. These models modify message passing while keeping the original graph fixed. That is limiting for transaction networks in which the observed neighbors themselves often lack suspicious-class signals. \method takes a complementary route: it keeps the encoder simple and changes the auxiliary graph view.

\subsection{Augmentation-free and Feature-view GCL}\label{app:rw_gcl_aug}

AFGRL~\cite{lee2022augmentation} finds homophilous anchors in representation space, and SimGRACE~\cite{xia2022simgrace} creates views through model perturbation. CCA-SSG~\cite{zhang2021canonical} reduces redundancy through canonical correlation, while GraphMAE~\cite{hou2022graphmae} and S2GAE~\cite{li2023s2gae} replace augmentation with masking and reconstruction. Because these approaches do not introduce a separate graph structure, the anti-homophily of the transaction graph remains embedded in the contrastive views. MLGCL~\cite{shang2023mlgcl} and GCPAL~\cite{lu2024graph} are the closest baselines because they explicitly use a k-NN view. Their limitations are that they build k-NN graphs from all available features, which weakens orthogonality to the transaction topology; they do not isolate the effect of k-NN feature choice; and they do not separate node-level from subgraph-level contrast. \method addresses these points by separating behavioral from structural features and by evaluating the four view/level settings directly.

\subsection{Graph Anomaly Detection}\label{app:rw_gad}

DOMINANT~\cite{ding2019dominant} derives unsupervised anomaly scores by jointly reconstructing attributes and structure. CoLA~\cite{liu2021anomaly} uses node-subgraph contrast, and ANEMONE~\cite{jin2021anemone} extends this idea to multi-scale patch-context objectives. BWGNN~\cite{tang2022rethinking} analyzes anomalies in the spectral domain and introduces band-pass filtering. The BOND benchmark~\cite{liu2022bond} shows that such unsupervised methods can be competitive on standard anomaly datasets. Most of these benchmarks, however, are citation or co-author graphs such as Cora, Citeseer, and ACM. Their graph statistics differ sharply from AML networks, where suspicious accounts deliberately interact with benign accounts to evade detection. \method closes this domain gap by treating auxiliary-view design as topology repair (behavioral homophily recovery) and by analyzing when aggregation and training stability preserve or wash out suspicious-class signal.

\section{Theory: Proofs and Empirical Checks}\label{app:theory}

This appendix gives the setup, proof sketches, DCSBM derivations, HOFINET numerical checks, and cross-dataset empirical checks for the theory in Section~\ref{subsec:theory}.

\subsection{Setup and Notation}\label{app:theory_notation}

Let $V = V_S \cup V_B$ denote the suspicious ($S$) and benign ($B$) node sets. Each node $v$ has behavioral features $x_v \in \mathbb{R}^d$ drawn from a class-conditional distribution $x_v \mid y_v = c \sim P_c$ for $c \in \{S,B\}$, independently across nodes. For any $x \in \text{supp}(P_y)$, $y' \neq y$, and independent samples $X' \sim P_y$, $X'' \sim P_{y'}$, assume
\begin{equation}\label{eq:sep}
    \Pr\big[\|x - X'\| < \|x - X''\|\big] \;\geq\; 1 - \epsilon.
\end{equation}
Thus a same-class sample is closer than a cross-class sample with probability at least $1-\epsilon$; $\epsilon=0$ gives deterministic separation. $G_{\text{knn},k}$ is the Euclidean k-NN graph in which each node connects to its $k$ nearest neighbors.

\subsection{View-level Homophily Recovery}\label{app:theory_view}

\begin{equation}\label{eq:knn_bound}
    h(G_{\text{knn}, k}) \;\geq\; 1 - \frac{N - 1}{k} \exp\!\left(-\frac{\big((N_{\min}-1)(1-\epsilon) - k\big)^2}{2(N_{\min}-1)(1-\epsilon)}\right).
\end{equation}

For a cross-class candidate $B_j$ to enter the top-$k$ list of a suspicious source $u$, it must satisfy $Z_j := |\{i : \|x_u - x_{S_i}\| < \|x_u - x_{B_j}\|\}| < k$. By assumption~\eqref{eq:sep}, $Z_j$ stochastically dominates $\text{Binom}(N_S{-}1,1{-}\epsilon)$. Applying a Chernoff lower-tail bound and a union bound gives Eq.~\eqref{eq:knn_bound}. When $\epsilon=0$, the deterministic limit is $h(G_{\text{knn},k})=1$.

For the transaction graph $G_{\text{tx}}$, we use a degree-corrected stochastic block model (DCSBM)~\cite{karrer2011stochastic}. With activity factor $\theta_v>0$ for node $v$ and class-pair coupling $\omega_{y_u,y_v}$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:dcsbm}
    \Pr[(u, v) \in E_{\text{tx}}] \;\propto\; \theta_u \theta_v \cdot \omega_{y_u, y_v}.
\end{equation}
Let $\Theta_c := \sum_{v: y_v=c}\theta_v$. Then
\[
    h(G_{\text{tx}}) = \frac{\Theta_S^2 \omega_{SS} + \Theta_B^2 \omega_{BB}}{\Theta_S^2 \omega_{SS} + 2\Theta_S\Theta_B \omega_{SB} + \Theta_B^2 \omega_{BB}}.
\]
In HOFINET, suspicious accounts often act as high-activity hubs, inflating $\Theta_S/\Theta_B$ and yielding $h(G_{\text{tx}}) < \rho^2 + (1-\rho)^2$, the random-mixing baseline.

Theorem~\ref{thm:knn_homophily} gives $h(G_{\text{knn},k}) \geq 1-\delta(N,N_{\min},k,\epsilon)$. Setting $h(G_{\text{tx}})=\alpha$ under the DCSBM and subtracting it from both sides gives the following lower bound on the homophily gap:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:gap}
    h(G_{\text{knn}, k}) - h(G_{\text{tx}}) \;\geq\; 1 - \alpha - \tfrac{N - 1}{k} \exp\!\left(-\tfrac{((N_{\min}-1)(1-\epsilon) - k)^2}{2(N_{\min}-1)(1-\epsilon)}\right).
\end{equation}
For HOFINET ($N=452{,}816$, $N_{\min}=9{,}644$, $k=10$, $\alpha=0.690$), taking $\epsilon=0.05$ gives the bound $1-0.690-10^{-1986}\approx0.310$. The measured gap is $0.981-0.690=0.291$, about 0.019 below the bound. This difference is consistent with imperfect separability near class boundaries and with structural dependencies (e.g., mule networks) that deviate from the DCSBM assumptions. Accordingly, we interpret the bound as a sufficient condition for substantial homophily recovery, rather than as a point-accurate predictor for every dataset detail.

\subsection{Aggregation-level Signal Preservation}\label{app:theory_pool}

Write the hidden representation of node $v$ as $z_v=s_v\mu+\eta_v \in \mathbb{R}^d$. Here $s_v\in\{+1,-1\}$ is the signed suspicious/benign signal, $\mu\in\mathbb{R}^d$ is the class-discriminative direction, and $\eta_v\perp\mu$ is i.i.d.\ zero-mean noise with $\|\eta_v\|\leq\epsilon\|\mu\|$. We compare mean pooling (Eq.~\eqref{eq:subgraph_pool}) with heterophily-aware pooling, $z_v^{(\text{hap})}=(z_v+\sum_u w_{uv}z_u)/(1+\sum_u w_{uv})$, where $w_{uv}=\max(\cos(z_u,z_v),0)$. The post-pool signal is $\rho^{(\cdot)}:=\langle z_v^{(\cdot)},\mu\rangle/\|\mu\|^2$.

Without loss of generality, take the ego node $v$ to be suspicious; hence, $s_v=+1$. If neighbor $u$ has the same class with probability $1-\eta_h$ and the opposite class with probability $\eta_h$, then
\[
    \mathbb{E}[s_u \mid u \in \mathcal{N}(v)] = (1-\eta_h)(+1) + \eta_h(-1) = 1 - 2\eta_h .
\]
The expectation is over the class composition of the neighborhood, conditioning on the ego node and $\eta_h$. The expected projection after mean pooling is therefore
\[
    \mathbb{E}[\rho^{(\text{mean})}]
    = \frac{s_v + \sum_{u \in \mathcal{N}(v)} \mathbb{E}[s_u]}{k+1}
    = \frac{1 + k(1 - 2\eta_h)}{k+1}
\]
The variance is at most $\epsilon^2/(k+1)$ by the i.i.d.\ noise assumption and $\|\eta_v\|^2\leq\epsilon^2\|\mu\|^2$.

Under the small-noise approximation, a cross-class neighbor satisfies $\cos(z_u,z_v)=-1+O(\epsilon^2)<0$; therefore, $w_{uv}=0$. A same-class neighbor has $\cos(z_u,z_v)=1-O(\epsilon^2)$. HAP therefore removes the cross-class contribution to first order, yielding the conservative $\mu$-projection lower bound in Proposition~\ref{thm:hap}.

\paragraph{Approximate pool-level implication.}
For $\eta_h \in [1/2,1]$, $\mathbb{E}[\rho^{(\text{hap})}-\rho^{(\text{mean})}] \geq 2\eta_h k/(k+1)-O(\epsilon^2)$.

Under the DCSBM in Eq.~\eqref{eq:dcsbm}, the ego-neighborhood heterophily of a suspicious source $v$ can be written as
\begin{equation}\label{eq:eta_dcsbm}
    \eta_h^{(v)} \;=\; \frac{1}{1 + (\Theta_S' / \Theta_B)(\omega_{SS} / \omega_{SB})}, \quad \Theta_S' = \Theta_S - \theta_v.
\end{equation}
With HOFINET parameters ($\Theta_S \approx 6.94\times10^4$, $\Theta_B \approx 6.20\times10^5$, $\omega_{SS}/\omega_{SB}\approx3.45$), a typical suspicious source has $\eta_h\approx0.72$, while a high-activity hub reaches about 0.95. The measured aggregate value, $\eta_h\approx0.85$ (S-S:S-B = 1:5.7), falls within this range.

Plugging in $\eta_h=0.85$, $k=10$, and $\epsilon=0.2$ gives $\mathbb{E}[\rho^{(\text{mean})}]\approx-0.545$; therefore, mean pooling can flip the signal. Proposition~\ref{thm:hap}, in contrast, gives the conservative bound $\mathbb{E}[\rho^{(\text{hap})}]\geq0.784$. Thus setting (c), transaction graph plus mean pooling, can invert the suspicious direction, while HAP preserves a positive projection.

\section{Theoretical Analysis: Homophily Recovery and Signal Preservation}
\label{subsec:theory}

We now analyze how each \method component behaves when suspicious accounts have low-homophily neighborhoods in the transaction graph. Two central issues arise: (1) a view derived directly from transaction edges rarely links suspicious accounts, resulting in weak suspicious-class homophily; and (2) aggregating information from such heterophilous neighbors can dilute or even invert the suspicious-class signal. Using theorems and a proposition, we identify when a behavioral view can restore homophily and how different pooling mechanisms affect signal preservation.

We model the transaction graph with a degree-corrected stochastic block model (DCSBM)~\cite{karrer2011stochastic} and assume behavioral features are i.i.d. with class-conditional distributions $x_v \mid y_v = c \sim P_c$. We define homophily $h(G) := |\{(u,v) \in E : y_u = y_v\}| / |E|$, set $|V_S| = \rho N$, and let $N_{\min} = \min(|V_S|, |V_B|)$. In behavioral space, we assume probabilistic class separability: a same-class node is closer to a query than a different-class node with a probability of at least $1 - \epsilon$.

\subsection{View-level Homophily Recovery}
\label{subsec:theory_view}

\begin{theorem}[Probabilistic homophily recovery]
\label{thm:knn_homophily}
For $k \leq (1-\epsilon)(N_{\min}-1) - 1$, the behavioral view graph $G_{\text{knn},k}$ satisfies the homophily lower bound
\[
    h(G_{\text{knn}, k}) \;\geq\; 1 - \tfrac{N - 1}{k} \exp\!\left(-\tfrac{((N_{\min}-1)(1-\epsilon) - k)^2}{2(N_{\min}-1)(1-\epsilon)}\right).
\]
\end{theorem}

\begin{theorem}[Combined homophily gap]\label{thm:combined}
Let $h(G_{\text{tx}}) = \alpha$ under the DCSBM, and let $\delta$ denote the error term on the right-hand side of Theorem~\ref{thm:knn_homophily}. Then
\[
    h(G_{\text{knn}, k}) - h(G_{\text{tx}})
    \geq 1 - \alpha - \delta(N, N_{\min}, k, \epsilon).
\]
\end{theorem}

\subsection{Aggregation-level Signal Preservation}
\label{subsec:theory_pool}

We now focus on the aggregation step. We decompose the hidden representation of node $v$ as $z_v = s_v \mu + \eta_v$, where $s_v \in \{\pm 1\}$ denotes the class signal, $\mu$ is the discriminative direction separating suspicious from benign nodes, and $\eta_v$ is a bounded noise term with $\|\eta_v\| \leq \epsilon \|\mu\|$. We quantify the heterophily of the 1-hop ego neighborhood of $v$ by
\[
\eta_h := \frac{|\{u \in \mathcal{N}(v) : y_u \neq y_v\}|}{|\mathcal{N}(v)|}.
\]
The $\eta_h$ values reported in the main text are dataset-level averages, obtained by computing this fraction for every node in the transaction graph.

To assess how effectively pooling retains the discriminative direction $\mu$, we compare the post-pooling projection
\[
\rho := \frac{\langle z_v^{(\cdot)}, \mu \rangle}{\|\mu\|^2}
\]
under mean pooling and heterophily-aware pooling (HAP, using cosine-similarity weights). The expectation is taken with the ego node $v$ and its heterophily $\eta_h$ fixed, while the class labels of its neighbors are treated as random. Without loss of generality, we set $s_v = +1$; hence, any neighbor $u$ satisfies
\[
\mathbb{E}[s_u \mid u \in \mathcal{N}(v)] = (1-\eta_h)(+1) + \eta_h(-1) = 1 - 2\eta_h.
\]

\begin{theorem}[Mean-pool signal reversal]
\label{thm:meanpool}
With mean pooling, the expected post-pooling projection is $\mathbb{E}[\rho^{(\text{mean})}] = \frac{1 + k(1 - 2\eta_h)}{k+1}$. Consequently, the expected signal flips sign once $\eta_h > \tfrac{1}{2} + \tfrac{1}{2k}$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proposition}[HAP signal preservation under small-noise regime]
\label{thm:hap}
Assume a small-noise regime with $\epsilon < 1/\sqrt{2}$ and preservation of the cosine similarity sign. Then, for any $\eta_h$, the HAP post-pooling expectation satisfies
\[
\mathbb{E}[\rho^{(\text{hap})}] \geq 1 - 2\epsilon^2 - 4\epsilon^2 \eta_h.
\]
\end{proposition}

Figure~\ref{fig:theory_inset} shows the sign-flip threshold from Theorem~\ref{thm:meanpool} and the measured $\eta_h$ on the three datasets. All three exceed $\eta_h^* = 0.55$ at $k{=}10$; therefore, mean pooling on the transaction graph places the suspicious signal in the sign-flip regime, explaining why setting (c) (transaction graph plus mean pool) can degrade performance.

\subsection{Setting-level Performance Prediction}
\label{subsec:theory_pred}

When $\eta_h^{\text{tx}} > 1/2 + 1/(2 \bar{d}_{\text{tx}})$, with $\bar{d}_{\text{tx}}$ the average ego-neighborhood size in the transaction graph, and the encoder uses batch normalization, the analysis predicts $F1(b) \approx F1(d) > F1(a) > F1(c)$, combining view-level homophily recovery with mean-pool signal reversal. On HOFINET, all six BN-equipped encoders follow this ordering ($\Delta(b{-}a) \in [+0.255, +0.405]$, $\Delta(c{-}a) \in [-0.070, -0.052]$).

Theorems~\ref{thm:knn_homophily}--\ref{thm:combined} specify when a behavioral view can offset scarce suspicious--suspicious transaction edges, while Theorem~\ref{thm:meanpool} and Proposition~\ref{thm:hap} compare pooling choices under high neighborhood heterophily. Together, they motivate the four-setting ablation used in the experiments below.


\section{Datasets: Detailed Description}\label{app:datasets}

\subsection{HOFINET}\label{app:dataset_hofinet}

HOFINET is a synthetic transaction dataset derived from activity on Korea's Home and Firm Banking Network, an inter-bank transfer network operated by the Korea Financial Telecommunications \& Clearings Institute (KFTC). The network integrates household channels such as internet and mobile banking with corporate channels such as firm banking. The snapshot used in this study contains 452,816 accounts and 4,732,130 directed multi-edges, with a suspicious-account ratio of 2.13\% (9,644 accounts). The simulator models six AML typologies, including structuring, layering, and mule-network patterns, over 40 months from 2021Q3 to 2024Q4.

\subsection{AMLworld}\label{app:dataset_amlworld}

AMLworld~\cite{amlpatterns2024realistic} is a public synthetic AML benchmark from the NeurIPS 2023 Datasets \& Benchmarks Track. We use the HI-Small variant, which contains 515,088 accounts and 5,078,345 directed multi-edges. Its transaction-level laundering rate is 0.10\%, and its account-level suspicious ratio is 1.23\%, lower than HOFINET's 2.13\%. The simulator covers eight AML patterns: fan-out, fan-in, scatter-gather, gather-scatter, cycle, bipartite, stack, and random.

\subsection{AMLNet}\label{app:dataset_amlnet}

AMLNet~\cite{huda2025amlnet} is a smaller public synthetic AML benchmark with 11,000 accounts and 1,090,172 directed multi-edges. Its account-level suspicious ratio is 13.52\% (1,487 accounts), more than an order of magnitude above HOFINET and AMLworld, making it useful for high-prevalence evaluation. AMLNet shares the same 22 behavioral feature schema as AMLworld, which enables the cross-dataset transfer study in Appendix~\ref{app:transfer}.

\subsection{Transaction-to-Account Label Aggregation}\label{app:dataset_label}

All three datasets provide labels at the transaction level (HOFINET: suspicious-transaction flag; AMLworld: Is Laundering; AMLNet: isMoneyLaundering). Our node-classification task requires account-level labels; therefore, we aggregate them as follows. For transaction $e=(u,v,t)$ with label $\ell(e)\in\{0,1\}$, the account label for $a$ is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:label_agg}
    y_a \;=\; \max_{e \in \mathcal{E}_a^{\text{out}} \cup \mathcal{E}_a^{\text{in}}} \ell(e),
\end{equation}
Thus $y_a=1$ if account $a$ participates in at least one suspicious transaction as either sender or receiver, and $y_a=0$ otherwise. This max-aggregation follows standard graph-based fraud detection practice~\cite{dou2020enhancing,liu2021pick} and gives a conservative suspicious-account definition that minimizes false negatives. The resulting label distribution matches the prevalence $\rho$ in Table~\ref{tab:datasets}.

In operational AML, suspicious activity reports are filed at the transaction or case level, but analysts first identify risky accounts and then inspect their transaction sequences before making a SAR decision. The account-level risk score studied here directly supports that analyst workflow and is complementary to isolated transaction-level review.

\section{BN-Conditional Collapse: Formalization}\label{app:bn_proof}

This section gives the proof sketch for Theorem~\ref{thm:bn_collapse} in Section~\ref{subsec:rq2}.

The BYOL-style bootstrap loss in Eq.~\eqref{eq:loss} uses no negative samples; therefore, a trivial constant solution $f_\theta(\cdot)\equiv c$ can achieve $\mathcal{L}=0$ when online and target representations coincide and the cosine similarity is 1~\cite{tian2021understanding,chen2021exploring}. BatchNorm~\cite{ioffe2015batch} imposes batch-level zero-mean/unit-variance normalization, $\hat{x}=(x-\mu_B)/\sqrt{\sigma_B^2+\epsilon}$, where $\mu_B$ and $\sigma_B$ are computed from the current batch. If every batch element equals the same constant $c$, the input has zero variance and the normalization becomes numerically ill-conditioned as $\sigma_B^2\to0$. A BN-equipped encoder therefore cannot attain the trivial constant solution as a stable global minimum.

The theorem explains why both BN-equipped families in the paper, per-layer BN and final-only BN in BGRL, avoid collapse, matching the measured pattern: per-layer BN reaches about 0.27 in setting (a), BGRL about 0.31, and BN-less encoders about 0.04. On AMLNet, whose prevalence is 13.5\%, the BN-less DGI encoder also escapes the collapse region and reaches $F1_{\text{susp}}\approx0.40$ in setting (a). This is consistent with the random-classifier upper bound $2\rho/(1+\rho)$ rising to about 0.24 at higher prevalence, reducing the absolute impact of collapse.

\section{Implementation Details}\label{app:impl}

The default GNN encoder is an $L$-layer GCNConv~\cite{kipf2016semi} stack with BatchNorm, activation, and dropout at each layer:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:gnn_layer}
    \mathbf{H}^{(l)} = \text{Dropout}\left(\sigma\left(\text{BN}\left(\hat{\mathbf{A}}\mathbf{H}^{(l-1)}\mathbf{W}^{(l)}\right)\right)\right)
\end{equation}
Here $\hat{\mathbf{A}}$ is the normalized adjacency matrix, $\mathbf{W}^{(l)}$ is the learnable weight matrix at layer $l$, $\sigma$ is PReLU or ReLU, and BN denotes BatchNorm. Real transaction networks combine power-law degree distributions with extreme class imbalance (2.13\% suspicious accounts in HOFINET and 1.23\% in AMLworld). Without normalization, high-degree benign accounts dominate the gradients and dilute the suspicious-account signal. Per-layer BatchNorm normalizes the batch-level activation distribution, allowing suspicious accounts to contribute proportionally to the contrastive objective; this is the same mechanism that blocks trivial constant collapse in Theorem~\ref{thm:bn_collapse}.

At inference time, we freeze the encoder and concatenate the subgraph representations from the two views to form the final embedding, $\mathbf{z}_i=[\mathbf{s}_i^{(1)}\|\mathbf{s}_i^{(2)}]$. Appendix~\ref{app:algorithm} provides the training and inference pseudocode.

\section{Training and Inference Algorithm}\label{app:algorithm}

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{\method: Training and Inference}\label{alg:becon}
\KwIn{Transaction graph $\mathcal{G}_{\text{trans}}$, encoder features $\mathbf{X}^{\text{enc}}$, k-NN features $\mathbf{X}^{\text{knn}}$, $k$, epochs $T$, momentum $m$}
\KwOut{Node embeddings $\mathbf{Z}$}
\tcc{Preprocessing}
Build $\mathcal{G}_{\text{knn}}$ from $\mathbf{X}^{\text{knn}}$ via Eq.~\eqref{eq:l2norm}--\eqref{eq:knn_edge}\;
Initialize online encoder $f_\theta$, target encoder $f_\xi \leftarrow f_\theta$\;
\tcc{Training}
\For{$t = 1$ \KwTo $T$}{
  $\tilde{\mathcal{G}}_{\text{trans}}, \tilde{\mathcal{G}}_{\text{knn}} \leftarrow \text{Augment}(\mathcal{G}_{\text{trans}}, \mathcal{G}_{\text{knn}})$\;
  $\mathbf{H}^{(1)}, \mathbf{H}^{(2)} \leftarrow f_\theta(\mathbf{X}^{\text{enc}}, \tilde{\mathcal{E}}_{\text{trans}}), f_\theta(\mathbf{X}^{\text{enc}}, \tilde{\mathcal{E}}_{\text{knn}})$\;
  $\mathbf{S}^{(1)}, \mathbf{S}^{(2)} \leftarrow \text{SubgraphPool}(\mathbf{H}^{(1)}), \text{SubgraphPool}(\mathbf{H}^{(2)})$\;
  Compute $\mathcal{L}$ via Eq.~\eqref{eq:loss}, update $\theta$\;
  $\xi \leftarrow m\xi + (1-m)\theta$\;
}
\tcc{Inference}
$\mathbf{S}^{(1)}, \mathbf{S}^{(2)} \leftarrow$ encode and pool with frozen $f_\theta$\;
$\mathbf{Z} \leftarrow [\mathbf{S}^{(1)} \| \mathbf{S}^{(2)}]$\;
\Return $\mathbf{Z}$
\end{algorithm}

Four patterns emerge. First, on the transaction view with per-layer BN encoders (GBT and GIN), HAP and Cycle recover substantial performance over mean pooling: +0.046 for GBT and +0.059 for GIN. This is the regime where the HAP signal-preservation bound is most relevant because mean pooling lies in the sign-flip region ($\eta_h>0.55$). Second, with final-only BN (BGRL), all three variants are within noise (mean 0.253, HAP 0.240, Cycle 0.243, all with std $\approx0.02$), suggesting that the final normalization already absorbs much of the heterophily noise after pooling. Third, the BN-free DGI encoder remains near the random-classifier region across settings ($F1_{\text{susp}}\approx0.04$--0.07); consequently, pool variants have little effect unless the encoder has already learned a usable signal. Fourth, on the behavioral view, the three variants are nearly identical: recovered homophily makes the $-4\epsilon^2\eta_h$ term in Proposition~\ref{thm:hap} negligible; hence, mean pooling already preserves the signal.

Overall, HAP is most useful for robustness in the mean-pool sign-flip regime, especially with per-layer normalization on the transaction view. Outside that regime, mean pooling is sufficient and remains the default design for \method.
